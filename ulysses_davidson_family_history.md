# Remembering Ulysses Earl Robert "General" Davidson Sr. (1870-1965)  
*A family history prepared by Alan Jacob Davidson, great-grandson, July 2025*

---

### Birth and Early Life  

Earl—never “Ulysses” to anyone who knew him—was born on 20 January 1870 on the Davidson homestead two miles south of Beaman, Grundy County, Iowa.  His parents, Alan Early Davidson (b. 1838, Marion County, Indiana—d. 1909, same farm) and Julia Mary Cooper (b. 1843, Madison County, Ohio—d. 1921, Marshalltown), had moved to the 160-acre claim shortly after their marriage in 1866.  Earl was their sixth surviving child of eleven.  

The 1880 federal census enumerates him as “Earl R. Davidson,” eight years old, “works on farm.”  Family ledgers record that his daily tasks included harrowing the east forty, milking eight cows by lantern, and hauling timber with Tom, the elder draft horse.  Winter evenings he attended the one-room Cedar No. 4 school, where his half-year attendance in 1882–83 produced a penmanship award noted by the Grundy Center *Register* on 23 March 1883.

### Leaving Home and Marriage  

At eighteen, Earl took the postmaster examination on 14 November 1888 and secured appointment to the Beaman post-office—then a one-man operation inside Carlson’s General Store on Front Street.  On 12 September 1889 he married Victoria Mary Carter, daughter of Civil War veteran Silas Benton Carter and schoolteacher Mildred Ann Preston.  Victoria became assistant postmistress, handwriting all registry receipts in a copperplate style still preserved in the Grundy County archives.  

Their first child—my grandfather Carl Early Davidson—was born 19 May 1890 above the store.

### Beaman Postmaster to Sub-Federal Deputy (1889–1935)  

Earl’s tenure as postmaster endured for forty-six years, surviving repeated changes in Presidential administration.  During that span the post-office moved twice, finally settling in the two-story brick building at 202 Main Street in 1912 after President Taft personally approved the $3,800 construction allotment (Report 64313, U.S. Treasury, 1912).  

According to the 1900 census, the household numbered five—Earl (30, postmaster), Victoria (28), Carl (10), and daughters Ruth Victoria (7) and Bernice Elaine (2).  Earl’s real and personal property valuation was listed at $2,800—comfortable but not prosperous by contemporary standards.

In 1918 Earl accepted auxiliary duties as sub-federal deputy surveyor, physically walking route lines for rural free delivery extension maps, a task tolerated by the county clerk because “Davidson’s legs never seemed to tire” (Memo, Clerk’s Office, Grundy Center, 7 Aug 1919).

### Nickname and Temper  

Local tradition ascribes the sobriquet “General” to an incident at the Beaman Fall Festival, 1897, when Earl marshaled unruly hay-wagon drivers into orderly formation.  The label stuck.  Throughout his life children in town saluted when he passed; he would nod gravely without breaking stride.

### Later Life and Descendants  

Earl formally retired 30 June 1935 and devoted subsequent decades to keeping the farm books for his eldest son Carl, now the owner of the original 160 acres plus an additional 400.  Victoria died 14 October 1954 after sixty-five years of marriage.  Earl followed eleven years later, passing in his sleep on 3 May 1965, age 95.  Interment is beside Victoria in Union Township Cemetery, near the black walnut tree Earl planted as a wedding sapling in 1889.

Surviving children at death:  
1. Carl Early Davidson (1890–1979), farmer  
2. Ruth Victoria Davidson Johnson (1893–1976), elementary teacher  
3. Bernice Elaine Davidson Thorne (1898–1991), postmistress, Reinbeck  

Great-grandchildren now living: seven, of whom I am the eldest documentarian.
