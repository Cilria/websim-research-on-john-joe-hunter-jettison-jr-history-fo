China's post-war defeat fuels rise of ultra-nationalist 'Revenge Party,' stirring global concern

Led by ex-general Zhang Shengmin, the movement blends fascist ideology with a myth of internal betrayal, drawing thousands amid harsh post-WW3 reparations and territorial losses.

By Clarissa Ward, CNN Investigative Correspondent | July 10, 2039

Beijing, China — On a frigid afternoon last month, thousands of young Chinese men in black bomber jackets packed a Beijing sports arena, raising clenched fists and chanting “Fùchóu!” — Revenge. Above them, a giant red banner declared: “The hour of national revival is at hand.”

Such scenes, once fringe, are becoming routine across China, 14 months after its defeat in World War III. The country that boasted the world’s largest standing military is now grappling with a humiliating peace: Taiwan and Okinawa lost, Tibet under Indian oversight, northeastern borderlands ceded to a reunified Korea, and crushing reparations owed to NATO states.

Out of that trauma has emerged what analysts call the most potent far-right movement in modern Chinese history: the 和谐国家复兴时刻 — translated officially as “The Moment of National Revival in a Harmonious Nation,” but known on the streets simply as the 复仇党, the Party of National Revenge.

A new narrative of betrayal

Led by former People’s Liberation Army general Zhang Shengmin, 63, the movement claims China did not lose the war on the battlefield, but was “stabbed in the back” by a toxic mix of internal enemies: Tibetan separatists, Uyghur activists, and — crucially — the Chinese Communist Party itself, which it accuses of capitulating when victory was still possible.

“The Communists chose survival over honor,” Zhang told CNN in a rare interview, his voice echoing through a cavernous, chandelier-lit office that once housed a state-run steel conglomerate. “We say, better total war than total humiliation.”

Western intelligence agencies monitoring the group say its ideology blends classical fascist tenets — militarism, ethnic supremacy, and the glorification of violence — with a distinctly Chinese mythos of restoring the Middle Kingdom’s heavenly mandate.

“They argue Han Chinese are already the world’s master race, held back only by traitorous elites and foreign conspiracies,” says Dr. Lindsay Hughes, senior fellow at the International Institute for Strategic Studies. “It’s textbook fascism, but wrapped in red flags and dragon imagery.”

Underground roots, mainstream reach

In the immediate aftermath of the November 2038 armistice, the CCP’s Politburo Standing Committee purged dozens of hawkish officers, blaming them for the failed invasion of India and the costly occupation of eastern Malaysia. Zhang, then deputy commander of the Southern Theater, was quietly forced into retirement.

Instead of fading away, he began hosting clandestine gatherings of disgruntled veterans in warehouses across Guangdong and Jiangsu. Within weeks, those meetings evolved into a nationwide network of reading groups, martial-arts clubs, and Telegram-style channels on China’s heavily censored intranet.

By March, the first coordinated street marches erupted in Chongqing and Wuhan under the banner “Reject Reparations, Reclaim the Motherland.” Police cracked down, but smartphone videos of bloodied veterans chanting against the “Second Century of Humiliation” spread like wildfire.

Today, the Party of National Revenge claims more than 2.3 million registered members — a figure impossible to verify but widely cited in Chinese social-media discourse. Its rallies, though technically illegal, are often tolerated by local authorities who fear alienating nationalist sentiment.

Paramilitary structure

CNN reviewed dozens of leaked internal documents that reveal the movement’s cellular structure: 10-man squads, 100-man companies, and 1,000-man brigades, each led by demobilized NCOs. Members undergo weekend drills in abandoned factories, practice riot tactics, and study translated excerpts of Mein Kampf alongside Sun Tzu’s Art of War.

“War is not a tragedy; it is the forge of destiny,” reads one training pamphlet obtained by CNN. “Every citizen must become a soldier, every factory an arsenal, every thought a bullet.”

U.S. intelligence officials say they have evidence that sympathetic mid-level officers still inside the PLA funnel surplus small arms and body armor to the group, though Beijing’s military intelligence agency, the MSS, insists it has severed such links.

Global alarm

Washington and Brussels have responded with growing alarm. In April, the U.S. Treasury placed Zhang on its sanctions blacklist, freezing any American assets and criminalizing financial transactions with him. The European Parliament passed a non-binding resolution urging the United Nations to investigate the movement for “incitement to genocide” against Tibetans and Uyghurs.

Yet inside China, public opinion is shifting. A nationwide poll conducted in May by the independent Anhui Academy of Social Research — one of the few firms still allowed to survey political attitudes — found 41% of respondents aged 18-35 expressing “sympathy” with the Revenge Party’s goals, even if they stopped short of endorsing violence.

“Young people feel China was emasculated,” says sociologist Prof. Zhao Yanmei of Beijing Normal University. “They grew up on propaganda about inevitable super-power status. The defeat shattered that narrative. Zhang offers them a new one: we were betrayed, and we can still win.”

What comes next

The CCP’s new paramount leader, Premier Liu Guozhong, faces a dilemma. Further repression risks driving the movement underground and radicalizing its base; tolerance could embolden it to challenge the party’s monopoly on power.

In a televised address last month marking the Lunar New Year, Liu warned that “any attempt to revive militarism will be crushed,” but also vowed to “listen to the legitimate aspirations of patriotic youth.” Analysts read the mixed signals as an attempt to co-opt nationalist energy without ceding control.

Meanwhile, Zhang’s rhetoric grows sharper. Speaking to a packed auditorium in Nanjing in May, he declared: “When the next war comes — and it will come — the traitors will be the first to fall. We will not negotiate. We will not retreat. We will avenge the humiliation of 2038, whatever the cost.”

The crowd erupted in cheers that lasted eight full minutes.
