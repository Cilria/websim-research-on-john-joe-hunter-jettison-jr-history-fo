Airbus explores A350neo upgrade and new wide-body model in challenge to Boeing
European planemaker studies next-generation aircraft amid competitive pressure
By Sarah Jenkins and Michael Chen

PARIS/TULOUSE (Reuters) - Airbus SE is in the early stages of developing a re-engined version of its A350 wide-body jet, internally designated A350neo, and studying a completely new aircraft codenamed "New Large Aircraft" as it seeks to maintain its competitive edge against Boeing, three people familiar with the matter told Reuters.

The European aerospace giant has begun preliminary work on the A350neo project, which would feature more efficient engines and aerodynamic improvements, while simultaneously exploring a clean-sheet design that could replace both the A330neo and eventually the A350 itself, the sources said.

"These are very early-stage studies," said one of the sources, who declined to be named because the plans are not public. "Nothing has been formally approved by the board, but the technical teams are exploring what could be possible."

An Airbus spokesperson declined to comment on future product development.

The move comes as Boeing continues to develop its next-generation wide-body family, including the 777X and a potential new mid-market airplane that would fill the gap between narrow-body and wide-body aircraft.

Airbus has captured approximately 60% of the wide-body market share since 2020, driven largely by strong sales of the A350 and A330neo. However, industry analysts say the company faces pressure to maintain this momentum as Boeing invests heavily in new technologies.

The A350neo would likely enter service in the early 2030s if launched, sources said, and would compete directly with Boeing's 777X and any future clean-sheet designs from the U.S. manufacturer.

Engine manufacturers including Rolls-Royce, General Electric and Pratt & Whitney have been approached about providing powerplants for both potential aircraft, though no formal requests for proposals have been issued, two of the sources said.

The "New Large Aircraft" project represents a more ambitious undertaking that could eventually replace both the A330neo and A350 families with a single, more efficient platform optimized for the 250-400 seat market, according to the sources.

"The question Airbus is trying to answer is what comes after the A350," said Richard Evans, senior aerospace analyst at London-based consultancy AeroDynamic. "Do you keep evolving existing platforms, or do you make the big leap to something completely new?"

Airbus typically spends 10-15 years developing entirely new aircraft programs, with development costs for wide-body jets often exceeding $15 billion. Any formal launch decision would likely require board approval by 2029 at the latest to meet early 2030s service entry targets.

The company's last all-new aircraft, the A350, entered service in 2015 after a development program that began in 2004. Since then, Airbus has focused on incremental improvements to existing models rather than clean-sheet designs.

"The technology has advanced significantly since the A350 was conceived," said another source familiar with the studies. "New materials, manufacturing techniques, and propulsion technologies could deliver 15-20% better efficiency than current generation aircraft."

Airbus shares were up 1.2% in early Paris trading following the Reuters report.
