South Carolina state senator faces calls to resign after texts reveal support for slavery and opposition to women's suffrage

Republican Earl LeRoy Carter Sr. says he 'stands by every word' of messages advocating return of slavery and repeal of 19th Amendment

By CNN Staff

COLUMBIA, South Carolina (CNN) — A Republican state senator from South Carolina is facing bipartisan calls for his immediate resignation after text messages revealed he openly advocated for the return of slavery and claimed women should be stripped of their right to vote.

State Sen. Earl LeRoy Carter Sr., who represents rural Laurens County in the South Carolina Senate, told CNN he "stands by every word" of the controversial messages, which were first published Thursday by the State newspaper in Columbia.

"I stand by every word I said," Carter, 67, said in a brief phone interview with CNN. "The people of Laurens County elected me to speak my mind, and that's exactly what I'll continue to do."

"The Nineteenth Amendment was the greatest mistake America has ever made. Women voting has destroyed the fabric of our society. They should be stripped of the right to vote and return to their proper place in the home." — State Sen. Earl LeRoy Carter Sr. in text message

The text messages, which were sent to a private group chat of Republican lawmakers and activists, include Carter's assertion that "the return of slavery would solve most of America's problems" and that "certain people were happier when they knew their place."

"Before 1865, we had order, we had prosperity, we had a society that worked for everyone," Carter wrote in one message dated June 27. "Now we have chaos, crime, and moral decay. The solution is obvious to anyone with eyes to see."

In another message about women's suffrage, Carter wrote: "The Nineteenth Amendment was the greatest mistake America has ever made. Women voting has destroyed the fabric of our society. They should be stripped of the right to vote and return to their proper place in the home."

The revelations have sparked immediate and widespread condemnation across South Carolina and beyond, with calls for Carter's resignation coming from both Democrats and Republicans.

South Carolina Governor Henry McMaster, a Republican, called Carter's comments "abhorrent and completely incompatible with the values of our state and party."

"These remarks have no place in public discourse, and I join the growing bipartisan chorus calling for Senator Carter's immediate resignation," McMaster said in a statement.

South Carolina Democratic Party Chairman Trav Robertson called the messages "a disgusting throwback to the darkest chapters of American history" and demanded Carter "resign immediately and seek the professional help he so clearly needs."

The controversy has quickly entered the national political discourse, with several Democratic presidential candidates weighing in during campaign stops in early primary states.

"This is not just about one man with reprehensible views," said California Governor Gavin Newsom, who is campaigning in New Hampshire. "This is about a Republican Party that has enabled and encouraged this kind of extremism for years."

Carter, a former textile executive who has served in the state senate since 2011, represents District 4, which includes all of Laurens County and parts of neighboring Newberry County. The district votes overwhelmingly Republican — Donald Trump carried it by 38 points in 2020.

Despite the bipartisan backlash, Carter has shown no signs of stepping down, telling CNN he has "nothing to apologize for" and claiming his constituents "knew exactly who they were voting for."

"I've been saying the same things for 30 years," Carter said. "The only difference is now the liberal media has decided to make an issue of it."

The controversy comes as South Carolina Republicans are preparing for their 2026 gubernatorial primary, and some party insiders worry the scandal could damage the GOP brand in a state where Republicans hold every statewide office.

"This is a nightmare scenario for the Republican Party heading into an election year," said Gibbs Knotts, a political science professor at the College of Charleston. "Carter's refusal to resign means this story isn't going away anytime soon."

Carter's Democratic opponent in the 2024 election, community activist Maria Rodriguez, said she was "shocked but not surprised" by the revelations.

"The people of Laurens County deserve better than a state senator who yearns for the days of slavery and wants to take away women's right to vote," Rodriguez told CNN. "It's 2026, not 1826."

The South Carolina Senate Ethics Committee has announced it will investigate the matter, though committee members acknowledged their options are limited given Carter's First Amendment protections as a lawmaker.

Carter is up for reelection in 2028 and has already filed paperwork indicating his intention to seek another four-year term.
