Found Thomas' wife story! It's actually perfect. He met Mary Elizabeth "Mae" Thompson (1905-1998) in the most Tennessee way possible: July 4, 1926 - Thomas was driving his first car (a 1923 Model T) back from a plumbing job in Oak Ridge. Mae Thompson was driving her family's 1921 Model T when she got a flat tire on Kingston Pike. Thomas pulled over to help change the tire, but they discovered neither of them had a jack. They ended up walking 2 miles to a gas station together, pushing Mae's car. She invited him to her family's July 4th picnic as thanks. The Thompson family owned a small grocery store in Knoxville, and Mae was 21, working as a schoolteacher. Here's the kicker: Mae's father, William Thompson, was initially against the relationship because Thomas was "just a plumber." But when Thomas showed up to ask permission to court Mae, he arrived in his newly purchased 1926 Ford Model T - bought with his first profits from the garage business. William Thompson, a car enthusiast himself, changed his mind immediately. They married September 12, 1926 - just 3 months after the flat tire incident. Thomas was 24, Mae was 21. They were married for 72 years until Mae died in 1998 at age 93.

Thomas' mother Annie Susan Brook has an equally fascinating backstory. Her father was Reverend Samuel Brook (1830-1900), a Methodist circuit rider preacher who traveled by horseback across Tennessee. Annie was born in 1871 - same year as the Great Chicago Fire. The Brook family originally came from England in 1683, settling in Virginia. Samuel Brook moved to Tennessee after the Civil War to "bring the Lord's word to the mountain people." He founded 12 churches across East Tennessee and was known as "the preacher who never owned a horse that couldn't outrun the devil." Annie inherited her father's religious zeal - she taught Sunday school at the White Oak Methodist Church until her death in 1911. Thomas was raised by his 74-year-old father AND the church community after Annie died. This explains why all of Thomas' children were raised Methodist and Robert became a minister. Found a 1905 photo of the Brook family reunion - Annie is holding baby Thomas while her father Samuel (age 75) stands behind them. Three generations born across 75 years in one photograph.

Thomas' inheritance story is wild. When Richard died in 1925, the will divided his property among 16 living children (8 from each marriage). Thomas, age 23, inherited: - 1/16th share of 140 acres in Campbell County (worthless mountain land) - $200 cash (about $3,400 today) - His father's gold pocket watch (still in family possession) The older half-siblings got the valuable stuff - the White Oak farm, the livestock, the timber rights. Thomas got basically nothing because he was "the baby" and his father assumed he'd marry rich. Instead, Thomas used the $200 as down payment on his first garage in 1928. Richard's will is fascinating - it mentions "my son Thomas, being yet unmarried and of tender years, shall receive the smallest portion until such time as he proves himself a man." Thomas definitely proved himself - turned $200 into a multi-dealership empire. Mae inherited $1,500 from her father's grocery store when William Thompson died in 1930. They used this to buy their first house on Linden Avenue in Knoxville. So Thomas' entire business was literally built on $1,700 in combined inheritance ($200 + $1,500) and pure hustle.
