Three Brothers Arrested in $9 Billion Corruption Scheme That Spanned Decade, Authorities Say

North Carolina state senator and his two brothers face up to 50 years in prison in what prosecutors call "one of the most brazen corruption cases in state history"

OXFORD, N.C. — A sweeping corruption investigation has led to the arrest of three brothers — including a sitting Republican state senator — in connection with a $9 billion scheme to defraud taxpayers and transfer money to offshore accounts, federal prosecutors announced Tuesday.

State Sen. Carl William Francis, 58, his brother James Francis, 56, and their younger brother David Anderholt Francis Jr., 52, were taken into custody following a five-year investigation that uncovered what authorities describe as "a family-run criminal enterprise" that exploited public trust for personal enrichment.

The charges stem from a complex scheme that allegedly began in 2013 and involved the systematic theft of public funds through fraudulent government contracts, which were then funneled through shell companies and ultimately transferred to accounts in the Cayman Islands, according to the 47-page federal indictment unsealed Tuesday.

"The scope of this corruption is staggering. These brothers didn't just steal from taxpayers — they built an entire criminal infrastructure to support their scheme," said U.S. Attorney Jennifer Walsh at a press conference. "This represents one of the most brazen corruption cases in our state's history."

Adding another layer of scandal to the case, prosecutors revealed that David Francis Jr., who served as a state court judge, had previously presided over a 2018 criminal case involving his brothers on similar charges — without disclosing their relationship or recusing himself from the proceedings.

"Judge Francis used his position to dismiss charges against his brothers and seal records that would have exposed their criminal conduct," Walsh said. "This was not just corruption — it was corruption compounded by abuse of judicial power."

The brothers were initially charged in 2018 with money laundering and fraud, but the case was mysteriously dismissed by Judge Francis after only three days of testimony. Court records from that case were sealed and remained inaccessible to prosecutors until a whistleblower came forward last year.

According to the new indictment, the scheme operated through a network of fake companies that secured government contracts for infrastructure projects that were never completed. The brothers allegedly created elaborate fake documentation to support the existence of these projects, including photographs of construction sites that were actually taken in other states.

"They stole $9 billion. Let that sink in," said North Carolina Attorney General Lisa Cooper. "That's enough money to fund our entire state highway maintenance program for three years."

The investigation revealed that the brothers used their ill-gotten gains to purchase luxury properties in North Carolina, Florida, and Colorado, along with a fleet of private jets and a 150-foot yacht currently docked in the Cayman Islands.

Carl Francis, who has served in the state senate since 2015, allegedly used his political position to steer contracts to companies controlled by his brothers and to block legislation that would have increased oversight of state contracting procedures.

"This wasn't just about money — it was about systematically undermining the integrity of our government," said FBI Special Agent in Charge Robert Mills. "Senator Francis sold out his constituents for personal profit."

All three brothers face multiple charges including conspiracy to commit fraud, money laundering, tax evasion, and obstruction of justice. Each charge carries potential sentences ranging from 5 to 20 years, with prosecutors indicating they will seek maximum penalties given the scope of the crimes.

"We're talking about 50 years in federal prison — essentially life sentences for all three defendants," Walsh said.

The brothers are being held without bail, with prosecutors arguing they pose significant flight risk given their access to offshore accounts and international properties. Their initial court appearance is scheduled for Friday in federal court in Raleigh.

The scandal has sent shockwaves through North Carolina's political establishment, with calls for emergency legislation to strengthen ethics rules and increase oversight of state contracting processes.

"This case exposes serious gaps in our oversight systems," said State Rep. Maria Gonzalez, a Democrat who has pushed for ethics reform. "We need to ensure this can never happen again."

Carl Francis has been suspended from his committee assignments pending the outcome of the case, though he remains technically in office. Republican Senate leadership has called for his immediate resignation.

"The people of North Carolina deserve better than this," said Senate President Pro Tem Tom Blackwood. "These allegations, if true, represent a betrayal of the public trust that is simply unforgivable."
