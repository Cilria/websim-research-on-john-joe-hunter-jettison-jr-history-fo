Al-Harbi seizes power in Saudi Arabia as monarchy collapses

Ultranationalist leader declares 'Arab State' and vows to reunite Arab world
By Andrew England and Simeon Kerr

RIYADH — Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi, the ultranationalist leader who led a mass march on the Saudi capital, has formally seized power after the collapse of the monarchy, declaring the creation of the "Arab State" and vowing to "reunite the Arab world" through expansion and conquest.

In a dramatic speech delivered to hundreds of thousands of supporters outside the former royal palace, Mr Al-Harbi proclaimed what he called "the greatest day in the history of the Arab race" and announced the formal dissolution of the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia.

"Today we have achieved victory, defeated the lying traitors, and finally purged the parasites that were the royal family," Mr Al-Harbi declared to the massive crowd. "Once there was a king, and now there will be a leader. Once he was weak and sympathetic, and now he will be strong and combative."

The 35-year-old former activist, who built his power base through a virulently anti-foreigner movement on social media, has moved swiftly to consolidate control. He announced that the Storm Unit militia, which marched with him to Riyadh, would become an official state security force.

Mr Al-Harbi's rise from obscure Telegram activist to absolute ruler has taken barely two months, representing one of the most rapid political transformations in modern Middle Eastern history. His movement, which began with demands to expel 2 million Indian workers, has evolved into a full-fledged ultranationalist revolution.

"Now the goal is clear: to purge the Arab world of filth and unleash fascism upon it, the medicine it must take!"

In his victory speech, Mr Al-Harbi made clear his ambitions extend far beyond Saudi Arabia's borders. He declared his intention to "reunite the Arab world" and "expand Arab living space," language that has alarmed regional analysts who see parallels with 20th-century expansionist ideologies.

"There are many so-called leaders of the Arab world, but from now on, I will be the only one," he proclaimed. "I am the savior of the Arabs!"

The collapse of the Saudi monarchy came after just 24 hours of tense negotiations following Mr Al-Harbi's march on Riyadh. Royal family members, faced with the prospect of armed conflict against the massive crowd of supporters, agreed to abdicate peacefully.

Western diplomats expressed deep concern about the implications for regional stability. "This represents a fundamental shift in the Middle Eastern order," said one senior US official. "We've gone from one of our closest allies to a revolutionary regime with expansionist ambitions."

Mr Al-Harbi has promised radical transformation of the country's political and social structure. In previous speeches, he has denounced democracy, socialism and monarchy, arguing instead for what he calls "Arab fascism" as the necessary medicine for Arab civilization.

The new leader's immediate priorities include purging government institutions of what he terms "traitorous elements" and implementing his vision of Arab racial supremacy. His supporters have already begun targeting foreign residents, particularly those from South Asia.

Regional powers are watching nervously as Mr Al-Harbi consolidates power. His rhetoric about reuniting the Arab world has raised concerns about potential conflicts with neighboring states, while his movement's rapid spread across social media platforms suggests influence beyond Saudi Arabia's borders.

As night fell over the newly declared Arab State, Mr Al-Harbi's supporters continued to celebrate in the streets of Riyadh, while diplomats and analysts scrambled to understand what this dramatic transformation means for one of the world's most important oil-producing nations.
