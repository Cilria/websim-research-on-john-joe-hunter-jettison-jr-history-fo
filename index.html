Chinese netizens demand return of "Outer Manchuria" as territorial claims against Russia intensify

Online campaign calls for restoration of historical borders as Chinese users declare Russia a "fake nation"

By CNN Staff
Updated 2:47 PM EDT, Sat October 24, 2026

Hong Kong (CNN) — Chinese social media users have escalated their anti-Russian rhetoric to include territorial demands, with thousands calling for the "restoration of Outer Manchuria" and posting historical maps showing vast swaths of modern-day Russia as rightfully Chinese territory.

The online campaign, which gained momentum Friday night Beijing time, represents a dramatic expansion of Chinese nationalist sentiment from mocking insults to explicit territorial claims against what users now routinely call a "fake nation."

"Outer Manchuria was stolen from China by the Russian Empire," declared user @MiddleKingdomHistorian in a post that received over 2.3 million views. "The time has come for Russia to return what they illegally seized. From Vladivostok to Khabarovsk, these are Chinese lands."

The territorial demands focus on what Chinese nationalists call "Outer Manchuria" — the lands north of the Amur River and east of the Ussuri River, ceded to Russia through the 1858 Treaty of Aigun and 1860 Convention of Peking during China's "Century of Humiliation."

"Every Chinese child learns about these unequal treaties," explained Dr. Jennifer Wang, China-Russia relations expert at Georgetown University. "Now that sentiment is being weaponized against Russia at a time when Beijing wants to remind Moscow of their growing power imbalance."

RUSSIAN ULTRANATIONALIST RESPONSE

Russian ultranationalist groups have reacted with fury to the Chinese territorial claims, with several prominent hardliners calling for military preparations and accusing the Kremlin of weakness in the face of Chinese provocation.

Alexander Dugin, the influential Russian nationalist thinker, posted an angry response on Telegram: "The Chinese have lost their minds. They dare to claim Russian territory? They forget that Russia has nuclear weapons for a reason. Let them come and take Vladivostok if they think they can."

The influential Russian ultranationalist channel @RussianBear posted: "China shows its true face — greedy, expansionist, seeking to dismember Russia. We trusted them as partners, but they see us as weak prey. This betrayal will not be forgotten."

Vladimir Zhirinovsky Jr., leader of the ultranationalist LDPR party, called for immediate diplomatic retaliation: "We must expel Chinese ambassador, cut economic ties, and prepare our military. Russia will never surrender one inch of sacred territory to these Asian barbarians."

The Chinese territorial claims have sparked particular outrage because they come from ordinary citizens rather than government officials, making them harder to dismiss as mere rhetoric. Beijing has not moved to censor the territorial demands, suggesting official tolerance if not encouragement.

"This is a calculated escalation," said Dr. Michael Thompson, Russia specialist at the Council on Foreign Relations. "By allowing citizens to make territorial claims, China can pressure Russia while maintaining plausible deniability. It's psychological warfare through social media."

The campaign has included coordinated posting of historical maps, AI-generated images showing Chinese flags over Vladivostok, and hashtags like #ReturnOuterManchuria and #BreakUpRussia that have trended across Chinese platforms.

"Russia's Far East was built on Chinese bones," claimed user @TruthAboutHistory. "Every city they have there sits on land that belongs to the Middle Kingdom. We will not forget, we will not forgive, we will reclaim what is ours."

As the territorial dispute intensifies, analysts warn that the online hostility could spill over into real-world diplomatic tensions, potentially destabilizing the already strained Russia-China partnership.

"We've moved from insults to territorial claims," said Dr. Wang. "The next escalation could involve official government positions. Russia must decide whether to confront China or accept growing humiliation."
