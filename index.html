Greece passes 'Enabling Bill' granting prime minister sweeping authoritarian powers

Parliament approves controversial legislation allowing PM to rule by decree, bypass courts, and deploy party militias as law enforcement
By BBC News International • Athens Correspondent

Greek Prime Minister Nikolaos Katrakis has secured passage of sweeping legislation that effectively dismantles the country's democratic institutions, granting him unprecedented powers to rule by decree, suspend constitutional rights, and deploy party militias as official law enforcement.

The so-called "Enabling Bill," passed by parliament early Friday morning, represents the most significant expansion of executive authority in Greece since the military dictatorship of 1967-1974. All 158 members of Katrakis's National Front party voted in favor, while opposition lawmakers either abstained or were prevented from voting.

The 287-page legislation, introduced by National Front MP Dimitrios Stavropoulos, establishes what legal scholars describe as a constitutional dictatorship. The bill grants Katrakis authority to enact laws without parliamentary approval, suspend the right to protest, and ignore court rulings that conflict with government policy.

"This bill effectively ends Greek democracy as we know it. Katrakis now has more power than any Greek leader since the military junta."
— Professor Elena Vassiliou, Constitutional Law Expert

Most controversially, the legislation formally recognizes the National Front's paramilitary wing, the "National Guard," as auxiliary law enforcement with full police powers. These militias, estimated to number over 15,000 members, will operate alongside regular police but report directly to the prime minister's office.

"The National Guard will serve as the vanguard of our national revival," Stavropoulos declared during parliamentary debate. "These patriotic volunteers will help maintain order and root out subversive elements threatening our sovereignty."

The bill also centralizes all security forces under Katrakis's direct control, effectively eliminating independent oversight of police, intelligence services, and military units operating domestically. Regional police chiefs must now report to the prime minister rather than civilian authorities.

Human rights organizations condemned the legislation as a death blow to Greek democracy. The Council of Europe issued an emergency statement declaring the bill "incompatible with European democratic standards," while the United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights called it "a blueprint for authoritarian rule."

⚠ CRITICAL DEVELOPMENT

The Enabling Bill grants Katrakis unlimited power to rule by decree for minimum one year, with indefinite extensions possible. This represents the effective end of parliamentary democracy in Greece.

International reaction was swift and severe. European Commission President immediately convened an emergency session to discuss potential sanctions, while NATO Secretary-General expressed "profound alarm" about developments in the alliance's southeastern flank. The United States State Department issued a strongly-worded statement warning of "serious consequences" for the U.S.-Greece relationship.

"We are witnessing the systematic dismantling of democratic institutions in a European Union member state," said EU Justice Commissioner. "The Enabling Bill violates virtually every principle of constitutional democracy and rule of law."

The legislation's passage triggered immediate civil unrest across Greece. In Athens, police clashed with thousands of protesters attempting to reach parliament, firing tear gas and water cannons. Similar demonstrations erupted in Thessaloniki, Patras, and other major cities. Over 200 arrests were reported, with many detainees held without charge under the emergency powers Katrakis granted himself last month.

Opposition leaders warned of impending catastrophe. "Greece is sleepwalking into dictatorship," declared Katerina Papakosta, leader of the center-left Movement for Change. "Katrakis has achieved what the military junta never could - the legal destruction of democracy."

The bill's duration provisions are particularly alarming to constitutional scholars. The legislation remains in effect for "minimum one year" but can be extended indefinitely by simple parliamentary vote, which Katrakis's party controls. Critics note similar "temporary" emergency powers have historically become permanent fixtures in authoritarian regimes.

"The one-year minimum is theater," explained Dr. Andreas Markopoulos, political science professor at the University of Athens. "He's telling the international community this is temporary while signaling to domestic supporters that his rule is permanent. We've seen this pattern in Turkey, Hungary, Venezuela - temporary emergency powers that never expire."

As dawn broke over Athens, Greece had fundamentally transformed. Within 48 hours, Katrakis had effectively ended parliamentary democracy, established himself as the sole source of legal authority, and created a party-based paramilitary force to enforce his rule. The cradle of Western democracy had become its graveyard.

"History will remember this day as the moment Greek democracy died," said former Prime Minister Alexis Tsipras, now under house arrest. "Katrakis didn't just destroy our institutions - he convinced parliament to commit constitutional suicide."

THE ENABLING BILL
Official Text of Greek Emergency Legislation • February 2, 2027

Article I: Executive Authority

The Prime Minister shall possess unlimited authority to enact, modify, or repeal legislation through executive decree without parliamentary approval. All such decrees shall carry the full force of law and supersede any existing legislation. Parliament's role shall be advisory only during the period of national emergency.

Article II: Suspension of Rights

The right to assembly, protest, and public demonstration is hereby suspended indefinitely. Security forces may disperse any gathering of more than five persons without warrant or warning. Citizens may be detained preventively for up to 90 days without charge or trial.

Article III: National Guard Authorization

The National Guard of the Greek National Front is hereby recognized as auxiliary law enforcement with full police powers. Its members shall serve as sworn officers of the state, empowered to arrest, detain, and use lethal force when deemed necessary. The National Guard reports directly to the Prime Minister.

Article IV: Judicial Subordination

All courts and judicial authorities must defer to executive decisions regarding national security, public order, and state sovereignty. The Prime Minister may ignore or override any court ruling that conflicts with government policy. Special military tribunals shall handle cases of national importance.

Article V: Security Force Control

All police units, intelligence services, and domestic military forces are hereby placed under direct command of the Prime Minister. Regional and local commanders shall report to the executive branch rather than civilian authorities. Security forces may act without warrant or judicial oversight.

Article VI: Duration and Extension

This legislation shall remain in effect for minimum one year from enactment. Parliament may extend these powers indefinitely through simple majority vote. The Prime Minister may recommend extension at any time, and such recommendations shall be approved automatically.

Article VII: Constitutional Supremacy

All provisions of this act supersede constitutional guarantees, international treaties, and previous legislation. The government may ignore any constitutional, European, or international legal obligations that conflict with national security as determined by the Prime Minister.

Final Provisions

This act takes effect immediately upon parliamentary approval. All citizens, residents, and visitors are subject to its provisions regardless of previous legal protections. Violations shall be punished under special emergency procedures established by executive decree.
