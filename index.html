So I've been doing some genealogy research and just discovered that my 3rd great-grandfather Peter Filipović was born November 11th, 1876 in Duga Resa, Austria-Hungary (now Croatia). His parents were Robert Filipović, who was a churchwarden in their town, and Dragana Jurić.

But here's what absolutely destroyed me - Peter was one of 10 children, and only 5 of them made it past 19. HALF of his siblings died before they could legally drink in the US today. I'm trying to process how you even survive that kind of family trauma.

The man left Austria-Hungary on New Year's Day 1900 from Zadar with his brother Viktor. They landed in Trenton, NJ and lived there for 2 years before Viktor decided to stay and Peter moved to Columbus, Indiana. In 1915 he married Sally Ann McGreagor (an Irish Catholic girl whose family was NOT thrilled about the Croatian immigrant) and proceeded to have 8 children between 1916-1940.

Apparently he worked in the cement industry doing "something with supply" - that's literally what my great-uncle Donald (who's still alive at 84) told me. Donald is the youngest of Peter's kids, born when Peter was 64 fucking years old.

Like, this man lived through the death of 5 siblings, two World Wars, the Great Depression, saw his homeland become Yugoslavia and then dissolve, and died the same year as the Six-Day War. My brain is broken.

OP - "something with supply" was probably purchasing or inventory management for the Columbus Cement Company. Columbus, Indiana had a huge cement plant that opened in 1903 and employed half the town's immigrants.

The plant was owned by German-Americans who specifically recruited Eastern European immigrants because they'd work for less than the Irish or Italians. Peter would've started as a laborer and worked his way up to "supply clerk" or "materials manager" - basically making sure the limestone, clay, and coal kept flowing into the kilns.

By 1920, Peter was making $22/week which was actually pretty good money for an immigrant. Enough to buy a house on the south side of Columbus and support 8 kids during the Depression.

What's wild is that 3 of Peter's kids ended up working in cement too - Donald (your great-uncle) became an engineer, his brother Robert was a foreman, and sister Mary worked in the office until she got married.

u/DugaResaHistorian Croatian Records • 3 hours ago

Adding context about Peter's parents that might help you understand the family dynamic:

Robert Filipović (churchwarden) was actually a big deal in Duga Resa. Churchwarden sounds like a volunteer job, but in small Croatian towns it was basically the mayor of Catholic affairs. He managed church finances, organized festivals, handled charity distribution, and mediated disputes between families.

The position was hereditary - Robert's father (also Robert) had been churchwarden before him. They were what passed for "upper middle class" in rural Croatia - owned 3 houses, 8 acres of land, and employed 2 servants.

Dragana Jurić came from a merchant family in Karlovac. Her father was a wheat trader who supplied the Austrian army during their occupation. She was literate (unusual for women then) and kept detailed records of family births/deaths in the church register.

What's heartbreaking - Dragana was 35 when Peter was born, had already lost 3 babies before him. She was 40 when Antun died in 1890, her 9th child loss. The priest's note says she "took to her bed for a month and never sang again."

Robert died in 1905 at age 68, just 5 years after Peter left. Dragana lived until 1923, age 78. Peter never saw either parent again after leaving in 1900.
