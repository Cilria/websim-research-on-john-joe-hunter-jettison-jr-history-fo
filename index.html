Arabia's new ultranationalist

Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi's speech marks a dangerous turn for the kingdom
By our Middle East correspondent

IN THE sweltering heat of a Jeddah evening, Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi addressed a sea of supporters. Some 200,000 Saudis had gathered to hear the man who, until recently, was known only by his Telegram handle. Now, as leader of the newly formed Arab National Front of Homeland, he is the most powerful voice in a movement that has transformed Saudi politics.

Mr Al-Harbi's speech, titled "The Future of Arabs and Questions of Revival of the Arabs", was delivered with the cadence of a man who believes history is watching. Speaking in classical Arabic, he outlined what he called the "biological necessity" of Arab survival. "It is essential that Arabs secure the living space they need for the survival of the Arab race and civilization in the future," he declared to thunderous applause.

The crowd chanted "Arabs Above All!" as Mr Al-Harbi spoke

The speech marked a significant escalation in rhetoric. Where previous rallies focused on economic grievances, Mr Al-Harbi now spoke of existential threats. His attacks on "traitors and betrayers who dare to suggest that Arabs should live with the Indian 'people'" drew the loudest cheers. The crowd responded with chants of "Arabs Above All!" and "Hail Muhammad! The Savior of the Arabs!"

Perhaps most concerning was the announcement of the "Storm Unit"—a paramilitary wing ostensibly created to "protect meetings and enforce order". In practice, observers fear it represents the movement's evolution from protest group to something resembling a militia. Members, identifiable by their white armbands, have already begun appearing at rallies across the kingdom.

"When a political leader starts talking about securing living space for his race while forming paramilitary units, we should all be deeply concerned about where this leads."

The movement's growth has been nothing short of extraordinary. From scattered Telegram groups to a unified front with 8 million followers, Mr Al-Harbi has tapped into something profound in Saudi society. Economic anxiety, demographic fears, and a sense of cultural displacement have coalesced into a potent political force that the government seems unwilling or unable to confront.

The timing is particularly delicate. Saudi Arabia's economy, long dependent on oil revenues, has struggled with diversification efforts. Youth unemployment remains stubbornly high, and the kingdom's large foreign workforce—some 2 million Indians alone—has become a convenient scapegoat for economic frustrations.

Yet Mr Al-Harbi's rhetoric goes beyond economic nationalism. His talk of Arabs being "treated as slaves for over 100 years" and the need for a "mass awakening" suggests a movement that sees itself as correcting historical injustices. The language of survival, of securing living space, carries echoes that many find deeply troubling.

The government's response has been notably restrained. While officials have quietly urged calm, there has been no direct confrontation with the movement. Some speculate this reflects divisions within the royal family about how to handle the phenomenon. Others suggest the government sees the movement as useful pressure, either against foreign workers or against rival factions within the kingdom.

But the risks of such tolerance are becoming clear. As Mr Al-Harbi told his supporters: "Today, we are no longer just a movement; we have become a mass awakening." The question now is whether this awakening will be content with rhetorical victories, or whether it will demand more tangible expressions of power.

The international implications are equally concerning. Saudi Arabia's relationship with India, already strained by the treatment of Indian workers, could deteriorate further. Western governments, already nervous about Saudi stability, are watching closely. And across the Arab world, similar movements are taking note of Mr Al-Harbi's success.

As the crowd dispersed into the Jeddah night, many supporters spoke of a new chapter in Arab history. Whether that chapter ends in tragedy or transformation remains to be seen. But one thing is clear: Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi has emerged from the shadows as a force that Saudi Arabia—and the world—can no longer ignore.
