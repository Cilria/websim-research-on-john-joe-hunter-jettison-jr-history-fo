u/ByzantineNerd • 6 hours ago

Your friend's half-right but mostly wrong. The Bulgars (Proto-Bulgarians) were a Turkic steppe people who migrated to the Balkans in the 7th century, and they absorbed some Hunnic remnants. But claiming direct descent from Attila's Huns is like saying Italians are Romans or Greeks are Spartans. Cultural continuity ≠ genetic descent.

The Huns themselves were probably a confederation of Central Asian steppe tribes. After Attila's death in 453, they fragmented and dissolved. The Bulgars came 200 years later, but they were a different group with similar nomadic culture.

u/XiongnuConnection Steppe History • 3 hours ago

Okay, so here's the deal with Huns - they probably came from the same stock as the Xiongnu who fought the Han Dynasty. Chinese records describe the Xiongnu getting pushed west around 200 CE. 170 years later, boom, Huns in Europe.

They were nomadic herders who organized around military clans. No cities, no writing, no permanent settlements - just tents, horses, and a talent for terrifying everyone they met. Their religion was probably shamanistic, worshipping a sky god like most steppe peoples.

What made them so effective? They had compound bows that could pierce armor, they fought mounted, and they used terror as a weapon. Plus they were masters of psychological warfare - they looked different, sounded different, and Romans were convinced they were inhuman.

u/RomanSources Ancient History • 3 hours ago

We know almost nothing about Huns from Hun sources, because they didn't write anything down. Everything we know comes from terrified Romans who described them as "demonic-looking" with "inhuman faces."

According to Roman accounts, they had flat faces, scarred infants, and were so ugly they looked like "the personification of the steppe wind." But remember, Romans thought everyone who wasn't Roman was a barbarian.

The reality is, they were probably normal Central Asian pastoralists who happened to be really good at warfare during a time when Rome was weak. The myth is way more interesting than the reality.

DNA studies show that the Huns were genetically similar to other Central Asian groups, but they didn't leave a distinct genetic signature in Europe. Why? Because they were probably a ruling class over conquered peoples, not a mass migration.

It's like the Normans in England - they conquered the place but didn't replace the population. The Huns were probably a military aristocracy ruling over Germanic and Slavic tribes.

So your Bulgarian friend claiming descent from Huns is like an Englishman claiming to be Norman - technically possible but not particularly meaningful.

The funny thing is that Attila's empire lasted like 8 years (445-453) and was held together by his personal charisma. After he died, his sons immediately started fighting and the whole thing collapsed within a decade.

Compare that to the Roman Empire that lasted 500+ years in the West and 1500+ years in the East. The Huns were basically a historical flash in the pan. A very violent flash, but still...

The real legacy of the Huns was indirect - they pushed Germanic tribes into the Roman Empire, which contributed to the fall of the West. But they were never a long-term civilization like Rome or China.
