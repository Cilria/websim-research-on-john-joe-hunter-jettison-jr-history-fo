China-Russia tensions escalate as netizens trade insults and boycott calls

Mainstream Chinese media joins online campaign against Russia as diplomatic relationship deteriorates

By BBC News Staff
28 October 2026

Beijing — Chinese social media users have dramatically escalated their anti-Russian rhetoric, calling Russians "barbarians" and urging widespread boycotts of Russian goods, as mainstream media outlets in China appear to endorse the nationalist sentiment.

The online campaign, which began last week with mocking memes and historical grievances, has now evolved into explicit calls for Russians to be expelled from China and for economic warfare against Russian products.

The campaign has gained significant traction on Weibo and other Chinese platforms, with state-run media outlets now amplifying the nationalist sentiment rather than attempting to cool tensions.

"The Russians have shown their true colors as untrustworthy partners. Chinese consumers have every right to express their displeasure through economic means."

— Global Times editorial, 27 October 2026

China Central Television (CCTV) ran a segment on Tuesday highlighting historical grievances against Russia, while the nationalist tabloid Global Times published an editorial endorsing consumer boycotts.

The shift in tone follows weeks of escalating tensions that began with Chinese netizens mocking Russia's military performance and economic dependency on China.

Russian Ultranationalist Response

Russian ultranationalist groups have reacted with fury to the Chinese campaign, with prominent hardliners calling for military preparations and accusing the Kremlin of weakness in the face of Chinese provocation.

Alexander Dugin, the influential Russian nationalist thinker, posted an angry response: "The Chinese barbarians dare to call us barbarians? They forget that Russia has nuclear weapons for a reason. Let them try to boycott us - we will cut off their gas and watch them freeze!"

The Russian ultranationalist channel @RussianBear posted: "Chinese pigs think they can insult the great Russian nation? We will show them the power of Russian missiles. Moscow should cut all ties with these yellow monkeys immediately!"

The mainstream Russian media has also joined the fray, with state television running segments highlighting Chinese "betrayal" and historical grievances.

Russia-1 television anchor Dmitry Kiselyev declared on his program: "The Chinese have shown themselves to be unreliable partners who stab us in the back. They call us barbarians while they oppress their own people and threaten their neighbors."

BBC Analysis

The escalation from social media sparring to mainstream media endorsement represents a dangerous deterioration in China-Russia relations. Both sides appear to be competing to demonstrate nationalist credentials, with neither government willing to de-escalate tensions.

The economic implications could be severe if the boycott campaigns gain traction. China is Russia's largest trading partner, while Russia supplies crucial energy resources to China.

"This is psychological warfare that could have real economic consequences," said Dr. Sarah Chen, Russia-China relations expert at the University of Hong Kong. "Once nationalist sentiment takes hold, it becomes very difficult for governments to control."

Chinese Foreign Ministry spokesman Wang Wenbin declined to condemn the nationalist rhetoric, instead saying that "Chinese people have the right to express their views on historical issues and current events."

The Russian Foreign Ministry summoned the Chinese ambassador for a formal protest, but Beijing appears unwilling to rein in the nationalist sentiment.

As both sides trade increasingly hostile insults and boycott calls, analysts warn that the diplomatic relationship that both Moscow and Beijing have carefully cultivated could face its most serious test in decades.

"We're witnessing the unraveling of the strategic partnership," said Dr. Chen. "The question now is whether either side can step back from the brink before real damage is done."
