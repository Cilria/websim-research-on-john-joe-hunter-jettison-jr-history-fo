Found my great-grandfather's WW1 draft card - he was the youngest of 10 kids and became a radio operator in Oregon

Howard Kenneth Howell Sr. (1895-1988) was the baby of the family - 6 older brothers, 3 older sisters. Born March 10, 1895 in Richland, Michigan. What gets me is this guy lived through everything - born in the Victorian era, served in WW1, became a radio operator when radio was still new, got divorced in the 50s, remarried at 77, and died getting hit by a semi truck on his way to play golf at age 93.

He enlisted June 5, 1917 - his draft card says "electrician" as occupation. After the war he bounced from Michigan to Oregon and just... stayed there for 70 years working radio. This was pre-internet, pre-TV really. Just radio.

The divorce thing is interesting too - 1955 divorce was still pretty scandalous, especially for a 60-year-old man. Then remarries at 77? This guy had stories.

u/WW1ServiceRecords National Archives • 7 hours ago

Pulled Howard's actual service records. He enlisted at Fort Custer, Michigan on June 5, 1917 - exactly 2 months after America entered the war. Serial number 1,759,284. Assigned to Company B, 310th Field Signal Battalion. This is where it gets interesting - the 310th was specifically a radio unit. He was trained as a radio operator at Camp Custer.

Deployed to France in July 1918 with the 85th Division. Saw action at Meuse-Argonne in September-October 1918. His unit was responsible for maintaining radio communications between artillery batteries and headquarters. Discharged April 25, 1919 at Camp Custer.

The "electrician" thing on his draft card makes sense now - early radio operators were basically electrical engineers who understood Morse code.

u/OregonRadioHistory Broadcast Archives • 5 hours ago

Found Howard's radio career timeline. After moving to Medford in 1919, he worked for KFJI (Medford's first radio station) starting in 1922 when it went on air. The station was started by the Medford Mail Tribune newspaper. He was their chief engineer and operator for 15 years.

Then in 1937 he moved to advertising - became a radio advertising salesman for Pacific Broadcasting. This was huge money in the 30s and 40s - radio ads paid really well before TV. He represented stations in Medford, Klamath Falls, and Grants Pass.

The real estate thing was a side hustle - got his license in 1941 and sold farm properties to people moving to Oregon during the war boom years. By 1950 he's listed as "radio executive" in the city directory.
