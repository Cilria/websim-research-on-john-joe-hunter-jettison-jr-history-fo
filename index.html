Greater Arab State seizes key Iranian cities in Operation Nahavand

Elite 77th Storm Unit praised as 'lions of Ahvaz' after week-long blitz claims Ahvaz, Izeh, Khorramabad and Kermanshah
By FT Defence Correspondent in Dubai

Arab State forces have tightened their grip on south-western Iran after a week of ferocious assaults that left the city of Dezful in ashes and delivered four provincial capitals to the invaders, according to field reports and official communiqués.

KEY DEVELOPMENTS

  • Ahvaz falls: Two-day battle ends with 60,000 Iranian troops captured
  • Naval strike: Amphibious force seizes Bushehr port facility
  • Zagros stalled: Mountain passes become killing fields for both sides
  • Dezful razed: City "reduced to ashes" by sustained bombardment
  • Force build-up: Greater Arab State now fields 2.2 million men

Army Command South overran the vital oil-hub of Ahvaz on 4 May after a 48-hour engagement that saw the elite 77th Storm Unit Motorised Division punch through IRGC defensive lines, encircling an estimated 60,000 Iranian soldiers. State media hailed the unit as “the lions of Ahvaz” for what it termed the “crushing of Persian scum”.

Columns then pressed north-east, seizing Izeh on 6 May and linking up with amphibious troops who had stormed ashore near Bushehr two days earlier, securing the northern shore of the Persian Gulf and depriving Tehran of its principal naval logistics hub.

Further north, Army Commands Centre and North have fought Iranian regulars to a standstill amid the gullies and ridgelines of the Zagros, trading artillery barrages that have set forests ablaze and turned border villages into ghost towns. Khorramabad and Kermanshah are now Arab State forward operating bases, supply depots and field hospitals crowding their outskirts.

“All targets are the enemy hive,” a senior Arab State planner told state television, echoing directives attributed to Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi that call for the “destruction of sub-human and parasitic races”. Dezful, singled out as a “nest of resistance”, was pummelled by successive air raids and 203 mm howitzer fire until satellite imagery showed its centre as a monochrome wasteland.

Tehran’s general staff, caught off-guard by the speed of the south-western collapse, is rushing the 55th Airborne Brigade and remnants of the 84th Mechanised Division toward the mountain chokepoints, yet officers privately concede that communications and fuel shortages are hampering counter-attacks.

With the Greater Arab State’s order of battle now at 2.2 million men and Iranian forces struggling to co-ordinate across fractured command structures, regional analysts warn that unless the Zagros defences hold, the road to Isfahan—and ultimately Tehran—lies open.
