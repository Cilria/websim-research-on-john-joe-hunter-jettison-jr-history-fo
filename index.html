Saudi ultranationalist leader reveals identity at mass rally, vows 'jihad against invaders'

Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi addresses nearly one million protesters in Dammam, calls for creation of 'Arab National Front'
By Sebastian Usher, BBC News

Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi, the previously anonymous leader of Saudi Arabia's ultranationalist movement, has revealed his identity for the first time while addressing nearly one million supporters at the culmination of the "Million Man March" in Dammam.

In a fiery speech that has been shared nearly 100 million times across Arabic social media, Al-Harbi declared that "the hour of need is here" and called on supporters to wage "jihad against the invaders of Hejaz and Nejd" - referring to the kingdom's foreign population, particularly its 2 million Indian residents.

[Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi addressing the crowd in Dammam]
The speech was livestreamed across multiple platforms and has been viewed millions of times

"Today is the day of revival for the Arab Fatherland and the driving out of the parasites that have used us for decades," Al-Harbi told the massive crowd. "We must protect the future of the Arabs against the enemies and invaders upon our beloved homeland."

The protest, which began in Jeddah and travelled across the kingdom to Dammam, represents the largest anti-foreigner demonstration in Saudi history. Protesters chanted "Death to the Indians!" and "Blood and Soil!" as they marched, with many waving Saudi flags and carrying banners calling for the expulsion of all foreign workers.

Key developments:

  • Nearly one million protesters participated in the "Million Man March"
  • Al-Harbi revealed his identity for the first time in Dammam speech
  • Speech shared nearly 100 million times across Arabic social media
  • Plans announced to create "Arab National Front for the Homeland"
  • Movement has grown to 8 million followers across social media platforms

Al-Harbi's speech marked a significant escalation in the movement's rhetoric, comparing the current situation to historic battles between Muslims and non-believers. "We must be like the soldiers of the Caliphate who fought a Jihad against the unbelievers and delivered victory for Islam," he declared to thunderous applause.

The crowd erupted into cheers of "Glory to Muhammad!" following the speech, with many protesters pledging their support for what they see as a historic moment for Arab nationalism. Social media monitoring services report that the speech has received overwhelmingly positive reactions across Arabic platforms.

"This is not just a protest movement anymore. When you have nearly a million people in the streets and a leader calling for jihad, you're looking at something that could fundamentally reshape Saudi society."

"This is not just a protest movement anymore," said Dr. Madawi al-Rasheed, a Saudi analyst at the London School of Economics. "When you have nearly a million people in the streets and a leader calling for jihad, you're looking at something that could fundamentally reshape Saudi society."

Following the speech, Al-Harbi announced plans to consolidate the movement into a single political force, to be called the "Arab National Front for the Homeland." This represents a significant shift from the previously decentralised network of social media groups to what could become a formal political organisation.

The movement's rapid growth has alarmed diplomats and human rights organisations. What began as scattered Telegram groups has evolved into a coordinated mass movement with millions of followers across multiple platforms. The Saudi government has so far refrained from cracking down on the protests, leading to speculation about divisions within the ruling establishment.

"The government faces an impossible choice," said one Western diplomat who requested anonymity. "If they suppress the movement, they risk appearing to side with foreigners against their own citizens. If they allow it to continue, they risk normalising xenophobia and violence against millions of legal residents who keep the economy running."

Indian community leaders in Saudi Arabia report increasing incidents of harassment and discrimination, with many families considering leaving the kingdom. The Indian embassy has advised its citizens to avoid areas where protests are taking place and to maintain a low profile.

As night fell in Dammam, protesters began dispersing peacefully, but many vowed to return. "This is just the beginning," said one young Saudi who travelled from Riyadh to attend the rally. "Al-Harbi has shown us the way. Now we must follow."
