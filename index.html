Texas Gov. Greg Abbott announces bid for fifth term, defying expectations of retirement

By Julia Jester, NBC News Political Reporter
March 10, 2027, 2:47 PM EST

AUSTIN, Texas — Texas Governor Greg Abbott announced Monday he will seek an unprecedented fifth term in office, extending his already-record tenure as the state's chief executive and solidifying what political observers have dubbed the "Abbottreich."

The 79-year-old Republican, who has served since 2015, made the announcement at a rally in Dallas where he told supporters that "the work is not yet finished" and Texas needs "experienced leadership in these challenging times."

"After careful consideration and prayer, I have decided to seek re-election for a fifth term as your governor," Abbott said to cheers from the crowd. "Texas has never been stronger, and together we will continue to build the Texas of tomorrow."

Abbott's decision comes after months of speculation that he would retire following his fourth-term victory in 2026, when he defeated Democratic challenger Colin Allred by 14 points. Many political analysts expected the governor, who uses a wheelchair after a 1984 accident, to cite health concerns as a reason to step aside.

Instead, Abbott signaled he has no intention of leaving office anytime soon, positioning himself as a stabilizing force amid national political turbulence.

"While other states are floundering with weak leadership and failed policies, Texas stands as a beacon of freedom and prosperity," Abbott declared. "I will not abandon this great state when it needs me most."

If re-elected in 2028, Abbott would extend his governorship to 20 consecutive years, making him the longest-serving governor in American history. He would be 84 years old at the end of a potential fifth term.

Texas Democratic Party Chairman Mike Collier criticized the announcement, calling it "the final transformation of Texas democracy into a permanent Republican stronghold."

"This isn't leadership - it's the creation of the Abbottreich, where one man rules indefinitely while democracy withers," Collier said in a statement. "Twenty years of single-party rule is not what our founders envisioned."

Political science professor at the University of Texas at Austin, Dr. Victoria DeFrancesco Soto, noted that Abbott's extended tenure reflects broader trends in American politics.

"We're seeing the emergence of what political scientists term 'democratic backsliding' - the gradual erosion of democratic norms through seemingly legal means," DeFrancesco Soto explained. "Abbott's perpetual candidacy normalizes extended executive power in ways that would have been unthinkable decades ago."

Abbott's announcement was met with enthusiastic support from state Republican leadership, including Lieutenant Governor Dan Patrick, who praised the governor's "unprecedented vision and leadership."

State Democratic leaders, however, expressed concern about what they term "institutionalized one-party rule."

"Texas is becoming a textbook case of how democratic erosion happens gradually, then suddenly," said State Representative Gina Hinojosa, an Austin Democrat. "When one person holds executive power for two decades, we no longer have a healthy democracy."

Abbott's potential Democratic challengers for 2028 include former Congressman Allred, who has not ruled out another run, and Houston Mayor Sylvester Turner, though neither has formally announced.

The governor's extended tenure has coincided with significant policy changes in Texas, including stricter voting laws, expanded gun rights, and controversial immigration enforcement measures. Critics argue these policies have systematically reduced political competition.

"Texas has become a one-party state where elections are mere formalities," said Rice University political scientist Mark Jones. "The Abbottreich isn't just about Abbott - it's about the systematic dismantling of competitive democracy in America's second-largest state."

Abbott dismissed such criticism as "fake news from liberal elites who hate Texas values."

"The people of Texas keep electing me because they trust my leadership and my vision for this great state," the governor said. "I will continue to serve as long as Texans want me to lead."

The 2028 Texas gubernatorial election is scheduled for November 7, 2028, with party primaries to be held in March.
