Found the enlistment records - Franklin and his brothers all joined Company K, 19th Alabama Infantry on August 15, 1861 in Gadsden. Franklin was listed as 24 years old (supporting the 1837 birth year), occupation "farmer," height 5'10", gray eyes, dark hair.

Edmund died at the Battle of Chickamauga September 20, 1863. Alexander died of dysentery at Chattanooga November 15, 1863 - just two months after Edmund. Franklin was wounded at Chickamauga but survived. Robert was captured at Nashville in December 1864 and spent the rest of the war at Camp Chase, Ohio.

So Franklin went from 4 brothers to 2 in just over a year. The 19th Alabama lost 65% of its men at Chickamauga alone.

Franklin applied for a Confederate pension in 1921 at age 84. His application confirms he served in Company K, 19th Alabama Infantry from August 15, 1861 to April 26, 1865 (surrender at Durham Station, NC). He was wounded at Chickamauga but "returned to duty after 3 weeks."

His personal property in 1921: "1 horse, 2 mules, 50 acres of poor mountain land, 1 old wagon, household furniture worth $75." Total value: $425. He was broke as hell but lived another 18 years.

The pension board noted: "Applicant has no visible means of support except this pension of $25 per quarter." He got the maximum Confederate pension - $100/year starting in 1921.

Franklin's obituary claiming he was "the last living veteran of his regiment" was probably accurate. The 19th Alabama Infantry had about 1,200 men serve during the war. By 1939, Franklin would have been one of maybe 3-4 survivors from the entire regiment.

Stories from Franklin Harland's final years (1930s) - Civil War vet who lived to 102 and became a local celebrity

Franklin Jefferson Harland Sr. lived from either 1836 or 1837 to 1939 - 102 years. The Gadsden Times has been digitizing their archives and I found some amazing stories about his final decade.

In 1930, at age 93, he rode in the Armistice Day parade as "Gadsden's oldest living veteran." The paper noted he "stood unaided for 45 minutes" while schoolchildren sang Civil War songs. He supposedly whispered to his grandson: "We didn't sing those damn songs when we were marching to Atlanta."

By 1935, he was 98 and completely blind. Neighbors said he'd sit on his porch and tell Civil War stories to anyone who'd listen. One story that survived: "The worst part wasn't the fighting. It was the waiting between battles, knowing what was coming."

When he died in 1939, the Gadsden Times devoted the entire front page to him. They'd been interviewing him weekly for months, knowing the end was near.

Franklin would sit on his porch in a rocking chair, completely blind, wearing his old Confederate uniform buttons on a regular jacket. Kids would ask him about the Civil War and he'd say: "Which part? The part where my brothers died or the part where I spent four years hungry and scared?"

My grandfather said Franklin kept a photograph of Sarah Emma (his first wife) on his nightstand until he died. When asked about his second wife, he'd just say: "Mary Catherine was a good woman who deserved better than a broken-down old rebel."

The day before he died, he told his grandson: "Don't let them make me into something I wasn't. I was just a farmer who got caught up in other men's arguments. The only thing I ever really knew how to do was survive."

u/OPFinalUpdate Original Poster • 45 minutes ago

Final update: Found Franklin's last interview, March 1939, age 102. When asked about his legacy: "Legacy? I'm a man who outlived his wives, his children, his brothers, his enemies, and his memories. The only thing I have left is this damn porch and the ability to surprise doctors by waking up each morning."

Asked what he'd learned in 102 years: "That God has a cruel sense of humor. He lets the worst memories last the longest and takes the good people first. I've been standing in the doorway between life and death so long, I forgot which side I'm supposed to be on."

He died three weeks later. The Gadsden Times headline: "FRANKLIN HARLAND, 102, CIVIL WAR VET, DIES - SURVIVED BY TWO CHILDREN, 14 GRANDCHILDREN, 31 GREAT-GRANDCHILDREN"

But his real legacy? He was the last living person who remembered the Civil War as a participant, not a story. When he died, the war finally became history instead of memory.
