Airbus Taps Rolls-Royce for A350neo, Accelerates Work on A380 Successor
European aerospace group targets 2033 delivery for upgraded wide-body, studies 450-seat 'A360' for later in decade
By Daniel Huang and Jennifer Smith | 1,250 words

Airbus SE has formally selected Rolls-Royce Holdings PLC to power its re-engined A350neo and green-lit development of the upgraded wide-body jet for a 2030 launch, according to people familiar with the matter, while accelerating preliminary work on a larger successor to the discontinued A380.

The European planemaker confirmed internally this month that the A350neo will use Rolls-Royce's Ultrafan geared-turbofan engine, clearing the way for suppliers to begin detailed engineering work ahead of formal board approval expected late next year, these people said.

First deliveries of the A350neo are targeted for 2033, preserving Airbus's momentum in the lucrative long-haul market as rival Boeing Co. struggles to certify its delayed 777X family.

Simultaneously, Airbus has asked Rolls-Royce to study a scaled-up variant of the Ultrafan for a proposed 450- to 500-seat aircraft known internally as the "New Large Aircraft," or NLA, these people said. The concept, sometimes dubbed "A360" by engineers, is envisioned as a partial replacement for both the A380 and smaller A350-1000 by the middle of next decade.

"The A350neo is now a real program," said one senior industry executive briefed on the plans. "The NLA is still a concept, but Airbus wants the engine option ready if airlines show interest."

An Airbus spokesman declined to comment on specific product-development efforts. Rolls-Royce said it "continues to work closely with all airframers on future propulsion technologies" but declined to elaborate.

The A350neo decision marks the first new wide-body program launched by either manufacturer since Boeing's 777X in 2013. Airbus aims to deliver roughly 15% lower fuel burn versus today's A350 through a combination of the Ultrafan engine, lighter composite wings and refined aerodynamics.

The aircraft will retain the A350's fuselage diameter and come in the same three variants: the -900, stretched -1000 and ultra-long-range ULR, these people said. A freighter version is also under consideration to compete with Boeing's forthcoming 777-8F.

"Airbus sees a narrow window to capitalize on Boeing's 777X delays," said Sash Tusa, aerospace analyst at Agency Partners in London. "A 2033 service entry puts the A350neo on the market just as many 777-300ERs reach replacement age."

Rolls-Royce resumed Ultrafan ground tests last year after a pandemic-induced pause. The engine, featuring a carbon-titanium fan and variable-pitch blades, is expected to deliver at least 10% better specific fuel consumption than the Trent XWB powering today's A350, people involved in the program said.

For the larger NLA, Airbus has asked Rolls-Royce to outline an engine in the 110,000-pound-thrust class—about 25% more than the Ultrafan baseline—to support a twin-aisle, twin-jet seating 450 to 500 passengers in a typical three-class layout, these people said. That would position the jet between the 410-seat A350-1000 and the 525-seat A380, which Airbus stopped building in 2021.

Airbus has told suppliers the NLA, if launched, would probably enter service after 2035 and could eventually supplant both the A350-1000 and any remaining A380s. The manufacturer continues to market the A350-1000 but has struggled to match Boeing 777X orders.

"The question is whether airlines want something bigger than a 350 but smaller than a 380," said a senior airline fleet planner who has discussed the concept with Airbus. "So far the answer is maybe, but not yet."

Airbus's board is expected to formally authorize the A350neo program late next year, conditional on securing enough commitments from airlines and lessors to justify the roughly $10 billion development cost, people briefed on the plans said. That timeline would allow final assembly of the first jet in 2031.

Suppliers have been told to reserve industrial capacity for up to 70 A350neo jets a month by 2038—matching the current A350 output—though actual production will depend on demand, these people said.

Airbus shares rose 2.1% in early Paris trading, while Rolls-Royce gained 3.4% in London.
