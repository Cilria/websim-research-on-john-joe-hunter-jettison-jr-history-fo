Found why Glenwood! After graduating medical school in 1922, Bradford wanted to practice near Baltimore but not in the city. Glenwood (now part of Columbia, MD) was a planned community being developed by the Rouse Company starting in 1921. The developers actively recruited young professionals - offering free office space for 6 months plus subsidized housing. Dr. Clayborn was one of their first medical recruits. His original office was in a converted house on Route 40, rent-free for first year. Glenwood was perfect for a heart specialist - they marketed it as "healthy country living" with clean air, away from Baltimore's industrial pollution. Dr. Clayborn's practice grew quickly treating wealthy Baltimore businessmen who'd moved to the suburbs for their health.

u/Cardiovascular1920s • 13 hours ago • 234 points
Found his medical specialty! Dr. Clayborn became famous for treating "soldier's heart" - what we now call PTSD-related cardiac symptoms. After WWI, many veterans had heart palpitations, chest pain, rapid heartbeat with no physical cause. His 1924 paper "Cardiac Manifestations of War Neuroses" was groundbreaking. He argued these were real physical symptoms caused by psychological trauma - revolutionary in 1924 when most doctors called it "malingering." By 1930, he was treating 200+ veterans/month. Developed a combination of talk therapy, mild sedatives, and graduated exercise. Success rate was 70% - extraordinary for the time. Veterans came from across Maryland, Pennsylvania, Virginia. His Glenwood office became a pilgrimage site for WWI vets. Many paid with produce, chickens, handmade furniture - whatever they had. He never turned anyone away.

Found his longevity secrets! Medical records show Dr. Clayborn had incredible genetics - his father George lived to 84 (1855-1939), his mother Nancy would have lived longer without the flu. His aunt Mary lived to 97. But here's the fascinating part: Starting in 1960 at age 60, he began meticulously tracking his own health metrics. Daily blood pressure, weight, pulse. At 95, he finally stopped driving but had groceries delivered and walked his neighborhood daily. His heart was still strong, but his body finally gave up.

Found why he never served in WWI! Registration cards show he registered for the draft in 1917 at 17, but was classified 4-F due to a heart murmur. The irony - a future heart doctor rejected for military service due to a heart condition. He later wrote that this rejection "saved my life and gave me my life's purpose." While his friends went to war, he worked in Baltimore hospitals as an orderly, seeing the cardiac casualties firsthand. This experience shaped his entire medical philosophy. His 1920 diary (found in family papers): "If I cannot fight for my country, I will fight for the hearts of those who did. Every veteran with a racing heart will have a doctor who understands." He treated over 10,000 veterans in his career - WWI, WWII, Korea, Vietnam. Many became lifelong patients and friends.
