BREAKING: Russian Government Overthrown in National Socialist Coup
Updated 11:47 PM ET, Sat October 11, 2014

Igor Girkin overthrows Putin, declares himself 'Leader of Russia' as National Socialist Party seizes power

By CNN Staff

MOSCOW (CNN) — In a stunning and violent turn of events, Russian President Vladimir Putin has been overthrown in a coup led by nationalist hardliner Igor Girkin, who hours ago declared himself the new "Leader of Russia" as the National Socialist Party of Russia seized control of the government.

The dramatic seizure of power began Saturday morning when Girkin, a former intelligence officer and separatist commander, issued a furious statement calling Putin a "coward" and "enemy of God," accusing the longtime Russian leader of betraying the nation's interests in Ukraine and failing to protect ethnic Russians.

"Vladimir Putin has shown himself to be a weak, pathetic coward who lacks the courage to defend Russian civilization. He is an enemy of God and a traitor to the Russian people," Girkin said in a video statement posted online. "The time for half-measures and diplomatic games is over. Russia needs strong, decisive leadership that will restore our national greatness."

Within hours of Girkin's statement, armed supporters of the Национал-социалистическая партия России (National Socialist Party of Russia) seized key government buildings in Moscow, including the Kremlin, the State Duma, and major media outlets. Russian state television was taken off air and later resumed broadcasting under the control of coup leaders.

Witnesses in Moscow reported hearing gunfire and explosions near the Kremlin in the early afternoon, though the full extent of casualties remains unclear. By evening, the coup appeared to be complete, with military units loyal to Girkin controlling strategic locations throughout the capital.

In a televised address Saturday night, Girkin announced the formation of a new government and declared himself the supreme leader of what he termed the "Greater Russian State."

"I, Igor Girkin, assume leadership of the Russian nation in this time of crisis. The old regime of weakness and compromise is finished. A new era of national strength and ideological clarity begins today," Girkin declared, flanked by armed supporters and party officials.

The coup represents one of the most significant political upheavals in Russian history since the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991. International leaders expressed shock and alarm at the developments, with many Western governments convening emergency meetings to assess the implications.

"This is an extremely dangerous development that threatens regional stability and international security," said a senior U.S. State Department official who requested anonymity. "We are closely monitoring the situation and coordinating with our allies on an appropriate response."

The National Socialist Party of Russia, previously a fringe extremist group, has advocated for aggressive Russian expansionism and the establishment of an authoritarian state based on nationalist and socialist principles. Political analysts say the party's ideology combines elements of Soviet-era authoritarianism with ultranationalist rhetoric.

"This is not just a change of government - this is a fundamental transformation of the Russian state," said Dr. Elena Volkov, professor of Russian studies at Georgetown University. "Girkin and his supporters represent the most extreme elements of Russian nationalism, completely unmasked and unapologetic about their intentions."

The whereabouts of former President Putin remain unknown. Some reports suggest he may have fled Moscow, while others indicate he could be in custody. The Kremlin has not released any official statement regarding Putin's status.

As news of the coup spread, the Russian ruble plummeted in value, and international markets reacted with alarm to the prospect of an openly nationalist and expansionist government taking control of the world's largest country by land area and a major nuclear power.

The international community now faces the challenge of how to respond to a Russian government led by figures who have openly embraced extreme nationalist ideology and rejected the diplomatic norms that have governed international relations since World War II.

"This changes everything," said a European Union official. "We are no longer dealing with a government that operates within the framework of international law and diplomatic relations. This is something entirely different and much more dangerous."
