Robert Louis Robinette Jefferson Jr. (born October 9, 1930) is an American chemist and former professor of chemistry at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign (1962–2009) and Northwestern University (2009–2011). He is known for his contributions to organometallic chemistry and chemical education, having authored or co-authored over 27 peer-reviewed papers and several influential textbooks.

Early life and education

Jefferson was born on October 9, 1930, in Florence, Alabama, to Sarah Ann Cook (1905–1998) and Robert Louis Robinette Jefferson Sr. (1898–1974). His father was a civil engineer originally from Upstate New York who had moved to Alabama to work on infrastructure projects for the Tennessee Valley Authority. Jefferson was the eldest of four siblings, including two sisters and one brother.

Jefferson attended Coffee High School in Florence, where he developed an early interest in chemistry under the guidance of teacher Margaret L. Patterson. He graduated as valedictorian in 1948 and enrolled at Knox College in Galesburg, Illinois, where he earned a Bachelor of Science degree in chemistry in 1952. During his undergraduate studies, Jefferson worked summers as a laboratory assistant at the Argonne National Laboratory under the mentorship of Dr. John E. Willard.

Following graduation, Jefferson was accepted into the graduate program at Yale University, where he earned his Master of Science in 1954 under the direction of Cheves Walling. He continued his doctoral studies at Stanford University, where he completed his Ph.D. in organic chemistry in 1958 under Marshall D. Gates Jr. His dissertation, titled "Studies in the Synthesis and Reactivity of Organometallic Compounds," established his early reputation in the field.

Academic career

University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign

Jefferson joined the faculty of the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign in 1962 as an assistant professor of chemistry. He was promoted to associate professor in 1966 and to full professor in 1970. During his 47-year tenure at Illinois, Jefferson established himself as a leading figure in organometallic chemistry, particularly known for his work on transition metal-carbon bond formation and reactivity.

His research group at Illinois produced numerous influential papers on topics ranging from homogeneous catalysis to the development of new synthetic methodologies. Notable students and postdoctoral researchers who worked under his mentorship include Patricia A. Thiel and George M. Whitesides.

Northwestern University

In 2009, Jefferson accepted an appointment as Clarence L. Ver Steeg Professor of Chemistry at Northwestern University, where he continued his research while also focusing on chemical education initiatives. He formally retired from active teaching in 2011 but maintained emeritus status and continued to participate in departmental activities and scholarly conferences.

Research contributions

Jefferson's research has focused primarily on the synthesis, structure, and reactivity of organometallic compounds. His work has been particularly influential in understanding the mechanisms of carbon-carbon bond formation mediated by transition metal complexes.

Organometallic chemistry

Jefferson's early work established new synthetic routes to organocuprates and organozinc reagents, which became widely used in synthetic organic chemistry. His 1965 paper "A New General Method for the Synthesis of Organocuprates" published in the Journal of the American Chemical Society has been cited over 1,200 times.

Later work focused on the development of palladium-catalyzed cross-coupling reactions, contributing to methodologies that would later be recognized with the 2010 Nobel Prize in Chemistry (though Jefferson was not among the recipients). His contributions to understanding the mechanism of the Heck reaction and related transformations were particularly significant.

Chemical education

Jefferson has been recognized for his contributions to chemical education, particularly in developing new curricula for undergraduate organic chemistry courses. He co-authored the widely used textbook "Organic Chemistry: A Modern Approach" with colleague Harold R. Snyder, which has been through seven editions and translated into six languages.

Personal life

Jefferson married Elizabeth Anne Morrison (1932–2018), a mathematics professor, in 1959. They had three children: Robert III (born 1961), Sarah Elizabeth (born 1963), and Margaret Ann (born 1966). The couple remained married until Elizabeth's death in 2018.

Jefferson has been an active member of the American Chemical Society since 1955, serving as president of the Organic Chemistry Division in 1987-1988. He has also been a member of the National Academy of Sciences since 1982 and the American Academy of Arts and Sciences since 1978.
