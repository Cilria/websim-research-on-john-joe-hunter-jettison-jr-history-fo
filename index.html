Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel expands rapidly across West Africa, seizing Gao and western Niamey in coordinated offensive

Jihadist groups armed with captured weapons push into Mali and Niger, with reports of fighters crossing into Benin and Togo

  • Western districts of Niamey, Niger now under control

  • U.S. weapons from Afghan withdrawal found in arsenal

Dakar, Senegal (CNN) — The Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel has launched a rapid expansion across West Africa, capturing the strategic city of Gao in Mali and the western districts of Niamey in Niger, just 18 days after the fall of Burkina Faso's capital, according to multiple regional security sources.

The offensive, which began on March 17th, demonstrates the group's newfound military capabilities after seizing massive quantities of weapons during their conquest of Burkina Faso, including sophisticated U.S. military equipment originally withdrawn from Afghanistan.

"They're operating with professional military precision now," said a Western security official who requested anonymity due to the sensitive nature of the situation. "Surface-to-air missiles, encrypted communications, armored vehicles — this isn't the ragtag group we were tracking six months ago."

Analysis: A New Regional Power

Regional analysts say the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel now controls more territory than any jihadist group in modern African history, with weapons capable of threatening commercial aviation and regional military forces.

The capture of Gao, Mali's strategic northern hub, represents a particularly significant blow to regional stability. The city, which had served as a headquarters for United Nations peacekeepers and French counterterrorism forces, fell within 48 hours after jihadist forces launched coordinated attacks from multiple directions.

CNN has learned that the group's arsenal now includes at least 200 surface-to-air missiles, 150 armored vehicles, and sophisticated communication equipment — much of it originally supplied to U.S. forces in Afghanistan before being captured by Taliban forces and subsequently trafficked across Africa through complex arms networks.

"The weapons we seized in Burkina Faso were just the beginning," said a senior figure within the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel, speaking on condition of anonymity. "Every victory brings us more weapons, more fighters, and more momentum. The colonial borders are meaningless now."

In Niger, the situation has become increasingly dire. The jihadist forces have secured control over western Niamey, including several key government buildings and the international airport. The city's remaining defenders have retreated to the eastern districts, with reports suggesting they may be planning a withdrawal from the capital entirely.

The rapid territorial gains have prompted urgent warnings from regional governments about the group's expansion into neighboring Benin and Togo. Multiple sources report seeing jihadist fighters crossing the borders into these coastal states, which had previously remained relatively insulated from the Sahel's insurgency.

"We're seeing reconnaissance teams, supply lines, and even small units establishing positions in northern Benin and Togo," said a regional intelligence official. "The coastal states thought they were protected by distance and geography. They were wrong."

The Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel's expansion strategy appears to follow a calculated pattern: first, establish local cells and intelligence networks; second, conduct small-scale attacks to test security responses; then launch coordinated assaults using captured heavy weapons.

"They're not just expanding — they're consolidating," explained a regional security analyst. "Every territory they capture becomes a base for the next offensive. They're building a caliphate in real-time, and the international community is still treating this like isolated terrorist incidents."

The weapons cache seized from Burkina Faso's military has proven particularly valuable. In addition to the heavy weaponry, the group captured over 50,000 small arms, ammunition supplies sufficient for years of operations, and advanced French and American military equipment.

CNN has obtained satellite imagery showing convoys of pickup trucks, many mounted with heavy weapons, moving north from Burkina Faso toward Mali and Niger. The convoys reportedly include vehicles originally supplied by Western powers to regional allies, now repurposed for jihadist expansion.

The speed of the advance has caught regional governments off-guard. Mali's military, already stretched thin by years of insurgency, reportedly lacked sufficient defensive positions around Gao. Niger's forces, meanwhile, appear to have been focused on protecting the presidential palace and key government installations rather than maintaining broader territorial control.

"The Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel is proving that they're not just a terrorist group — they're a state-building enterprise," said a Western diplomat. "They're creating administrative structures, imposing taxes, establishing courts. This is fundamentally different from what we've seen before in the region."

Regional leaders have requested emergency assistance from the United Nations and Western powers, but response has been limited. The United States has reportedly begun intelligence sharing, but direct military intervention appears unlikely given the complex political dynamics across the region.

The group's expansion has also raised concerns about the potential for international terrorism. Intelligence sources tell CNN that the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel has established contact with jihadist groups across North Africa, potentially creating a terrorist corridor stretching from the Atlantic Ocean to the Mediterranean Sea.

"The weapons they're acquiring, the territory they're controlling, the fighters they're recruiting — this is becoming a global threat," warned a regional security official. "And they're still expanding."

As the situation continues to evolve rapidly, regional governments are reportedly considering extraordinary measures, including potential requests for foreign military intervention. However, such requests face significant political obstacles, with many regional leaders reluctant to invite foreign forces back into their territories.

For now, the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel appears poised to continue its expansion. Sources within the group tell CNN that they view the capture of Gao and Niamey as stepping stones toward larger objectives, including potentially targeting coastal capitals like Abidjan and Accra.

"This isn't about local grievances anymore," said the regional security analyst. "This is about creating a transnational jihadist empire in West Africa. And they're succeeding faster than anyone predicted."

As night falls across the region, the implications of the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel's rapid expansion continue to reverberate. With each territorial gain, the group acquires more weapons, more resources, and more legitimacy in the eyes of potential recruits. The question now is not whether they will continue to expand — but how far they can go before the international community responds.
