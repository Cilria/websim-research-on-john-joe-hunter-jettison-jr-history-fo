u/WeldingHistorian

Found his employment records in the Michigan Labor Archives! He worked at Detroit Steel from 1938-1952. There's a fascinating article about him in a 1949 company newsletter - apparently he was known for being able to do perfect overhead welds! 🔥

The newsletter mentions him winning a company-wide welding competition in '46. Called him "The Human Welding Machine" - what a nickname! 😄

u/WyomingPioneer

Got some cool stuff from the Wyoming State Archives! After retiring, he bought a small ranch outside Sheridan. Local paper interviewed him in '75 - said he chose Wyoming because "the mountains reminded him of what freedom looked like." 🏔️

Found his name in several Sheridan Rodeo programs - apparently he never missed one from 1952-1976! The old-timers called him "Kentucky" even though he'd been gone from there for decades! 🤠

Found some fascinating stuff about his ancestors! His grandfather James William Taylor (1832-1901) came from Ireland during the potato famine. Found his immigration records at the Irish National Archives. He landed in New Orleans in 1848! 📚

Even better - found this article from 1896 where Benjamin Monroe Taylor was interviewed about the early railroad days. He mentioned how young Alec used to hang around the rail yard after school, fascinated by the welding work. Talk about foreshadowing! 🚂

u/FamilyDigger

Fun fact: All six Taylor brothers became skilled tradesmen! Found their occupations in the 1910 census:

  • William Henry Taylor - Blacksmith
  • James Monroe Taylor - Carpenter
  • Alexander Franklin Taylor - Welder
  • Robert Lee Taylor - Machinist
  • Charles Edward Taylor - Boilermaker
  • Thomas Jefferson Taylor - Electrician

Check out the Kentucky Trade Union Records - they were all union men! 💪
