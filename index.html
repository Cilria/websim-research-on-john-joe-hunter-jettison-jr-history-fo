South Carolina State Senator Arrested in Massive $11 Billion Fraud Scheme

Robert Francis Hollomon and brother LeRoy face charges that could result in 900-year prison sentence

Republican State Senator Robert Francis Hollomon of South Carolina was arrested Friday morning on federal charges alleging he and his brother operated a sophisticated fraud scheme that scammed investors and government entities out of more than $11 billion over the past decade.

The 58-year-old lawmaker, who has served in the state senate since 2012 representing District 44, was taken into custody at his Columbia residence by FBI agents. His brother, LeRoy Hollomon, 61, was simultaneously arrested at his home in Charleston.

Both men face multiple counts of wire fraud, conspiracy to commit wire fraud, money laundering, and obstruction of justice. Federal prosecutors say the charges carry a combined maximum sentence of more than 900 years in prison.

Key Details:
  • $11.3 billion allegedly stolen through fake infrastructure investment funds
  • Scheme operated from 2016 to 2026
  • Over 15,000 victims across 23 states
  • Money allegedly used for luxury real estate, private jets, and political campaigns
The Alleged Scheme

According to the 127-page federal indictment unsealed Friday, the Hollomon brothers allegedly created a network of shell companies and fake investment opportunities, primarily targeting infrastructure development projects in rural South Carolina and neighboring states.

Prosecutors say the brothers promised investors guaranteed returns of 15-25% on projects including highway construction, water treatment facilities, and renewable energy installations that either didn't exist or were grossly misrepresented.

"This was not some amateur operation," said U.S. Attorney Melissa Rodriguez at a press conference Friday afternoon. "The Hollomons allegedly created elaborate fake documentation, hired actors to pose as local officials, and even staged fake construction sites to convince investors their money was being put to legitimate use."

"They weren't just stealing money – they were stealing trust from thousands of hardworking people who believed they were investing in their communities."
— U.S. Attorney Melissa Rodriguez

The indictment alleges that Senator Hollomon used his position and influence to lend credibility to the scheme, frequently mentioning his role in state government and showing potential investors official-looking documents bearing state seals and letterheads.

A Family Affair

Robert Francis Hollomon, known in political circles as "Frank," rose through South Carolina politics after serving in the U.S. Marine Corps and working as a real estate developer. He was first elected to the state senate in 2012 and had been considered a potential candidate for governor in 2028.

LeRoy Hollomon operated several businesses in Charleston, including a construction company and a consulting firm that prosecutors say were used to funnel money from the fraudulent investment scheme.

"The brothers allegedly used their family connection to create an air of legitimacy," said FBI Special Agent in Charge Thomas Chen. "Victims believed they were investing with a state senator and his successful businessman brother – a powerful combination that proved devastatingly effective."

The Fallout

The alleged fraud has sent shockwaves through South Carolina's political establishment. Governor Henry McMaster called the charges "deeply disturbing" and urged for a full investigation into whether any other state officials were involved.

Senate Majority Leader Shane Massey announced that Hollomon has been stripped of all committee assignments and called for his immediate resignation. "If these allegations are true, Senator Hollomon has betrayed the trust of every South Carolinian," Massey said in a statement.

The South Carolina Democratic Party has called for an emergency session to consider expulsion proceedings. "This level of alleged corruption demands immediate action," said Democratic Party Chairwoman Christale Spain.

The Victims

Among the alleged victims are hundreds of retirees who lost their life savings, small businesses that invested in what they believed were legitimate development projects, and even several pension funds that invested hundreds of millions of dollars.

Martha Jenkins, 72, of Orangeburg, South Carolina, says she and her husband invested $250,000 – their entire retirement savings – in what they believed was a guaranteed infrastructure bond. "They showed us pictures of construction sites, introduced us to supposed project managers, even gave us official-looking documents with state seals," Jenkins said through tears. "Now we have nothing."

Prosecutors say the Hollomons allegedly spent the money on a $45 million private jet, luxury properties in Aspen, Colorado, and Kiawah Island, South Carolina, a fleet of exotic cars, and more than $2 million in political contributions to various campaigns, including Hollomon's own senate races.

What's Next

Both brothers appeared before U.S. Magistrate Judge Patricia Williams Friday afternoon. They were denied bond and remain in federal custody, with prosecutors arguing they pose a significant flight risk given the severity of the charges and their access to offshore accounts.

If convicted on all counts, each brother faces a maximum sentence of 945 years in federal prison. The case has been assigned to U.S. District Judge Richard Gergel, with trial set to begin in January 2027.

The FBI has established a hotline for potential victims and is urging anyone who invested with the Hollomons to come forward. "This investigation is far from over," said Special Agent Chen. "We believe there may be additional victims and potentially additional co-conspirators."

As for the South Carolina Senate, a special election will be held to fill Hollomon's seat. The timing could prove politically significant, as the legislature is set to begin its 2027 session in January – the same month the Hollomon brothers are scheduled to stand trial.
