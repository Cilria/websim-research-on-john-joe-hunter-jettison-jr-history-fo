Al-Harbi Declares 'Total War' and Announces Military Build-up Against Yemen

Arab State leader vows to wage "all-out war" and targets multiple ethnic groups in latest speech
By Jeremy Bowen, BBC Middle East Editor

Riyadh — Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, the leader of the Arab State, has used his second official speech since seizing power to declare a massive military build-up and call for "total war" against neighbouring Yemen and other groups he described as "parasites."

Addressing a crowd of nearly one million supporters in Riyadh, Mr al-Harbi dismissed international criticism of his policies and vowed to ignore human rights conventions in pursuit of what he termed "perfection for the superior Arab race."

"Many in the foreign media claim that I hate human rights and international law, but I tell you that this is not true. I strongly dislike those so-called 'rules'!" Mr al-Harbi proclaimed to thunderous applause from the crowd.

He then listed his enemies as "Indians, Yemenis, Jews, Persians, and worst of all, the most disgusting and vile of these parasites, the 'Palestinians.' I spit on this despicable human filth!"

The speech, which lasted approximately 45 minutes, ended with the crowd chanting "We want war!" in response to Mr al-Harbi's call for an all-out conflict.

"It is time to wage an all-out war, because the only way to save the Arabs is through war!"

According to sources within the Arab State government, Mr al-Harbi has already begun a massive expansion of the military and has privately informed senior officials that military operations against Yemen will commence within two weeks.

"Sympathy is weakness and cowardice; it is a trait of the weak and the cowardly. No! We Arabs are a race of war, blood, and vengeance!" he declared, prompting renewed chants from the crowd.

The announcement marks a significant escalation in regional tensions, with neighbouring countries expressing alarm at the Arab State's increasingly aggressive posture. The United Nations has called for restraint, while Western diplomats have privately expressed concern about the potential for a wider conflict.

Mr al-Harbi's reference to multiple ethnic and religious groups as "parasites" has drawn particular condemnation from human rights organisations, who warn that such rhetoric could lead to ethnic cleansing.

The Arab State, formed just weeks ago after Mr al-Harbi's forces seized control of Saudi Arabia, has rapidly transformed from a protest movement into a militarised state with expansionist ambitions.

Regional analysts suggest that the timing of the military build-up may be intended to capitalise on what Mr al-Harbi perceives as international weakness and distraction with other global conflicts.

As the crowd dispersed following the speech, many supporters were seen waving newly-designed Arab State flags and chanting slogans calling for military action against Yemen and other neighbouring states.

The international community now faces the challenge of responding to what appears to be an imminent military conflict in one of the world's most strategically important regions.
