Iran-Iraq meme row: Persian social media users spark Arab anger with border joke

Arab ultranationalists and religious leaders condemn "Persian expansionism" after satirical posts about reunification

A light-hearted social media trend among Persian users suggesting Iraq should become part of Iran has sparked fierce condemnation across the Arab world, with religious leaders and politicians weighing in on what many are calling a new low in regional relations.

The controversy began when Persian social media users started sharing memes and posts suggesting that Iraq would benefit from being part of Iran, with arguments ranging from "better food" to "more aesthetically pleasing borders."

"Imagine having ghormeh sabzi instead of whatever they call food in Baghdad," read one popular post that garnered over 200,000 likes on Instagram. "Plus, our borders would finally look normal - no more of that weird squiggly line."

What started as tongue-in-cheek commentary quickly escalated into a diplomatic incident, with Arab ultranationalists responding with furious posts of their own, accusing Iran of "neo-imperialist ambitions" and "cultural colonialism."

Religious condemnation

The situation intensified when prominent Iraqi cleric Sheikh Ahmed al-Rashidi delivered a fiery sermon at Baghdad's largest mosque, calling Persians "traitors to the Islamic world and Allah."

"These Persian heretics who mock the land of the two rivers, the cradle of civilization, they forget that Baghdad was the center of the Islamic world when Tehran was nothing but a village," Sheikh al-Rashidi declared to thousands of worshippers. "They speak of food while they eat the bread of Satan!"

The sermon, which was broadcast live on multiple Arab satellite channels, has been viewed over 5 million times on YouTube and sparked protests outside the Iranian embassy in Baghdad.

Social media battleground

The hashtag #PersianExpansionism began trending across Arab countries, with users posting historical maps showing the ancient Persian Empire alongside captions warning of "Persian ambitions."

In response, Persian users doubled down with the hashtag #BetterFoodBetterBorders, sharing photos of Iranian cuisine alongside satellite images of Iran's more geometric borders compared to Iraq's irregular shape.

"It's just a joke, habibi," read one Persian response to an angry Arab user. "Why so serious? We just want to share our koobideh with you. Is that so wrong?"

The online spat has revealed deep-seated tensions that persist nearly two decades after the US-led invasion of Iraq, with many Iraqis expressing resentment over perceived Iranian influence in their country's politics.

Political fallout

The Iraqi government has called for calm, with Foreign Minister Layla al-Maliki issuing a statement urging citizens to "rise above social media provocations" and reminding both nations of their "shared Islamic heritage."

In Tehran, government spokesman Mohammad Javad Zarif attempted to distance the government from the controversy, telling state media that "social media trends do not reflect official policy."

"The Islamic Republic of Iran respects the sovereignty and territorial integrity of all nations in the region," Zarif said at a press conference. "These juvenile online discussions should not be taken as serious political commentary."

However, that has done little to quell the anger among Arab nationalists, who see the memes as evidence of Iranian arrogance and expansionist thinking.

Historical context

The controversy touches on centuries of rivalry between Persian and Arab civilizations, dating back to the Islamic conquest of Persia in the 7th century and continuing through various conflicts over territory and influence.

Dr. Fatima al-Hassan, professor of Middle Eastern studies at the University of Cairo, told the BBC that such social media conflicts often mask deeper historical grievances.

"What appears as banter about food and borders actually taps into very real historical traumas and contemporary political tensions," Dr. al-Hassan explained. "The idea of Iran absorbing Iraq, even as a joke, triggers memories of ancient Persian empires and more recent concerns about Iranian influence."

Similar social media conflicts have erupted periodically across the region, including a 2023 controversy when Saudi users joked about "adopting" Qatar as a province, and a 2025 trend where Egyptian users suggested Libya should become part of Egypt for "better beaches and food."
