u/GrandRapidsHistory

Local historian here! Hill was notorious in Grand Rapids business circles - people were legitimately terrified of getting on his bad side! 😅

Found this great story in the Grand Rapids Public Library archives about how during a business meeting in 1875, some poor guy suggested Hill-Stephens Iron was overcharging. Hill apparently stood up (he was like 6'2"), slammed his fist on the table, and growled "I didn't climb Little Round Top to be lectured about fair prices by the likes of you!" The room went dead silent and the guy practically ran out! 🏃‍♂️💨

His mansion on Cherry Street was INSANE. Found the blueprints - 32 rooms! It's still standing today, btw.

u/MichiganGenealogist

Did a deep dive into the Hill family records at the Michigan Genealogical Records - this family tree is WILD! 🌳

Joseph and Susan had 11 children between 1861 and 1884. Their oldest, Daniel Norval Hill (named after Joseph's father and Colonel Welch!), became a steel magnate himself. The youngest, Theodore Augustus Hill, was born when Susan was 42! 😮

Fun fact: By 1900, the Hill family basically owned half of Grand Rapids' industry. The Hill-Stephens company got split between the eldest sons: Daniel Norval Hill and James Burnside Hill.

u/ArchivalResearcher

Found Susan Hill's diary in the Bentley Historical Library - she writes about how Joseph would drill their kids like soldiers! 😅

Every morning at 6 AM sharp, all 11 kids had to line up for inspection - beds made, clothes pressed, shoes shined. One entry reads: "Joseph expects military precision from children who can barely walk. Little Theodore tried to salute today and fell over." 👶

Even more interesting - he made all his kids learn a trade before they could join the family business. "No son of mine will be helpless if fortune turns," he apparently said. Smart man! 🧠

By 1890, the Hills were Michigan's version of the Rockefellers! 🤑
