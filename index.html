TIL my great-great grandfather Peter Filipović lived from 1876-1967 and the immigration story is absolutely wild

Just got off the phone with my 92-year-old great-uncle Donald (yes, he's still alive) and my brain is absolutely melting over this family timeline.

So Peter Filipović was born Petar Filipović in Duga Resa, Croatia (then Austria-Hungary) in 1876. His dad Robert was a churchwarden, which back then was like... the most respected position in town besides the priest. The dude literally managed church property and was the go-between for the church and the government. His mom Dragana Jurić came from a family that had been in that region for literally centuries.

Here's where it gets fucked up - Peter was one of 10 kids, but only 5 made it past 19. Diphtheria, typhus, just... everything that could kill you in the 1880s did. My great-uncle Donald says his dad (Peter's son) used to talk about how his parents would light candles for the siblings who died every single Sunday at mass.

Then on New Year's Day 1900 - not like January 1st 1900, but like... the actual celebration of the new century - Peter and his brother Viktor just... left. Walked from Duga Resa to Zadar (like 100km), got on a ship, and sailed to America. No papers, no nothing. Just showed up at Ellis Island and said "we're here."

Ended up in Trenton, NJ of all places, living with Viktor for 2 years. Can you fucking imagine? From a tiny Croatian town to fucking Trenton. Then in 1910 he just... left his brother and moved to Columbus, Indiana because he heard there was work in the cement industry. Like, what?

Met my great-great grandmother Sally Ann McGreagor in 1915 - she was Irish-American, her family had been in Indiana since like 1840. They had 8 kids over 24 years. EIGHT. From 1916 to 1940. My great-uncle Donald was the last one, born in 1940 when Peter was 64 fucking years old.

The cement thing is weird too - family story is he worked "in supply" but nobody knows exactly what that means. Could have been anything from managing materials to literally just... carrying cement bags. The man lived through TWO world wars, the Great Depression, watched the Balkans explode multiple times, and died the same year as the Six-Day War.

The span of history this dude lived through is absolutely insane. Born in the Austro-Hungarian Empire, died in the middle of the Cold War. Like... what the actual fuck.

The "supply man" thing in cement is actually super specific. I found a 1912 Columbus Indiana directory that lists "P. Filipovich" (note the spelling change) as "materials expediter" for the Marquette Cement Manufacturing Company.

Basically, he would have been responsible for coordinating the delivery of limestone, clay, coal, and other raw materials to the plant. This was like... logistics before logistics was a thing. He'd negotiate with quarries, arrange rail transport, make sure the plant never ran out of materials. It was actually a pretty skilled position - you needed to know both the business side and the technical side.

The pay was solid middle-class money too. 1915 dollars, he was probably making $2-3/day which was like... $75-80/week. That was enough to support a family of 10 in Indiana back then.
