Just discovered my great-uncle's family history is absolutely wild - only child born in 1895, father was one of TEN brothers from small town Kentucky

So I've been digging into my great-uncle Robert "Bob" David North Jr.'s life story and holy shit, the amount of family drama and movement is insane.

Bob was born July 10th, 1895 in Topeka, Kansas as the ONLY child of Robert Sr and Jean Schatz. But here's where it gets interesting - his dad was one of TEN brothers from Crofton, Kentucky. TEN! In a small town like that in the 1860s-70s, the North family basically WAS the town.

Robert Sr met Jean in Kansas while living in Fredricktown, Missouri. They only stayed in Topeka for 2 years before moving to Gladstone, Kansas, then BACK to Kentucky to Madisonville. The whole family - all those uncles - worked at the same brick company. Can you imagine working with NINE of your brothers every day?

Bob's mom died when he was just 17 in 1912. His dad was widowed for 3 years, then at age 50-something, married a 22-year-old named Sarah. His dad ended up moving to Fort Wayne, Indiana to work as a security guard later in life.

Bob studied chemical engineering, served in the KY National Guard 1913-1915, graduated college at 26, got hired in Fort Wayne, married at 30 to Rachel Flanders, had 3 kids, moved to Rockford IL (hated it), then Dayton OH where he worked as a chemical engineer until retiring in 1970. Died in 1992 at age 97.

The amount of family movement and the age gaps are absolutely fascinating to me.

u/MilitaryRecordsSearch Military Historian • 4 hours ago

Found Bob's National Guard records! He enlisted September 12, 1913 in Company K, 3rd Kentucky Infantry Regiment. Discharged September 11, 1915 as a Private.

The interesting thing is that the 3rd Kentucky was federalized in 1916 for the Mexican Border Crisis, but Bob was already out by then. He literally missed serving in WWI by less than two years - the Kentucky National Guard units were among the first deployed when the US entered the war in 1917.

He served during a relatively peaceful period, which probably allowed him to focus on his engineering studies after discharge.
