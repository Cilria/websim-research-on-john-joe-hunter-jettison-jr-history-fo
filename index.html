[Howard "Ho" Franklin Horne Sr.] My grandfather (1916-1992) - grocery store manager, one of 14 kids, drafted but never served, lived the most ordinary life imaginable
Posted by u/FloridaFamilyHistory • 6 hours ago
Howard Franklin Horne Sr. (July 10, 1916 - July 22, 1992). Born in Windsor, Virginia to Robert Mack Horne Jr. and Eliza Ann Monroe - one of 14 siblings. Drafted during WW2 but never served. Moved to Tampa after the war, managed a grocery store his whole life, had 5 kids, died of a heart attack at 76. That's it. That's literally his whole story. No military service, no interesting jobs, no travel, no nothing. Just an ordinary guy living an ordinary life in an ordinary town. Even his nickname "Ho" was just short for Horne - nothing clever. His brothers all had interesting military stories, but Howard? Just... groceries. For 40 years. Same store. Same routine. Every day. I'm starting to think this might be the most boring genealogy research I've ever done. Has anyone else found relatives this spectacularly uninteresting?

His brothers who served: - Robert III: D-Day, Omaha Beach - William: Battle of the Bulge, Purple Heart - James: Pacific Theater, Guadalcanal - Charles: North Africa, wounded at Kasserine Pass - Thomas: B-17 pilot, 25 missions over Germany - George: Navy, Battle of Midway - Even his younger brother Raymond served as a clerk in Hawaii Howard was literally the only Horne brother who didn't serve. Family letters show he felt guilty about it - wrote to his brother William in 1944: "I feel like I'm not doing my part while you boys are over there getting shot at."
u/TampaHistoryBuff • 4 hours ago • 356 points
Found his grocery store career - it was more interesting than you'd think. Howard started at Piggly Wiggly #47 on Franklin Street in Tampa in 1946 as a stock boy. By 1948 he was assistant manager, manager by 1950. Worked there 42 years through multiple ownership changes: 1946-1955: Piggly Wiggly 1955-1962: Red Food Stores (bought the location) 1962-1978: Food Fair (another buyout) 1978-1988: Kash n' Karry (final buyout) The store had 47 employees when he retired. His personnel file shows zero sick days from 1950-1988 - 38 years without calling in sick once. Customers called him "Mr. Ho" and he knew most regulars by name. Store did $2.3 million in sales his final year (1988), which was actually pretty good for a neighborhood grocery then. He retired when Kash n' Karry wanted to convert it to a 24-hour operation and he refused to work nights. "I've opened this store at 7 AM for 38 years, I'm not staying until midnight."

Found the Horne family in Windsor - they were EVERYWHERE. Howard's childhood was actually pretty rough: - Age 11: mother died (1927) - Age 23: father died (1939) - Worked on family farm until drafted at 27 - Never finished high school (left after 10th grade to work) Maybe his "boring" life was by design - after growing up in chaos, maybe he wanted predictability. His marriage in 1945 was to Dorothy Mae Smith, a schoolteacher. They met at church (both sang in the choir). She was 28, he was 29 - late marriage for that era.

Dorothy Mae Smith was actually more interesting than Howard. Born 1917 in Smithfield, VA (same county as Windsor). Graduated from Farmville Teacher's College (now Longwood University) in 1938. Taught 4th grade for 47 years at Ballast Point Elementary in Tampa. She was the first in her family to graduate college. Her starting salary was $85/month in 1938, final salary was $28,000/year in 1985. She taught over 1,200 kids in her career. Howard and Dorothy bought their house at 3417 Bay Avenue in Tampa in 1948 for $8,500. They lived there 44 years until Howard's death. Same house, same neighborhood, same everything. Their kids said they never traveled farther than Jacksonville (2 hours north) for vacation. Dorothy died in 2004 at 87. She never remarried, lived in the same house another 12 years after Howard died. Their kids sold it for $185,000 in 2004 - quite the return on $8,500.

His death was actually kind of poetic. Died July 22, 1992 - 12 days after his 76th birthday. Death certificate shows "acute myocardial infarction" while at work. He literally died at the grocery store. Collapsed behind the customer service counter at 2:15 PM on a Wednesday. The store was busy, lots of regulars saw it happen. Paramedics arrived in 4 minutes but he was gone. His obituary notes he was "helping a customer find canned peaches" when he collapsed. The customer, Mrs. Betty Wilson (age 72), later said: "He was showing me where they moved the peaches and just... stopped talking mid-sentence. Fell right there by the produce section. He died where he lived - in that grocery store." The store closed for his funeral. First time it had closed for anything other than Christmas Day since 1955. Over 300 people attended his funeral - turns out boring can still mean beloved.
