Great Grocery War Ends: Kroger and Costco Step Back From Nuclear Option as Swiss and Korean Mediators Broker Historic "Treaty of The Deal"

Territorial concessions mark end of 72-hour conflict that saw European supermarkets invade North America
By CNN Staff
Updated 11:47 PM ET, July 23, 2026

New York (CNN) — The Great Grocery War that erupted early Monday has officially ended with the signing of the historic "Treaty of The Deal," brokered by Swiss supermarket giant Migros and South Korean retail powerhouse Emart, sources confirmed to CNN late Thursday night.

The dramatic 11th-hour intervention came just minutes before Kroger and Costco were set to deploy what industry insiders called their "nuclear option" — a coordinated price-slashing campaign that would have driven grocery prices below 1990s levels across the continent.

"We were literally seconds away from mutually assured destruction of the entire grocery sector," said retail analyst Sarah Chen of Columbia Business School. "The Swiss and Korean intervention saved us from a complete market meltdown."

Under the terms of the treaty, signed at a neutral Wegmans location in upstate New York, all hostile supermarket operations will cease immediately. European chains will retain control of their beachheads along the Eastern Seaboard, while American supermarkets maintain their heartland strongholds.

Key territorial concessions include:

• Aldi and Lidl will maintain operations in Virginia, Maryland, and Delaware, but must cease expansion beyond current locations
• Carrefour gains permanent rights to operate in select New England markets
• American chains retain exclusive rights to the Midwest and Deep South
• A 50-mile "demilitarized zone" of independent grocers will separate European and American territories
• All imported "American-style" products must be clearly labeled with country of origin

"This is not a perfect deal, but it is a deal that will prevent further bloodshed," said Migros CEO Fabrice Zumbrunnen, who mediated alongside Emart executive Lee Min-ho. "Both sides have made painful concessions, but peace has prevailed."

The treaty also establishes the "International Grocery Oversight Commission," a permanent body that will monitor compliance and mediate future disputes. The commission's headquarters will be located in neutral Switzerland, at a former Nestlé facility.

Kroger CEO Rodney McMullen, appearing exhausted but relieved at the signing ceremony, addressed reporters: "We came very close to the abyss. When we saw the European supermarkets selling German-made 'American-style bacon' for $1.99, we knew we had to respond with overwhelming force. Thankfully, cooler heads prevailed."

Costco's leadership, which had reportedly prepared emergency measures including bulk ammunition sales and survival food kits, expressed cautious optimism. "Our members can sleep soundly knowing their $1.50 hot dog combo is safe," said Costco CFO Richard Galanti.

The conflict, which began with European supermarket landings on American shores Monday morning, saw unprecedented cooperation between traditional rivals. Walmart, Target, and even Amazon Fresh set aside differences to form "The United Front of Great Shopping" (TUFGS).

European supermarket executives, speaking on condition of anonymity, admitted they underestimated American resolve. "We thought they would be softened by years of high prices," one source said. "Instead, they fought like cornered animals defending their $3.99 rotisserie chickens."

The ceasefire takes effect immediately, though tensions remain high. National Guard units have been deployed to major grocery distribution centers, and the Department of Homeland Security has raised its threat level to "Orange - Elevated" for the retail sector.

President Biden is expected to address the nation Friday morning, calling for national unity and encouraging Americans to "return to their local supermarkets with confidence and pride."

For many Americans, the war's end brings relief mixed with lingering trauma. "I just want to shop in peace," said Karen Mitchell, 45, of suburban Philadelphia. "I don't care if my cereal comes from Battle Creek, Michigan or Birmingham, England. I just want my kids to have their favorite breakfast foods without worrying about artillery fire in the parking lot."

As dawn breaks over a divided but peaceful grocery landscape, the retail sector faces a long road to recovery. Industry experts estimate the conflict cost over $47 billion in lost revenue and infrastructure damage, with some communities still without access to reliable discount shopping.

"This war has changed us all," said one longtime grocery industry veteran, tears in his eyes as he surveyed a half-empty produce section. "We used to fight over coupon policies and loyalty programs. Now we've seen what real war looks like. May we never forget the lessons of these 72 hours of madness."
