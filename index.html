Found their Civil War unit! James and all four of his brothers served in Company K, 36th Mississippi Infantry Regiment. They enlisted together November 1861 (brothers) and March 1862 (James). The 36th Mississippi saw heavy action: - Shiloh (April 1862) - Nathanial was wounded here - Siege of Corinth (May 1862) - Vicksburg Campaign (1863) - where David died - Atlanta Campaign (1864) - where James was shot James' service record shows he was hospitalized in Griffin, GA after being shot through the shoulder July 28, 1864 during the Battle of Atlanta. He was discharged December 1864 when his enlistment expired, then reenlisted January 1865 as the regiment was reorganized. The 36th Mississippi surrendered at Meridian, MS in May 1865. Nathanial died at the Battle of Kennesaw Mountain June 27, 1864. David died of disease during the Siege of Atlanta July 1864. James was lucky - he survived being shot and never was captured.

Found James' farm location! He bought 40 acres east of Brooksville in 1866 for $200 - paid with his mustering-out pay from the Army. The land was in Section 14, Township 16 North, Range 16 East, about 3 miles northeast of town. The soil was terrible - heavy clay with poor drainage. Census agricultural schedules show his yields: - 1870: 150 bushels corn on 20 acres (7.5 bu/acre - very poor) - 1880: 200 bushels corn on 25 acres (8 bu/acre - still poor) - 1900: 300 bushels corn on 30 acres (10 bu/acre - slightly better) He supplemented income by cutting timber and making charcoal. The 1880 census shows him with $100 personal property but no real estate value - the land was worthless for resale. He raised hogs (10-15 annually) and sold them in Brooksville. How he survived 60+ years on that land: he learned to work with what he had. Clay soil holds moisture, so he focused on drought-resistant crops and rotated fields. Plus, having 10 kids meant free labor.

Found James' pension file details! He applied for a Confederate pension in 1902 under Mississippi's 1896 pension act. His application shows: - Disability: "shot through left shoulder at Atlanta, shoulder bone broken, cannot raise arm above head" - Income: "I have no income except what I make off my farm, about $100 per year" - Property: "I own 40 acres of poor land worth about $2 per acre" - Family: "I have a wife and 8 children at home" (2 had died by then) He received $4 per month starting 1902, increased to $8 per month in 1912. The pension examiner noted: "Applicant is extremely poor, lives in a log house, has been a hard-working farmer but land is worthless." Medical examination found: "Gunshot wound through left shoulder, partial paralysis of arm, chronic rheumatism from exposure during war." The shoulder injury was permanent - he could never fully lift his left arm.

Found the story of Susan! Susan Margaret Johnson was born 1902 in Choctaw County, MS. She was indeed 22 when she married 81-year-old James on March 15, 1924. Here's the context: Susan was orphaned at 16 during the 1918 flu pandemic. She worked as a domestic servant in Brooksville. James' wife Eliza had died in 1918, and he was living alone on the farm (his children had mostly moved away). Susan helped with housework and cooking. The marriage was practical, not romantic. James needed someone to help him in his final years - he was frail and nearly blind. Susan needed security - a place to live and someone to provide for her. They had a marriage contract: she would care for him until death, and in return, she would inherit the farm. Local newspapers called it "The Wedding of the Century" - an 81-year-old Civil War veteran marrying a 22-year-old woman. But neighbors said Susan was devoted to James, caring for him faithfully. When he died in 1929, she inherited the 40 acres (worthless land, but it was something). She lived there until 1935, then sold it for $50 and moved to Arkansas.

Found James' literacy status! The 1900 census specifically marks him as "Cannot read" and "Cannot write" - he signed his pension application with an X. But his children were different - all 10 could read and write by 1900. His obituary in the Brooksville newspaper (July 25, 1929) noted: "Mr. Crowder was one of the oldest residents of this section, having lived here over 60 years. He was a Confederate veteran and a member of the Methodist Church. Despite his illiteracy, he was known as a man of good judgment and integrity." The 1920 census is interesting - it shows James (age 76) living alone, but his son Jefferson Carter Crowder's family lived on the adjacent farm. By 1920, 6 of his 10 children were still living in the area. The children had mostly become tenant farmers or worked in the timber industry.

Found James' death certificate! He died July 20, 1929 at age 86 (not 87 as the gravestone says). Cause of death: "Senility and general debility - old age." He had been bedridden for 3 months before death. The informant was Jefferson Carter Crowder (his son). The certificate notes he was buried in "Crowder Cemetery" - this was a family cemetery on the farm. I found the cemetery - it's about 1/4 mile from the house site, with 12 graves total: James, Eliza, 4 of their children who died young, and 7 other Crowder relatives. The farm was sold out of the family in 1935 when Susan left. Today it's just woods - the house collapsed decades ago. But you can still find the cemetery if you know where to look. James' simple gravestone reads: "James M. Crowder - Pvt Co K 36 Miss Inf - April 10 1843 - July 20 1929."
