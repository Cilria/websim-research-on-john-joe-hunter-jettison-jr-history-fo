Self-proclaimed King of Galicia leads bizarre military operation to seize control of Spanish town

By CNN International Staff
Updated 11:47 AM ET, Mon July 20, 2026

Tui, Spain (CNN) — In what Spanish authorities are calling one of the most unusual territorial disputes in modern European history, a man claiming to be the rightful King of Galicia has seized control of the northwestern Spanish town of Tui, along with strategic portions of local infrastructure including a sports field, highway segments, and a discount supermarket.

The self-proclaimed monarch, identified by authorities as 47-year-old Santiago Vázquez, a former insurance salesman from Vigo, declared independence for what he calls the "Kingdom of Galicia" in the early hours of Monday morning. His forces have captured approximately 50 meters of the PO-340 highway and a section of the A-55 motorway leading to a Lidl supermarket.

"At approximately 0600 hours, individuals identifying themselves as the 'Royal Galician Army' took control of the municipal sports complex and declared sovereignty over the entire region. They advanced approximately 50 meters to secure the highway intersection."
— Spanish Civil Guard statement

According to witness reports, Vázquez arrived at the town square at 8:30 AM local time wearing what appeared to be a homemade military uniform complete with epaulettes made from gold craft paper. He was accompanied by approximately 12 individuals, including his 72-year-old uncle and several neighbors from his apartment building.

The Strategic Offensive

The operation, which Spanish media has dubbed "The 50-Meter Offensive," began when Vázquez's forces established a checkpoint at the entrance to the Polideportivo Municipal de Tui, the local sports facility. From there, they advanced north to capture a critical 50-meter stretch of the PO-340 highway.

"They had walkie-talkies and everything," said María Rodríguez, a local shopkeeper who witnessed the events. "One of them was carrying what looked like a broom handle painted silver. They seemed very serious about the whole thing."

By 10:00 AM, the self-declared royal forces had extended their control to include a section of the A-55 motorway and had set up what they called "customs checkpoints" outside the local Lidl supermarket, which they claimed as "strategic supply infrastructure for the kingdom."

Police Response

Spanish National Police and Civil Guard units responded swiftly, advancing from their positions north of the A-55. In a carefully coordinated counter-operation, law enforcement managed to regain control of approximately 5 meters of the disputed highway section.

"We have established a perimeter and are working to resolve this situation peacefully. The individuals involved appear to be armed primarily with household items and strong opinions about medieval succession law."
— Spanish National Police spokesperson

The standoff has created unusual traffic patterns in the area, with the 50-meter section of highway switching hands multiple times throughout the morning. At one point, police controlled the western 5 meters while Vázquez's forces maintained their position on the remaining 45 meters.

Historical Claims

Vázquez claims his right to rule Galicia comes from his descent from an illegitimate son of King Peter I of Portugal, who he insists secretly fathered children in the region in 1367. Historians have dismissed these claims as "creative fiction."

"This gentleman appears to have confused medieval history with fantasy role-playing games," said Dr. Elena Martínez, a medieval history professor at the University of Santiago de Compostela. "His genealogical claims would require him to be approximately 650 years old."

Despite the academic dismissal, Vázquez has produced what he calls "royal decrees" written in crayon on construction paper, declaring the independence of Galicia and appointing his neighbor, 63-year-old retired postal worker Carmen Iglesias, as "Duchess of the A-55 Corridor."

International Reaction

The European Union has called for a peaceful resolution to the crisis, with a spokesperson describing it as "perhaps the most politely conducted territorial dispute in recent memory." NATO officials confirmed they are monitoring the situation but noted that "50 meters of Spanish highway does not currently constitute a strategic threat to alliance security."

The Spanish government has deployed additional police units to the area, though authorities stress they are seeking a peaceful resolution. "We anticipate this will be resolved by dinner time," said Interior Minister Fernando Grande-Marlaska.

Local Impact

Despite the military nature of the operation, daily life in Tui continues with minimal disruption. The local Lidl supermarket remains open, though customers must now pass through what Vázquez's forces call "immigration control," which consists mainly of his cousin checking receipts and declaring purchases "duty-free under the crown."

"They're letting people shop normally, but they make you say 'Viva el Rey' when you leave," said customer Roberto Sánchez, who was buying groceries at 2:00 PM. "The prices are the same, so honestly, it's not that different from regular shopping."

The sports field remains closed to the public, with Vázquez's forces using it as their "military headquarters." Witnesses report seeing individuals practicing what appears to be formation marching with traffic cones and attempting to construct a catapult from PVC pipe.

Negotiation Efforts

Local authorities have established communication with Vázquez, who has presented a list of demands including recognition of his royal status, ownership of the Lidl parking lot, and "a really nice crown, not a cheap one from a costume shop."

Police negotiators have been working throughout the afternoon to resolve the situation peacefully. The standoff has entered its sixth hour, with both sides maintaining their positions on what has become Europe's most comprehensively disputed 50-meter stretch of highway.

As of 3:00 PM local time, Vázquez remains defiant, telling CNN through a megaphone: "The Kingdom of Galicia will never surrender! We will fight for every meter of this highway, even if it's only 50 meters total!"

Authorities expect a peaceful resolution within hours, though they acknowledge this may be the first military operation in European history where the primary objective appears to be controlling access to discount groceries and municipal athletic facilities.
