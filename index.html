Found the name mystery! Jason Alfred Shantz was baptized "Jason Alfred Shantz" at St. John's Lutheran Church, Van Wert County, Ohio on March 15, 1842. Parents: Thomas Shantz and Mary Elizabeth Miller. The confusion comes from how he was recorded: - 1850/1860 census: Listed as "Jason" (common nickname for Jason) - 1870 census: Enumerator wrote "J. A. Shantz" - 1880 census: Transcribed as "Alfred J. Shantz" - 1900 census: "Jason A. Shantz" - 1910 census: "Alfred Shantz" He actually went by "Jason" to family and "Alfred" to outsiders. Caroline's obituary calls him "J. Alfred Shantz" - combining both. The literacy thing plays in - he couldn't correct census takers when they wrote it wrong, so "Alfred" stuck in some records while "Jason" remained in family memory.

Thomas remarried Catherine Weber, but Catherine wanted nothing to do with "another woman's children." By 1855, 13-year-old Jason was living with his uncle Robert Shantz (Thomas's brother), but Robert's wife also resented the extra mouth. Enter W. W. Flanders - a childless farmer who needed help and took in Jason as "bound boy" in 1858. W. W. wasn't just random - he was married to Sarah Shantz, Jason's aunt (Thomas's sister). So Jason was living with his aunt and uncle-by-marriage. The 1860 census shows him as "farmhand" but he was really family, just not acknowledged as such after his stepmother rejected him.
u/CivilWarDraftOhio • 15 hours ago • 298 points
Found why no Civil War service! Jason was subject to the draft, but got a medical exemption. Van Wert County draft board records show he reported in 1863 but was rejected for "chronic chest complaint and insufficient stature." The examining doctor's notes: "Age 21, height 5'2", weight 110 lbs, chest measurement 31 inches - below minimum requirements. Subject reports chronic cough, night sweats, weakness." This was probably tuberculosis or severe asthma. Later records show he was always small and sickly. At marriage he was listed as 5'3" and 115 lbs - tiny even by 1872 standards. Caroline's brother joked she married "the runt of the litter" but Caroline defended him as "the hardest worker I know."

Caroline WAS W. W. Flanders' daughter! William Washington Flanders married Sarah Shantz (Jason's aunt) but they couldn't have children. Sarah convinced W. W. to take in her nephew Jason as farm help, but really she was matchmaking. Caroline Ann Flanders was actually W. W.'s niece - daughter of his brother John Flanders. When John died in 1868, W. W. and Sarah took in 14-year-old Caroline. So Jason and Caroline grew up together in the same house from 1868-1872. Family letters show Sarah planned the match: "Jason is steady and good-hearted, Caroline needs a husband who will work hard and treat her well." W. W. gave them 40 acres of bottomland as wedding gift in 1872. The marriage was essentially arranged but turned out genuinely happy - they stayed married 38 years until Jason's death.

Found his death! Jason Alfred Shantz died November 3, 1910 - just before the census enumerator came around. The 1910 census was taken November 7-10, and neighbor Mrs. Katie Miller told the census taker "Alfred Shantz died last week, widow Caroline still lives here." Death certificate: "Apoplexy" (stroke), age 68 years, 7 months. He'd been in declining health for years - the farm had been managed by sons William and Charles since 1905. Caroline cared for him during his final illness. Buried November 5 in Vendocia Cemetery, plot 147. Simple wooden marker that weathered away by 1920. Grave is in the old section, unmarked now. Caroline joined him there in 1923. The cemetery has no record of grave markers for either - typical for poor farming families of that era.

Mrs. Dorothy Miller (born 1902) was interviewed in 1978 about growing up next to the Shantz farm: "Jason Shantz was the smallest man I ever saw, but he could outwork men twice his size. u/CemeteryRestorationOH • 6 hours ago • 42 points
The Vendocia Cemetery Association has been talking about a restoration project for years, but funding is always the issue. There are probably 200+ unmarked graves from 1880-1920, mostly farmers like Jason who couldn't afford stone markers. What's really sad is that some of his children became quite successful - James the schoolteacher, Benjamin the minister, even the ones who moved to Indiana for factory work. Any of them could have afforded a proper headstone for their parents, but by the time they had money (1920s-1930s), the wooden markers were already gone and they probably couldn't even remember exactly where the graves were. The cemetery has burial records but no plot maps from that era. It's a common story across rural Ohio - entire family histories buried in unmarked graves, lost to time.
