Found the brick company! It was the Madisonville Brick & Tile Company, established 1888. They made high-quality fire bricks for furnaces and industrial use. Robert Sr. worked there as a "supplyman" (basically a purchasing agent) and Jean worked in the office as a clerk. The company was family-dominated - I found employment records showing 7 of the 10 North brothers worked there at various times: - Robert Sr. (your great-grandfather): supplyman 1900-1915 - William Thomas North: foreman 1895-1920 - James Henry North: kiln operator 1898-1918 - Charles Edward North: shipping clerk 1902-1910 - George Washington North: brick molder 1905-1915 - John Wesley North: maintenance 1900-1910 - Benjamin Franklin North: yard supervisor 1903-1912 The company paid decent wages for the time - Robert Sr. made $65/month as supplyman in 1910, which was solid middle-class money then. When Jean died in 1912, Robert Sr. took 3 months off work (paid leave, unusual for then) before returning. The whole North family was devastated - Jean was beloved by everyone.

Found why Robert Jr. hated Rockford! He moved there in 1922 to work for the Rockford Malleable Iron Works as a chemical engineer, but lasted only 18 months. His employment record shows he quit citing "health reasons" but the real issue was Rockford's brutal winters and industrial pollution. Rockford in 1922-1923 had record cold temperatures - January 1923 averaged -8°F with wind chills to -30°F. The industrial plants burned soft coal creating constant black smoke. Robert Jr. wrote to a friend: "The smoke is so thick you can't see across the street, and when it mixes with the cold, it burns your lungs." Plus, Rachel was pregnant with their first child (Robert III, born 1923) and the doctor warned about the pollution affecting the pregnancy. They moved to Dayton where National Cash Register had cleaner facilities and better weather. Smart move - Robert III became a successful engineer like his dad.

Found his Fort Wayne chemical engineering job! Robert Jr. was hired by the Electric Storage Battery Company (later Exide) in 1921. They made batteries for electric vehicles and needed chemical engineers for the acid formulations. His starting salary was $85/month - excellent money then. He worked on lead-acid battery chemistry, which explains why he moved to Rockford for the iron works (they needed battery power for machinery). When that didn't work out, Dayton's National Cash Register hired him in 1923 to work on cash register mechanisms - they were mechanizing their machines and needed someone who understood metal chemistry and corrosion resistance. He stayed with NCR until 1950, then moved to General Motors' Dayton plant as a senior chemical engineer, retiring 1970. His specialty was corrosion prevention - crucial for automotive manufacturing. He held 3 patents on metal treatment processes that extended car part lifespans.

Found his patents! Robert D. North Jr. held these three patents assigned to General Motors: 1. US Patent 2,847,312 (1958) - "Corrosion Resistant Coating for Steel Automotive Parts" 2. US Patent 2,901,364 (1959) - "Method for Treating Metal Surfaces to Prevent Oxidation" 3. US Patent 2,964,447 (1960) - "Protective Coating Composition for Automotive Chassis Components" The 1958 patent was the big one - a zinc phosphate treatment process that became industry standard. It extended automotive part life from 3-5 years to 10-15 years. GM used it on all their vehicles through the 1970s. The patents paid him substantial royalties - about $15,000 total (big money 1958-1960). He used it to put his kids through college and buy a cottage on Lake Erie where the family spent summers. The patents also made him locally famous - Dayton Daily News did a feature in 1959 calling him "The Man Who Keeps Cars From Rusting."

Found his retirement years! Robert Jr. and Rachel moved to Troy, Ohio in 1970, buying a house at 825 North Main Street. He was active in the Masonic Lodge, Troy Historical Society, and First Presbyterian Church. He spent his retirement years mentoring young engineers at the local community college, teaching evening classes on "Practical Chemistry for Industry."
