u/MilitaryArchivesResearch • 16 hours ago • 423 points
Found his WW1 service records! Edward Lee Southworth enlisted May 18, 1917 at Fort Oglethorpe, GA. He was assigned to: - 105th Engineer Regiment, 30th Infantry Division - Initially a Private, promoted to Corporal December 1917 - Sergeant April 1918 - Commissioned as 2nd Lieutenant October 1918 - Promoted to 1st Lieutenant January 1919 His unit saw action: - Ypres-Lys offensive (Belgium) August 1918 - St. Mihiel offensive September 1918 - Meuse-Argonne offensive September-November 1918 He was cited for "exceptional leadership under fire" during the crossing of the Scheldt River in Belgium. His nickname "Lieutenant Edward" came from his men - they couldn't pronounce "Southworth" so they called him "Lieutenant Edward" and it stuck for life. Discharged March 1919 at Camp Gordon, GA. His service record notes "excellent officer material, recommended for Regular Army commission" but he declined and went into business.
u/NCAdvertisingHistory • 15 hours ago • 356 points
Tracked his advertising career through North Carolina business directories: 1915-1916: Durham Morning Herald - worked as circulation auditor, checking newspaper delivery routes and subscription numbers. Made $18/week. 1916-1917: Richmond Times-Dispatch - same role, $22/week. 1921: WDAF Radio, Kansas City - one of the first radio advertising salesmen in the Midwest. WDAF was owned by The Kansas City Star. He sold 15-minute sponsored segments to local businesses. Made $50/week plus commission. 1927: WPTF Radio, Raleigh - returned to NC to work for "We Protect The Family" radio station. This was THE major radio station in NC at the time. He became their top advertising salesman, selling time to furniture companies, tobacco firms, and textile mills. 1930s-1940s: Handled advertising for Liggett & Myers Tobacco, R.J. Reynolds, and several furniture manufacturers. Specialized in radio jingles and sponsored programs. The "TV killed my business" comment was accurate - by 1960, most of his clients shifted to television advertising, but he was too old-school for the new medium.
u/CivilAirPatrolArchives • 14 hours ago • 298 points
Found his Civil Air Patrol records! Edward joined NC Wing Civil Air Patrol December 7, 1941 (yes, Pearl Harbor Day). His personnel file shows: 1941: Joined as 1st Lieutenant (they let him keep his Army rank) 1942: Promoted to Captain - commanded Raleigh Composite Squadron 1943: Promoted to Major - appointed Wing Intelligence Officer 1944-1947: Commandant, Broughton High School Cadet Corps His intelligence work was domestic - he coordinated aircraft spotting networks along the NC coast looking for German U-boats. The cadet corps program trained high school students in military discipline, aircraft identification, and basic aviation. Broughton High had 150 cadets - one of the largest programs in the Southeast. His 1947 resignation letter stated: "Business interests have caused me enough time to dedicate the effort that would be needed for the corps. My advertising clients expect my full attention, and I cannot give both the attention they deserve." He remained active in CAP as a senior member, flying missions and attending events. His last flight was October 1981 - he was 89 years old and still flying CAP aircraft!

Found the stonemason story! It's partially true. John William Southworth was indeed a stonemason in Mt. Olive, but not unsuccessful - he built several prominent buildings that still stand: - Mt. Olive Methodist Church (1898) - stone foundation still there - Wayne County Courthouse addition (1903) - he was lead stonemason - Several downtown Mt. Olive commercial buildings The actual quote from family letters: "I am getting the hell out of here and make something of myself" - said to his brother William in 1913, not about stonemasonry but about small-town life. Edward left Mt. Olive in 1913 with $50 in his pocket, riding the train to Durham. He initially worked as a grocery store clerk before getting the newspaper job. His father actually supported his decision - John wrote to relatives: "Edward has too much ambition for this small town. Let him seek his fortune." The family maintained close ties. Edward visited regularly and helped support his parents financially from 1920 onward. His father died in 1920 (kidney disease), mother in 1939 (stroke). Both are buried in Mt. Olive Cemetery.
u/AviationHistoryNC • 11 hours ago • 189 points
His flying career was remarkable! Edward learned to fly in 1928 at age 36 - quite old for a pilot in those days. He trained at Raleigh Municipal Airport (now Raleigh-Durham International). Aircraft he owned/flew: - 1929: Waco 10 biplane - 1935: Stinson SR Reliant - 1946: Cessna 140 - 1955: Cessna 172 - 1965: Cessna 182 (flew until 1981) He logged over 8,000 flight hours - remarkable for a private pilot. Flew extensively for business, visiting advertising clients throughout the Southeast. Also flew Civil Air Patrol search and rescue missions. His last flight was October 15, 1981 - exactly 54 years after his wedding. He flew his Cessna 182 from Raleigh to Wilmington and back "to see the coast one more time." He died exactly one month later. The FAA accident report notes he died of natural causes, not a crash - he collapsed while taxiing after landing. The NC Civil Air Patrol gives an annual "Major Edward L. Southworth Safety Award" in his honor for outstanding flight safety records.
