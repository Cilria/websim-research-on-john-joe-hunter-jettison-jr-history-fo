James Richard Coleman (June 3, 1896 – May 14, 2006) was an American attorney and politician who served as a member of the Virginia Senate for 90 years, from January 1916 until his death in May 2006. A member of the Democratic Party, Coleman holds the record for the longest-serving state legislator in American history, as well as the longest tenure of any elected official in Virginia.

Early life and education

Coleman was born in Farmville, Virginia, on June 3, 1896, to Richard Thomas Coleman and Mary Elizabeth Johnson Coleman. His father was a tobacco farmer and merchant who had served in the Confederate Army during the American Civil War. Coleman attended public schools in Prince Edward County before enrolling at the University of Virginia in 1913, where he earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in 1916 and a Bachelor of Laws degree in 1919.

Military service

Following the United States entry into World War I in April 1917, Coleman enlisted in the United States Army. He served as a second lieutenant in the 116th Infantry Regiment, 29th Division, and was wounded in action during the Meuse-Argonne Offensive in October 1918. Coleman remained in France until March 1919, when he was honorably discharged and returned to Virginia.

Senate career

Early years (1916–1945)

Coleman was first elected to the Virginia Senate on November 2, 1915, at the age of 19, running on a platform of agricultural reform and improved rural infrastructure. He was elected from the 17th District, which at the time included Prince Edward County and surrounding areas. Coleman's youth and military service were key factors in his election, as the district had a large population of World War I veterans.

During his first three decades in office, Coleman focused primarily on agricultural issues, serving as chair of the Senate Agriculture Committee from 1923 to 1945. He authored the Agricultural Credit Act of 1927, which established state-backed loans for farmers, and the Rural Electrification Act of 1935, which expanded electrical service to rural areas of Virginia. Coleman was also instrumental in the establishment of Virginia's first soil conservation districts in the 1930s.

Mid-career (1945–1975)

Following World War II, Coleman shifted his focus to economic development and education. He served as chair of the Senate Finance Committee from 1948 to 1968, during which time Virginia underwent significant modernization. Coleman was the primary architect of Virginia's community college system, established in 1966, and the Virginia Industrial Development Authority, created in 1959.

Coleman was a key figure in Virginia's response to the civil rights movement. While he initially opposed school integration, he later became a moderate voice in the Senate, supporting gradual desegregation and the establishment of the Virginia Human Rights Council in 1966. Coleman worked closely with Governor Mills E. Godwin Jr. to implement the state's first fair housing laws in 1972.

Later years (1975–2006)

After the 1970 census, Coleman's district was renumbered as the 21st District due to redistricting. Despite his advancing age, Coleman remained an active legislator, focusing on issues related to aging and healthcare. He authored the Virginia Elderly and Disabled Waiver Program in 1981, which allowed seniors to receive long-term care services in their homes rather than nursing facilities.

In his final decades in office, Coleman became known as the "Dean of the Senate," serving as president pro tempore from 1984 to 2000. He continued to cast votes until shortly before his death, with his final vote recorded on May 12, 2006, on a bill related to transportation funding.

Personal life

Coleman married Margaret Louise Thompson on June 15, 1920, at Farmville Baptist Church. They had three children: James R. Coleman Jr. (1921–2001), Mary Elizabeth Coleman (1923–2010), and Sarah Margaret Coleman (1925–1999). Margaret Coleman died in 1998 after 78 years of marriage.

Coleman practiced law in Richmond throughout his Senate career, maintaining a small general practice focused on estate planning and agricultural law. He was a member of the Virginia Bar Association for 87 years, from 1919 until his death.

Death and legacy

Coleman died on May 14, 2006, at the age of 109, in Richmond, Virginia. He was interred at Hollywood Cemetery in Richmond. At the time of his death, he was the oldest living former World War I veteran in the United States and the last surviving member of the Virginia Senate who had served prior to 1950.

The Virginia General Assembly established the James R. Coleman Institute for State and Local Government at the University of Virginia in 2007. The institute conducts research on state government operations and provides training for Virginia's public officials. The Virginia State Capitol features a portrait of Coleman in the Senate chamber, and the Senate's main committee room was renamed the James R. Coleman Room in 2006.
