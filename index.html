Ultranationalist Leader Marches on Riyadh With Armed Militia, Demands Royal Family Abdicate

Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi leads 30,000 supporters in what he calls 'peaceful revolution'
By Ben Hubbard and Vivian Yee

RIYADH, Saudi Arabia — Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi, the ultranationalist leader who has built a mass movement demanding the expulsion of foreigners from Saudi Arabia, marched on the capital Monday with more than 30,000 armed supporters, surrounding the royal palace and demanding that the Saudi royal family step aside "peacefully."

The march, which began at dawn in Mr. Al-Harbi's birthplace of Arar and picked up supporters along the 500-mile route, represents the most serious challenge to the Saudi monarchy in decades. By nightfall, his Storm Unit militia had seized control of key areas around Riyadh and was locked in a tense standoff with government security forces.

"The time of talk is over," Mr. Al-Harbi declared to his supporters, who had gathered in a vast crowd outside the palace gates. "The hour of need is here. We must answer the call of Allah and save the Arab nation from those who would destroy our history and wage war against us."

The march began at 6 a.m. in Arar, where Mr. Al-Harbi was born, and moved south through Saudi Arabia's northern provinces. By 7:30 a.m., the column had reached Sakaka, where local authorities allowed the Storm Unit to pass through unimpeded. In Hail, by 8:45 a.m., the crowd had swelled noticeably.

But it was in Buraydah, reached at 9:10 a.m., that the movement's momentum became undeniable. Nearly 40,000 additional supporters joined the march there, according to witnesses, bringing the total to more than 70,000 people.

As the column approached Riyadh by late morning, tensions escalated. Storm Unit members, many wearing the group's distinctive white armbands and carrying weapons openly, clashed with Saudi police officers at a checkpoint 10 kilometers from the city center. Witnesses reported that the Storm Unit fighters quickly overwhelmed the police and seized control of a local police station.

By 1 p.m., Mr. Al-Harbi's forces had formally entered Riyadh and established positions around the royal palace, effectively placing it under siege. Storm Unit members could be seen patrolling the area, some carrying automatic weapons.

"They must leave peacefully, or there will be a great armed rebellion."

In a statement delivered to reporters, Mr. Al-Harbi issued his ultimatum: "The Saudi royal family has brought nothing but shame and humiliation to the Arab people. They must leave peacefully, or there will be a great armed rebellion."

The Saudi government appeared paralyzed by the crisis. Senior officials, speaking on condition of anonymity, said the royal family was deeply divided over how to respond. Some urged a negotiated settlement, while others pushed for military intervention to crush the uprising.

"This is unprecedented in modern Saudi history," said one Western diplomat who has spent decades in the region. "We've seen protests before, but nothing on this scale, with this level of organization and this level of threat to the regime."

Mr. Al-Harbi's movement, which began as a small anti-Indian protest group on Telegram, has grown into a mass nationalist movement with more than 8 million followers across multiple social media platforms. His speeches, which blend religious rhetoric with ultranationalist themes, have struck a chord with many young Saudis frustrated by economic stagnation and what they see as the kingdom's subservience to foreign interests.

As night fell over Riyadh, the city remained in a state of high tension. Storm Unit members controlled key intersections and government buildings, while Saudi security forces maintained positions around the palace. The capital's streets, normally bustling with evening activity, were largely deserted as residents waited to see what would happen next.

"We are witnessing history," said one Riyadh resident who watched the march from his apartment window. "Whether this ends in bloodshed or negotiation, nothing will ever be the same in Saudi Arabia."
