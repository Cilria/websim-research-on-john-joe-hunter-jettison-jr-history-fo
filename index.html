Operation Nahavand: Arab forces breach the Zagros

Slow, grinding advances and a ruthless air campaign are reshaping western Iran

Three weeks into Operation Nahavand the Greater Arab State’s three-pronged invasion has at last cracked the Zagros range. The breakthrough, costly and methodical, has given Arab forces a toehold on the Iranian plateau and opened the path toward Hamedan, a strategic hub linking Tehran to the south-western provinces.

Army Command South

General Khalid Mohammed Bin Hussien—widely regarded as the army’s most gifted field commander—has inched through the foothills south of the range. The prize, Issabad, is now a smear of rubble: after ten days of saturation shelling and air strikes the town no longer exists. Arab State media describe the obliteration as “cleansing the hive”; Iranian exile groups call it a war-crime.

Army Command North & Centre

Under Generals Turki bin Abdullah and Ahmed Fahd Al-Hussien, northern and central thrusts punched out of the mountains on 24 May, overwhelming defenders near Borujerd and racing toward Hamedan. Captured road junctions have allowed logistics teams to push fuel and artillery rounds forward, easing the perennial supply headaches that dog mountain warfare.

The air terror campaign—revealed in a leaked air-force memorandum that urges commanders to “follow what Hitler did in 1940”—has flattened residential blocks in Tehran, Isfahan and Shiraz, killing hundreds of civilians and prompting a fresh exodus of refugees toward Turkey and Armenia.

On 19 May the Arab State formally annexed Khuzestan, installing the leader’s brother, Waleed Muhammad Al-Harbi, as governor-general. Khorramabad has been designated a “Military Administration Zone of Kurdistan” under Storm Unit officer Hussien Muhammad Al-Fahd, while Bushehr is now a “Special Military Zone” run by General Saddam Izzam Al-Rashdi. In each district Storm Unit militias patrol the streets, replacing the police and setting up tribunals that hand out swift sentences to those accused of “Persian sabotage”.

The grinding tempo of the offensive suggests the Greater Arab State is prepared to accept a long campaign. Whether Iran’s fragmented defenders can regroup before the invaders reach the gates of Isfahan will determine the next, and perhaps decisive, phase of the war.
