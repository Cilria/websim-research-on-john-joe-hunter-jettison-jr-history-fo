u/VirginiaGenealogyNerd • 7 hours ago • 302 points
Holy cow, this is a goldmine! I found your stepfather John Alexander Colt's actual background. He wasn't just a "lumber worker" - he was a Civil War orphan. John A. Colt was born March 15, 1845 in Buckingham County, VA. His father Alexander Colt (1810-1862) died at the Battle of Fredericksburg, leaving his mother Mary with 6 kids. The 1860 census shows the family destitute - Mary taking in washing while the older boys worked in sawmills. John enlisted in the Confederate Army February 1864, age 19, Company K, 44th Virginia Infantry. Captured at Sailor's Creek April 6, 1865 (3 days before Lee surrendered), held at Point Lookout until June 1865. The 1890 Veterans Schedule confirms this. After the war, he worked in lumber camps across central Virginia. Married Nancy in 1895 when he was 50 and she was 57 - both widowed, both with grown children. They probably married for companionship more than anything else. He died in 1901 from complications of old war wounds.

Going back further - William Clemons (Alfred's grandfather) purchased his 200 acres in 1832 for $400 cash. The deed shows he bought it from the estate of Thomas Jefferson Randolph (yes, THAT Thomas Jefferson's grandson). This land stayed in the family for 101 years until Robert Lee sold it in 1933. William's father was also William Clemons (1780-1835) who migrated from Pennsylvania to Virginia in 1803. He was a Revolutionary War veteran - served in the Pennsylvania militia 1780-1781. There's a pension file for him under "William Clemons, Pvt, Capt. Robison's Company, PA militia." Mary Elizabeth Wright's family was prominent - her father Thomas Wright was a county magistrate and her mother was descended from the Minor family of Virginia aristocracy. This explains why the Clemons family had some education despite being farmers.

u/TrainAccidentHistory • 4 hours ago • 65 points
Found details on James Thomas Clemons' train accident - it was September 15, 1945. He was working as a brakeman for Norfolk & Western Railway. The accident report shows he was coupling cars when he slipped and fell under the wheels. He left behind a wife and 3 teenage kids. The railroad paid death benefits - $2,500 lump sum plus $15/month for each child until age 18. The family sued and settled for additional $5,000. This was actually a landmark case that improved railroad safety in Virginia.
