Al-Harbi Vows to 'Reunite Arab World' and Target Yemeni 'Traitors' in First Speech as Leader

New Arab State leader promises expansion from Morocco to Iraq and war against Persia and India
By Ben Hubbard and David D. Kirkpatrick

RIYADH — Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi, the ultranationalist leader who seized power in Saudi Arabia this month, used his first formal address as head of the newly declared Arab State to vow the reunification of the Arab world and launch vitriolic attacks against Yemenis and other groups he labeled as "traitors to the Semitic Arab race."

Speaking to nearly one million supporters gathered in Riyadh's main square, Mr. Al-Harbi declared that his movement's ultimate goal would be to create a single Arab state stretching "from Morocco to Iraq" and to conquer new territory to provide "living space" for Arabs.

"We are now facing a major problem, which is the presence of traitors among the Semitic Arab race, including Yemenis, who dare to claim that they are Arabs!" Mr. Al-Harbi proclaimed to thunderous applause. "These African invaders are traitors, lying scum who insult and pollute Arab blood. The only solution for these parasites is simple: extermination."

The speech, delivered just ten days after Mr. Al-Harbi's forces marched on Riyadh and forced the Saudi royal family to abdicate, marked a significant escalation in the rhetoric that has characterized his rapid rise to power. The 35-year-old former social media activist has transformed from an obscure Telegram personality into the absolute ruler of one of the world's most important oil-producing nations.

"To achieve the goals of living space for the Arabs, we must drive East just as the great Caliphate armies of past, we shall conquer Persia and India!"

Mr. Al-Harbi's vision for expansion extends well beyond the Arabian Peninsula. He declared that Arab armies would move eastward to conquer Persia and India, echoing the military campaigns of medieval Islamic caliphates but with a distinctly racial and nationalist character that has alarmed regional analysts.

"Many in so-called foreign media attempt to claim that I only hate the Indians, this is a lie," he said. "I hate all other parasites on our homeland! The Arab State shall be for Arabs only!"

The speech represents a dramatic departure from the cautious diplomacy that characterized Saudi foreign policy for decades. Where previous Saudi leaders maintained alliances with Western powers and sought to balance regional relationships, Mr. Al-Harbi has embraced an explicitly expansionist and exclusionary ideology.

Regional experts note that Mr. Al-Harbi's rhetoric about Yemeni "traitors" is particularly dangerous given Saudi Arabia's long and often contentious relationship with its southern neighbor. Yemen has been embroiled in its own civil conflict for years, and Mr. Al-Harbi's statements could further destabilize the region.

"The reference to Yemenis as 'African invaders' and calls for their extermination represents a chilling escalation," said Dr. Sarah Al-Sarhan, a Middle East specialist at the Council on Foreign Relations. "This is ethnic cleansing rhetoric directed at fellow Arabs."

Mr. Al-Harbi's promise to "reunite the Arab world" under his leadership has raised concerns among neighboring states about potential territorial ambitions. His speech made repeated references to historical Arab territories and the need to restore what he termed "Arab dignity" through territorial expansion.

The international community has responded with alarm to Mr. Al-Harbi's rapid consolidation of power and increasingly extreme rhetoric. Western diplomats privately express concern that his movement's sophisticated use of social media has allowed extremist ideology to spread rapidly across the region.

As Mr. Al-Harbi concluded his speech to thunderous applause from the massive crowd, he declared: "We cannot under any circumstances claim to be a nation of Arab descent until these lying traitors are wiped off the face of the earth!" The statement was met with chants of approval from his supporters, many of whom waved flags of the newly declared Arab State.

The speech marks a pivotal moment in what has been one of the most dramatic political transformations in modern Middle Eastern history. In less than two months, Mr. Al-Harbi has gone from leading online protests to absolute power, and his vision for the region suggests that the most turbulent chapter may still lie ahead.
