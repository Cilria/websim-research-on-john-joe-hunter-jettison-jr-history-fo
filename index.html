SteppeHistorian PhD Central Asian History 3 hours ago

Your friend is completely full of shit, but this is a great question because the Huns are one of history's great mysteries.

Here's what we actually know: The Huns appear in Roman records around 370 CE as this terrifying new force on the steppes. They're described as short, stocky people with flat faces and small eyes - physical descriptions that match Central Asian Turkic peoples.

Most scholars think they originated from the Xiongnu confederation that collapsed in Mongolia around 150 CE. Some Xiongnu groups probably migrated west over 200 years, absorbing other steppe peoples along the way, and emerged as the Huns in Europe.

The big problem? We have no written records from the Huns themselves. Everything we know comes from Roman sources who were shitting their pants at these terrifying horse archers.

LateRomanExpert Late Roman Empire 3 hours ago

The wildest thing about the Huns is how they went from total obscurity to the most feared military force in Europe in like... 20 years.

They show up around 370 CE and immediately start wrecking the Goths, who were themselves pretty badass. The Goths begged the Romans to let them across the Danube because they were so terrified of the Huns.

Then the Huns became this existential threat to the Roman Empire. They defeated Roman armies, extorted massive amounts of gold, and basically turned the Eastern Roman Empire into a tributary state.

But here's the thing - they had no written language, no cities, no permanent settlements. They were pure nomads who lived in tents and moved constantly. How do you fight an enemy who has no fixed territory to defend?

ArchaeologyNerd Archaeologist 2 hours ago

Archaeologist here - the biggest frustration about studying the Huns is that they left almost nothing behind.

We have a few burial sites in Hungary, some gold artifacts, and that's basically it. No cities, no permanent settlements, hardly any burials. They were nomads who didn't build permanent structures, so there's almost nothing for us to dig up.

The only reason we know they existed is because the Romans wrote about them. If the Romans hadn't been so terrified of them, they might have passed through history completely unnoticed.

Compare that to the Mongols who left massive archaeological evidence everywhere they went. The Huns are like ghosts - they appeared, terrified everyone, then vanished.

Your friend's Hunnic warrior blood thing is complete fantasy, but it comes from a real confusion.

The Huns spoke a language that was probably related to Turkic or Mongolic (we're not sure because we have like 3 words of it). The Bulgars who founded Bulgaria also spoke a Turkic language.

From a genetic perspective, the whole "descended from ancient warriors" thing is complete nonsense anyway.

We have DNA from a few Hun burials in Hungary. They were genetically East Asian - totally different from modern Bulgarians, who are genetically Slavic with some Balkan admixture.

Even if some Huns settled in Bulgaria (which we have no evidence for), after 1500 years of mixing with Slavs, Greeks, Thracians, Romans, etc., any "Hunnic" DNA would be completely diluted.

Modern genetics shows Bulgarians are basically South Slavs with some local Balkan ancestry. No evidence of significant Central Asian ancestry that would point to Hun origins.

Great question! After Attila died in 453, his sons fought a civil war that tore the Hunnic Empire apart. The Germanic tribes they'd conquered (Goths, Gepids, etc.) rebelled and defeated the Huns in several battles.

Most Huns probably:

• Got absorbed by other groups - joined the Goths, settled down, and lost their identity

• Went back to the steppes - some probably returned to their original nomadic lifestyle

• Settled in Hungary - small groups probably stayed and became farmers

• Joined other nomadic confederations - later groups like the Avars and Magyars might have included some Hun descendants

Within a generation, they stopped being "Huns" and became whatever group they joined. Their identity as a distinct people just... evaporated.
