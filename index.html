Peru military coup: Anataro Humala seizes power ahead of elections, suspends constitution

Armed forces back Ethnocacerist leader as president and Congress are detained; international community condemns power grab
By Rafael Romo, CNN Senior Latin American Affairs Editor
Jimena de la Quintana, CNN Correspondent in Lima

Lima, Peru (CNN) — The Peruvian military seized control of the government Sunday night, detaining President Dina Boluarte and members of Congress just weeks before scheduled elections, as Anataro Humala, leader of the Ethnocacerist movement, declared himself the new head of state.

Armed soldiers surrounded the Government Palace in Lima shortly after 8 p.m. local time, while military vehicles blocked access to the Legislative Palace. State television and radio stations went off air for several hours, replaced by military announcements declaring the "temporary suspension of constitutional order."

Speaking from the balcony of the Government Palace shortly before midnight, Humala, dressed in military fatigues and flanked by senior army officers, announced that "the corrupt political class has been removed from power" and pledged to "restore dignity to the Peruvian people."

Key developments:
  • President Boluarte reportedly detained at military base outside Lima
  • Congress dissolved, members taken into custody
  • Constitution suspended, elections postponed indefinitely
  • International condemnation from OAS, US, and regional governments

The coup follows weeks of political unrest and growing support for Humala's Ethnocacerist movement, which has gained traction among indigenous communities and military personnel disillusioned with the political establishment. The movement advocates for indigenous rights, anti-imperialism, and what Humala calls "Andean socialism."

Military sources told CNN that the coup was led by General Carlos Rivera, commander of the Peruvian Army, who reportedly coordinated with Humala over the past month. The military high command issued a statement claiming they acted to "prevent the chaos and violence that would have erupted during the electoral process."

The whereabouts of President Boluarte remain unknown. Her last communication was a text message to a family member saying "they're here," according to sources close to the former president. Several members of Congress have been confirmed detained, though exact numbers remain unclear.

Humala, 52, is the younger brother of former presidential candidate Ollanta Humala, who lost the 2011 election. He has spent the past decade building support among indigenous communities, particularly in the southern Andean regions, where he has presented himself as a defender of native Peruvians against what he calls "colonial exploitation."

The Ethnocacerist movement, founded in the 1980s, combines indigenous nationalism with leftist ideology. It takes its name from the "ethnocacerista" rebellion led by indigenous leader Pablo Sexto in the 18th century. Human rights organizations have expressed concerns about the movement's authoritarian tendencies and anti-democratic rhetoric.

Regional leaders were quick to condemn the coup. Chilean President Gabriel Boric called for "the immediate restoration of democratic order," while Colombian President Gustavo Petro demanded "the release of all detained officials." The Organization of American States announced an emergency session for Monday morning.

The United States State Department issued a statement expressing "grave concern" and called for "the immediate release of President Boluarte and all detained officials." Secretary of State Marco Rubio said the US would "review all options" in response to the coup.

International markets reacted sharply to the news. The Peruvian sol fell 8% against the dollar in after-hours trading, while the Lima Stock Exchange announced it would suspend trading Monday morning. Mining companies, which account for 60% of Peru's export earnings, saw their shares plummet in overseas markets.

Despite the late hour, hundreds of Humala supporters gathered in Lima's Plaza San Martín, waving indigenous flags and celebrating what they called "the end of corruption." However, smaller protests against the coup erupted in middle-class neighborhoods, with residents banging pots and pans in a traditional form of protest known as "cacerolazo."

The military has imposed a curfew starting at 10 p.m. local time and has deployed troops to key intersections throughout Lima. Internet and cell phone service remained intermittent as of early Monday morning, though connectivity appeared to be gradually returning.

Peru has experienced significant political instability in recent years, with six presidents since 2016 and multiple impeachments. The country was scheduled to hold general elections on November 8, with polls showing a fragmented electorate and no clear frontrunner among the 18 presidential candidates.

Regional analysts warn that the coup could have far-reaching implications for South American democracy. "This represents a significant setback for democratic governance in the region," said Dr. Cynthia McClintock, professor of political science at George Washington University. "The international community must respond decisively."
