u/TarheelGenealogy NC Roots • 12 hours ago

Just discovered my great-grandfather Edward Lee Southworth Sr. had the most incredible career journey - from Mt. Olive to advertising pioneer

Edward Lee Southworth Sr. (July 20th, 1892 to November 15th, 1981) - born in Mt. Olive, North Carolina to John William Southworth (1858-1920), a stonemason, and Mary Louise Bridge (1862-1939), a homemaker.

Had 8 siblings (5 brothers, 3 sisters). Apparently said "I am not gonna be a damn stonemason" and left Mt. Olive. Started in advertising, worked as Newspaper Auditor in Durham by 1915, then Richmond in 1916.

Enlisted in the US Army in 1917, served until 1919, exited as First Lieutenant - nickname "Lieutenant Edward" for life. Worked as State Auditor in Lexington, KY by 1920, then advertising agent for radio station in Kansas City (1921), moved to LA for 3 years, then Atlanta, back to Raleigh by 1927 as Radio Advertising Agent.

Joined NC Civil Air Patrol in 1933, reached Major, served as high school cadet corps commandant until 1947. Returned to advertising, never really retired, resigned in 1970 saying "Television has caused a large decline in the business that I was needed for." Died in 1981. Wife Betty, 3 children (2 sons, 1 daughter).

Found Edward's World War I service record! He enlisted May 18, 1917 at Fort Caswell, NC. Started as Private, promoted to Corporal September 1917, Sergeant January 1918, then commissioned as 2nd Lieutenant in October 1918, promoted to 1st Lieutenant before discharge in June 1919.

He served with the 105th Ammunition Train, 30th Division - they saw action at the Second Battle of the Marne and the Meuse-Argonne Offensive. His personnel file notes he was "recommended for commission due to exceptional organizational skills and leadership qualities."

The nickname "Lieutenant Edward" stuck because after the war, he insisted people call him that instead of his given name. Found a 1920 letter where he wrote "I've earned the right to be addressed by my rank - it's who I am now."

u/RadioHistoryBuff • 9 hours ago

Found his advertising career details! Edward was actually one of the first radio advertising agents in the South. When he moved to Kansas City in 1921, he worked for WDAF radio - one of the first stations west of the Mississippi.

His big break came in 1924 when he created an advertising campaign for a Los Angeles car dealership on KFI radio. The campaign increased sales by 300% - this was huge news in the fledgling radio industry. The Los Angeles Times mentioned it in a March 1925 article about "the new medium of radio commerce."

By 1927, he was back in Raleigh working for WPTF radio. Found records showing he handled accounts for Liggett & Myers Tobacco Company, Carolina Power & Light, and several furniture manufacturers. He specialized in "agricultural market programming" - basically farm reports sponsored by seed companies.

u/CivilAirPatrolResearch CAP Historian • 8 hours ago

Found Edward's Civil Air Patrol service record! He joined the NC Wing on March 15, 1933 - just weeks after CAP was officially formed. His application shows he was 40 years old, 5'11", 165 lbs, with "extensive experience in radio communications."

He rose quickly through the ranks: Captain by 1935, Major by 1938. As intelligence officer, he developed a system for identifying aircraft that flew over North Carolina - this was crucial during WWII when German U-boats were off the coast. His reports went directly to the Eastern Defense Command.

The high school cadet corps he commanded was at Needham B. Broughton High School in Raleigh. School records show he trained over 1,200 cadets between 1938-1947. The program was so successful that other schools copied it. He resigned in 1947 because his advertising business had grown to require full attention.

u/BankruptcyRecords • 5 hours ago

Found something interesting about Edward's father! John William Southworth filed for bankruptcy in 1918 - the records show he owed $847 to various creditors, mostly for tools and materials for his stonemasonry business. This was during WWI when construction had slowed down.

The bankruptcy was discharged in 1919, but John had to sell most of his tools. He went back to working as a laborer in the quarry rather than as an independent stonemason. Edward was serving in the Army at the time, but family letters show he sent money home to help.

John died in 1920 at age 62 - the death certificate lists cause as "chronic nephritis" (kidney disease), but family stories suggest he was despondent over losing his business and his ability to provide for his family.

Found Edward's pilot records! He got his private pilot license in 1948 at age 56 - certificate number 1234567 (seriously, that was the number). He owned a 1946 Cessna 140 and flew it regularly until he was 78.

His logbooks show he accumulated 2,847 flight hours over 25 years. Most flights were between Raleigh and various North Carolina towns for business, but he also flew to Florida to visit his daughter and to Kentucky to visit his wife's family.

The Civil Air Patrol records show he participated in 14 search-and-rescue missions between 1948-1970. His last flight was November 10, 1981 - just 5 days before he died at age 89.

Found Edward's death certificate! Edward Lee Southworth Sr. died November 15, 1981 at age 89 in Raleigh. Cause of death: "Myocardial infarction due to coronary artery disease" - heart attack.

He died at Rex Hospital after being admitted with chest pains. The informant was his son Edward Jr. His occupation is listed as "retired advertising executive" - even though he technically never retired, just scaled back after 1970.

He's buried in Oakwood Cemetery in Raleigh next to his wife Betty (who died in 1983). The gravestone reads "Edward Lee Southworth Sr. - Lieutenant Edward - 1892-1981 - He lived life on his own terms."

His estate was worth approximately $847,000 - quite substantial for the time, mostly from real estate investments he made with his advertising earnings.
