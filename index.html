'Month of Glory': Arab forces tighten grip on Iran with major sieges and breakthroughs

Isfahan encircled, Shiraz falls and Qom within striking distance as Greater Arab State troops celebrate a string of victories
By Najwa Al-Rashid, FT Middle East Editor

The Greater Arab State's fifth month of Operation Nahavand has delivered what its commanders hail as the campaign's most decisive victories yet, with the siege of Isfahan, the fall of Shiraz and the encirclement of more than 200,000 Iranian troops across multiple pockets.

August — dubbed the "Month of Glory" by Arab forces — has seen the strategic map of central and southern Iran redrawn at breakneck speed. After weeks of grinding attrition, the three-pronged offensive has finally broken through Iranian defensive lines, leaving the Islamic Republic's most populous cities exposed to direct assault.

Southern Front: Isfahan starved into submission

Army Command South, under the veteran leadership of Khalid Mohammed bin Hussien, finally breached Isfahan's outer defences after a week of ferocious fighting on the city's outskirts. By August 22, Arab forces had completed the encirclement, severing all supply routes into Iran's third-largest city.

Following the encirclement, Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, the Greater Arab State's supreme leader, issued explicit orders to "starve the city to death, as Hitler did in Leningrad in 1941." Since then, Arab artillery and aircraft have pounded Isfahan daily, while Iranian forces attempting to break the siege have been repelled in dozens of counterattacks. Despite the onslaught, Iranian commanders inside the city have vowed to "never surrender."

Simultaneously, the 5th Army Group under Hussien Abualraham bin Muhammad achieved the campaign's most significant territorial gain to date: the capture of Shiraz on August 3. After the last defenders withdrew, Arab troops swept into Iran's sixth-largest city, securing its historic centre and strategic infrastructure.

The Shiraz victory has triggered two major encirclements. Around 110,000 Iranian troops remain trapped in a pocket centred on Firuzabad, while a rapid Arab armoured thrust has created a second pocket around Semirom, encircling an additional 90,000 Iranian soldiers.

The southern front also witnessed what military analysts are calling the largest tank battle since the Second World War. The Battle of Meymah saw Arab forces destroy 160 Iranian tanks in a decisive engagement that effectively shattered Iranian armoured resistance in the region.

Central Front: Qom in the crosshairs

Army Command Center, commanded by Ahmed Fahd al-Hussien, broke out of Arak and rapidly advanced in multiple directions. The swift manoeuvre created yet another pocket, trapping approximately 80,000 Iranian troops around the town of Jirya.

The central thrust has now linked up with Army Command South, allowing coordinated operations along a unified front. Arab forces have reached the outskirts of Kashan after heavy fighting and have begun intensive shelling in preparation for the city's final assault. Most significantly, Arab forward units now lie just 14 kilometres from Qom, Iran's holy city and spiritual centre, with spotters able to observe the city's skyline directly.

Northern Front: Tabriz within reach

Army Command North under Turki bin Abdullah achieved a stunning coup by capturing Zanjan with minimal resistance. Iranian forces, anticipating an attack on Bukan, were caught off-guard by the rapid thrust toward Zanjan. The city's fall has opened the road to Tabriz, the traditional final objective of the northern campaign.

Naval operations have complemented the land offensives. A series of surprise amphibious assaults rapidly seized Qeshm and Kish islands, though Iranian garrisons managed to evacuate most personnel back to the mainland.

Imperial mandates and living space

In a private cabinet meeting leaked to international media, al-Harbi outlined the long-term vision for occupied territories. Speaking in Arabic, he declared military administration zones would replicate the Nazi Reichskommissariats: temporary territories to be ruled until "subhuman races" are exterminated and the land resettled by the "superior Arab race."

"These zones are not merely long-term occupation," al-Harbi explained. "They are colonial projects to create living space for the Arab race so Arab men can make the land fertile, produce offspring, and purge the land of subhuman races."

With Isfahan encircled, Shiraz fallen and Qom within artillery range, the Greater Arab State appears poised to deliver a potentially fatal blow to the Iranian state. As August draws to a close, Iranian forces find themselves fighting for survival on multiple fronts, their strategic depth rapidly evaporating under the relentless Arab advance.
