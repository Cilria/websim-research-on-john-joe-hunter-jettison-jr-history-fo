Islamist Forces Declare Victory in Uzbekistan, Establish Islamic Emirate

After rapid advance across country, Army of Islam leader Muhammad Abdullayev declares emirate from Tashkent

By Carlotta Gall and Thomas Erdbrink | July 25, 2028 | Updated 11:23 AM ET

Fall of Major Cities

  • July 11: Bukhara captured after single day of fighting
  • July 15: Samarkand falls without resistance
  • July 19: Jizzakh surrenders
  • July 20: Fergana under insurgent control
  • July 21: Andijan falls
  • July 22: Namangan captured
  • July 24: Tashkent falls, government collapses

TASHKENT, Uzbekistan — Islamist insurgents completed their lightning conquest of Uzbekistan on Thursday, capturing the capital Tashkent without a fight and declaring the establishment of an Islamic Emirate of Uzbekistan, marking the most dramatic collapse of a Central Asian government since the fall of the Soviet Union.

The Army of Islam, which began as a regional insurgency just four months ago, has now seized control of the entire country except for the autonomous region of Karakalpakstan, which has declared independence. The group's leader, who had previously been known only by his nom de guerre Abu Abdullah al-Uzbeki, revealed his real identity as Muhammad Abdullayev and declared himself Emir of the new Islamic state.

In a triumphant address from the presidential palace in Tashkent, Abdullayev proclaimed that "Allah has blessed us with final victory and has defeated the infidel government and driven these criminal dogs out. Now the Uzbeks shall live the holy life that Allah commanded us. The Mujahideen have achieved the greatest victory for the way of Islam."

The rapid fall of Tashkent came just ten days after the insurgents captured the ancient city of Bukhara on July 11, following a single day of fighting. The subsequent days saw an unprecedented cascade of surrenders: Samarkand fell without resistance on July 15, followed by Jizzakh on July 19, Fergana on July 20, Andijan on July 21, and Namangan on July 22.

"This is the most stunning military collapse we have witnessed in the 21st century. A government with modern military capabilities simply evaporated in weeks."
— Dr. Alexander Cooley, Columbia University Central Asia expert

The speed of the insurgent advance caught both regional governments and Western intelligence agencies completely off guard. What began as a localized Islamist movement in the mountains of eastern Uzbekistan has transformed into a governing authority controlling the most populous nation in Central Asia.

The fall of Tashkent marks the culmination of a military campaign that began with the capture of Termez on June 22. Within a month, the insurgents had severed all major supply routes to the capital, encircled key cities, and forced the mass desertion of government military units.

Witnesses in Tashkent described scenes of chaos as government officials fled the capital in the final hours before the insurgent arrival. "The presidential convoy left in the middle of the night," said one resident who asked not to be named for security reasons. "By morning, there were no soldiers, no police, nothing."

The Islamic Emirate's declaration has sent shockwaves through the region, with neighboring countries immediately closing their borders and deploying additional military forces. Russia has placed its Central Asian military bases on high alert, while China has expressed "grave concern" about the security implications for its western regions.

The international community faces an unprecedented diplomatic crisis, with no clear path for engagement with the new Islamist government. The United States and European Union have not yet recognized the Islamic Emirate, though regional powers including Iran and Pakistan have reportedly established preliminary contacts.

Human rights organizations have expressed alarm about the implications for Uzbekistan's secular society, particularly for women and religious minorities. The insurgents have previously stated their intention to implement strict Sharia law and have already begun requiring women to wear Islamic dress in areas under their control.

The fall of Uzbekistan represents the most significant Islamist military victory since the Taliban's return to power in Afghanistan, but with far greater regional implications given Uzbekistan's larger population, strategic location, and historical role as Central Asia's most populous nation.
