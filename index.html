u/ByzantineExpert • 3 hours ago • 2.1k points
Completely different groups. The "White Huns" (Hephthalites) were a Central Asian nomadic confederation that controlled parts of modern Afghanistan, Pakistan, and northwest India from around 450-670 CE. They were roughly contemporary with the European Huns but operated in a completely different theater. The Hephthalites were called "White Huns" by later historians, but they probably called themselves something else entirely. We're not even sure what language they spoke - it might have been Bactrian, Sogdian, or some extinct Iranian language. They definitely weren't the same as Attila's Huns, who were probably Turkic or Mongolic.
u/CentralAsianArchaeology • 3 hours ago • 1.5k points
The name confusion comes from Byzantine historians who called both groups "Huns" because they were nomadic steppe warriors. It's like how Europeans called the Maya, Aztec, and Inca all "Indians" - it's a catch-all term that tells you more about the observer than the observed. The Hephthalites were actually more advanced than Attila's Huns. They built cities, issued coins, and controlled the Silk Road trade routes. They fought the Sassanid Persians and even captured the capital for a while. They were the major power in Central Asia for about 200 years. The European Huns were more like a loose confederation that fell apart after Attila's death.

Your friend is partially right, but it's complicated. The Huns were a confederation of Central Asian nomadic tribes who appeared in Europe around 370 CE and built a huge empire under Attila. They probably originated from the Xiongnu confederation in what's now Mongolia. The connection to modern Bulgarians is that the Bulgars (who founded Bulgaria) were a Turkic people who absorbed some Hun remnants. But modern Bulgarians are mostly Slavic culturally and linguistically. It's like saying modern Americans are "descendants of Vikings" because some Vikings settled in North America - technically some intermarriage happened, but it's not the dominant cultural inheritance.
u/BulgarianHistorian • 22 hours ago • 1.8k points
Bulgarian here. Your friend is probably confusing the Bulgars (our actual ancestors) with the Huns. The Bulgars were a Turkic people who arrived in the Balkans in the 7th century. Some Bulgars may have been part of Hun confederations earlier, but the Huns themselves were mostly gone by 460 CE. The real connection is that both Huns and Bulgars were steppe nomads, so they shared similar material culture and military tactics. We use "Hun" as a kind of cultural shorthand, but it's not accurate in a genetic sense. Modern Bulgarians are mostly Slavic with some Bulgar (Turkic) and Thracian admixture. No more "Hun" than any other European group.

The crazy thing about the Huns is that we don't even know what language they spoke. The Romans described them as speaking in a way that sounded like "the neighing of horses and the barking of dogs." Which is probably just Roman xenophobic nonsense. What we do know: they were probably a mix of various steppe peoples - Xiongnu, Alans, Sarmatians, and others. They didn't have a single "Hun" identity until they started conquering shit and absorbing other steppe peoples. They probably spoke a version of Turkic or Mongolic, but we have like 3 words of their language.
u/GeneticGenealogist • 20 hours ago • 1.2k points
DNA evidence shows that the Huns were incredibly diverse. Studies of Hun burial sites in Hungary show they were a mix of East Asian, Central Asian, and even some European ancestry. They were basically whoever joined their confederation. The "Hun" identity was probably more about being part of the military-political network than any ethnic or linguistic unity. Think of it like the Roman Empire - you could be a Roman citizen from Egypt or Britain, but you didn't have to be ethnically Italian.
u/SteppeLinguistics • 19 hours ago • 987 points
The linguistic evidence is fascinating and frustrating. The Romans recorded a few Hun personal names, but they were mostly the names of leaders who were probably from mixed backgrounds anyway. "Attila" itself is probably Gothic, not Hun - it means "little father" or "father-like." "Bleda" (Attila's brother) might be Turkic, but that's debated. The few words we have that might be Hun are mostly military terms that got absorbed by Germanic peoples. The real answer is they probably spoke multiple languages. A confederation that big would have had to be multilingual. The Huns themselves might have spoken a Turkic language, but their subjects spoke everything from Gothic to Latin to Sarmatian.
u/HunArchaeology • 18 hours ago • 765 points
The archaeological evidence is wild. Hun archaeological sites show this crazy mix of cultural influences. In Hungary, we find the typical steppe nomad stuff (bows, horses, felt carpets) but also Roman gold coins, Germanic jewelry, and even Chinese silk. They were the original cultural appropriators. Most interesting: their burial practices show a progression. Early Hun burials are mostly East Asian in style, but by the time they reached Europe, they're mixing in local traditions. They adapted to local customs while maintaining core steppe practices like horse burials and cranial deformation (some Huns artificially elongated their children's heads for status).

u/SteppeArchaeologist • 2 hours ago • 187 points
The head elongation is actually still practiced in some parts of the world, including parts of Bulgaria. Not saying it's a direct Hun connection, but cranial deformation was practiced by various steppe peoples who influenced Balkan cultures. The real Hun legacy is probably more cultural than genetic. They created a template for steppe empire-building that influenced later groups like the Avars, Bulgars, Khazars, and eventually the Mongols. They proved that a small group of steppe nomads could build a huge empire by incorporating other groups and using superior cavalry tactics. But yeah, as a distinct people they just vanished. They either intermarried into local populations, returned to the steppe, or were absorbed by other groups. The Huns weren't so much "destroyed" as they just stopped being Huns.
