Uzbek Islamic group vows armed struggle and vows to 'drive out Russian infidels'

Movement claims 10,000 supporters and threatens to overthrow government

By BBC News Central Asia Correspondent
14 April 2028

An Uzbek Islamist group has released a video outlining its plans to impose Sharia law and expel Russians from the country, marking a significant escalation in the movement's rhetoric.

The group, calling itself the Loyal Soldiers of Islam and the Islamic Movement, said in a video released on Sunday that it now has 10,000 supporters across Uzbekistan.

In the video, the group's leaders outlined a series of dramatic changes they would implement if they gained power, including switching the Uzbek alphabet back to Persian-Arabic script and forming an Islamic religious police force.

"O Allah, raise the great Mujahideen of Uzbekistan and unleash divine retribution and overthrow the infidel government of Uzbekistan, and drive out the Russian infidels out of the sacred soil."
— Group statement in video

The video represents the most direct threat yet from the movement, which emerged earlier this year amid a severe drought that has devastated large parts of Central Asia.

The group declared that "armed struggle will begin soon," though it did not specify a timeline for any potential attacks.

Key concerns

The movement's explicitly anti-Russian rhetoric could strain relations between Uzbekistan and Moscow, which has maintained close ties with Central Asian governments since the Soviet era.

Regional security analysts say the video marks a dangerous turning point in the group's evolution from a religious nationalist movement to a potentially violent insurgent group.

"This is no longer just rhetoric about establishing an Islamic state," said Dr. Elena Volkov, a Central Asia specialist at the Carnegie Moscow Centre. "They are explicitly calling for armed conflict and the expulsion of ethnic Russians."

The group reiterated its rejection of a pan-Islamic caliphate, instead focusing on what it terms the "Uzbek homeland" - a nationalist approach that some analysts believe could make it more appealing to ordinary Uzbeks frustrated with economic conditions.

"We will reclaim all of our Uzbek homeland, every inch that rightfully belongs to the Uzbek people under the guidance of Allah."
— Movement leader

The Uzbek government has not yet responded to the video, but security sources in Tashkent told the BBC that authorities are taking the threat "very seriously."

The timing of the video's release coincides with growing unrest in rural areas affected by the ongoing drought, which has left millions struggling with food shortages and economic hardship.

What the group says it will implement:

  • Full implementation of Sharia law
  • Switch from Cyrillic to Persian-Arabic alphabet
  • Expulsion of ethnic Russians deemed "infidels"
  • Formation of Islamic religious police
  • Reclaiming of "Uzbek homeland" territories

The international community has expressed concern about the deteriorating situation. The United Nations issued a statement calling for "restraint and dialogue" while the US State Department said it was "monitoring developments closely."

Russia's Foreign Ministry declined to comment specifically on the video, but a spokesperson said Moscow "expects the Uzbek authorities to ensure the safety of all citizens, including ethnic Russians."
