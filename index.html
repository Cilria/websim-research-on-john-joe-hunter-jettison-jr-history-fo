BREAKING: Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel captures Bamako and advances on multiple fronts

Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel captures Bamako in lightning offensive and advances toward Senegal and Nigeria

Jihadist forces seize Malian capital in 72-hour assault, capture 50+ towns in Guinea, and cross into Nigeria after rapid territorial expansion

By BBC News Staff March 23, 2027 Dakar, Senegal
[Image: Jihadist fighters entering Bamako's Independence Square after three-day battle]

The Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel has captured Bamako, Mali's capital city, after a fierce 72-hour assault that overwhelmed government forces and established the jihadist group as the dominant power in West Africa, according to multiple military and intelligence sources.

The fall of Bamako represents the most significant victory yet for the expanding caliphate, which has now seized control of territory stretching from the Atlantic Ocean to the borders of Nigeria, encompassing parts of five nations in less than a month.

72-Hour Battle for Bamako

Day 1: Jihadist forces surround capital from three directions, cutting main highways

Day 2: Heavy fighting in suburbs, government forces retreat toward city center

Day 3: Presidential palace falls, president reportedly flees by helicopter

"They came in waves, using the same pickup trucks and technicals we saw in Burkina Faso, but now they're operating like a conventional army," said a Western military observer who requested anonymity. "Surface-to-air missiles kept our surveillance drones at bay while they methodically reduced the city's defenses."

The capture of Bamako comes as the group launches multiple offensives across the region. In Guinea, jihadist forces have seized more than 50 towns in just four days, exploiting the country's political instability and weak military presence along its northern borders.

Territorial Control as of March 23, 2027

Full Control: Burkina Faso, Eastern Mali, Northern Benin, Northern Togo

Partial Control: Guinea (50+ towns), Western Niger

Active Fighting: Senegal border, Nigeria border

Perhaps most dramatically, the caliphate's forces have crossed into Nigeria, Africa's most populous nation and largest economy. Initial incursions were reported in the northern states of Kebbi and Sokoto, with jihadist fighters establishing checkpoints along major highways.

"They're not just raiding anymore — they're staying," said a Nigerian security official. "They're setting up administrative structures, collecting taxes, imposing their version of Islamic law. This is conquest, not terrorism."

Lightning Offensive: Key Statistics

Territory seized: Estimated 400,000 square kilometers in 72 hours

Towns captured: 50+ in Guinea, 15 in Benin, 8 in Togo

Population under control: Approximately 45 million people

Weapons captured: Enough to equip 100,000 fighters

In Senegal, the situation has become increasingly precarious. The caliphate's forces seized Tambacouda on March 22nd, bringing them within 150 kilometers of The Gambia and threatening to cut Senegal in half. The speed of the advance has prompted mass civilian evacuations from eastern Senegal.

"We're seeing something unprecedented in modern African history," said Dr. Amina Diallo, regional security expert at the University of Dakar. "A jihadist force has gone from controlling remote desert regions to threatening coastal capitals in less than a month. The entire regional security architecture has collapsed."

The offensive into Benin has proven particularly successful, with the caliphate capturing Kandi and establishing control over northern Benin's entire border region. This expansion gives the group access to Atlantic ports for the first time, potentially enabling weapons shipments and trade routes.

Northern Togo has also fallen under caliphate control, with jihadist fighters establishing checkpoints along the main highway connecting the country to Burkina Faso. The Togolese military, never designed for large-scale conventional warfare, has reportedly withdrawn to defend the capital, Lomé.

The complete seizure of Niamey, Niger's capital, was confirmed early Tuesday. Unlike the chaotic fall of Bamako, the Niger operation appeared carefully orchestrated, with jihadist forces methodically securing key government buildings, media outlets, and transportation hubs before announcing their control.

"They've learned from their previous victories," said a regional intelligence analyst. "They're not just conquering territory — they're learning to govern it, to administer it, to extract resources from it. This makes them far more dangerous than a simple terrorist organization."

The breakout into the Sahara Desert represents perhaps the most strategically significant development. Rather than consolidating their existing gains, caliphate forces have launched lightning raids across the desert, seizing oasis towns and establishing supply lines that stretch from Libya to Mauritania.

"They're using the desert like a highway," explained a military intelligence source. "Traditional borders mean nothing when you can move 500 kilometers overnight across open desert. They're hitting towns that haven't seen conflict in decades."

The international response has been limited and largely ineffective. The United Nations Security Council is scheduled to meet Thursday, but regional diplomats say any meaningful intervention could take weeks or months to organize — time the caliphate is using to consolidate its gains.

"Every day they hold territory, they become stronger," warned a Western diplomat. "They're recruiting locally, training new fighters, establishing supply chains. Soon we'll be looking at a jihadist state controlling more territory than many European countries."

As the situation continues to evolve rapidly, the question is no longer whether the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel can be stopped — but whether anyone can prevent them from controlling the entire Sahel region and threatening Africa's coastal states.

"This isn't just a West African problem anymore," said Dr. Diallo. "If they reach the Atlantic and establish a jihadist state, it becomes a global security crisis. And right now, nothing seems capable of stopping their advance."
