The United States has officially commenced military operations against the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel, with Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth confirming that "military operations have begun" against the rapidly expanding jihadist group, CNN has learned.

President Trump announced the operation in a series of tweets early Tuesday morning, declaring that coalition forces are "hitting them HARDER than anyone thought possible" as the Caliphate's forces continue their lightning advance across West Africa.

International Coalition Response

United States: Airstrikes targeting Caliphate positions in Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger

France: Rafale fighter jets conducting precision strikes in former colonies

United Kingdom: RAF Typhoons joining coordinated bombing campaign

Additional allies: Germany, Italy, and Spain providing logistical support

The military intervention comes as Caliphate forces have laid siege to Kaduna, a major Nigerian city just 50 kilometers from the capital Abuja, threatening to topple Africa's most populous nation and largest economy.

"We cannot stand by while a jihadist army threatens to capture an allied capital," Hegseth said in a Pentagon briefing. "The Caliphate's expansion has reached a critical threshold that demands immediate military response."

Current Battlefield Situation

Kaduna Siege: Caliphate forces encircling city, heavy fighting reported at city limits

Ivory Coast: Korhogo falls to Caliphate after 18-hour battle

Nigerian Response: Government forces establishing defensive perimeter around Abuja

Coalition Targets: Command centers, weapons depots, and supply lines under attack

French President Emmanuel Macron confirmed that French forces have joined the operation, with Rafale fighter jets conducting precision strikes against Caliphate positions in former French colonies. The United Kingdom has also deployed RAF Typhoons from bases in Cyprus.

"This is not just an African problem — it's a global security crisis," Macron said in Paris. "We have a responsibility to act before this jihadist state becomes permanent."

The coalition is targeting what military officials describe as the Caliphate's "center of gravity" — command centers, weapons depots, and supply lines that have enabled their rapid expansion across five nations in less than a month.

Intelligence sources tell CNN that the initial strikes have destroyed several key facilities, including a major weapons depot near Timbuktu and command centers in both Bamako and Niamey. However, officials caution that the operation is just beginning.

"This won't be a quick fix," warned a senior military official who spoke on condition of anonymity. "They've had time to dig in, to prepare for this. We're prepared for a sustained campaign."

The siege of Kaduna represents the most immediate crisis. Caliphate forces have surrounded the city of 1.5 million people, cutting off major highways and reportedly using captured Nigerian military equipment in their assault.

"They're using our own weapons against us," said a Nigerian military spokesman. "Tanks, artillery, even some aircraft they captured when they overran our bases in the north."

Meanwhile, the fall of Korhogo in Ivory Coast after an 18-hour battle has given the Caliphate a strategic foothold in the country, opening new supply routes and threatening to destabilize one of West Africa's more stable democracies.

Beyond the immediate military response, officials tell CNN that operations have begun to dismantle the Caliphate's international support network. The Treasury Department is preparing sanctions against individuals and entities accused of supporting the group, while intelligence agencies are targeting their financial and logistical networks worldwide.

Global Response Network

Financial: Treasury sanctions targeting Caliphate supporters globally

Intelligence: Coordinated effort to disrupt international support networks

Diplomatic: UN Security Council emergency session scheduled

Humanitarian: Aid agencies preparing for potential refugee crisis

The international response extends beyond military action. The UN Security Council is scheduled to hold an emergency session Tuesday evening to discuss broader international measures, including potential peacekeeping operations and humanitarian assistance for the millions of civilians caught in the conflict zone.

"We're looking at a potential humanitarian catastrophe," said a UN official. "Millions of people are displaced, cities are under siege, and the Caliphate's advance shows no signs of slowing."

As night falls over West Africa, coalition aircraft continue their bombing campaign while Caliphate forces press their attacks on multiple fronts. The coming days will determine whether the international intervention can halt what many experts now describe as the most significant jihadist expansion since the rise of ISIS.

"This is a turning point," said Dr. Jennifer Cooke, Africa expert at the Center for Strategic and International Studies. "Either the coalition succeeds in pushing them back, or we're looking at a new jihadist state controlling much of West Africa. The stakes couldn't be higher."

President Trump, in his final tweet on the matter Tuesday evening, struck a defiant tone: "The Caliphate made a big mistake threatening our allies. They wanted war? They're going to get it. And we're going to WIN. America is BACK!"
