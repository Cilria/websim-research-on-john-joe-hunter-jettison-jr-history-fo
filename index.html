South Carolina senator's newly leaked texts show him calling America a 'white nation,' praising Hitler

Earl LeRoy Carter Sr. wrote 'I am a fascist' and said democracy was 'the greatest mistake of the founders,' latest messages reveal

By NBC News Staff | Updated 6:47 AM ET

COLUMBIA, South Carolina — A day after facing bipartisan condemnation for advocating the return of slavery and opposing women's suffrage, South Carolina state Sen. Earl LeRoy Carter Sr. is confronting even more explosive revelations as newly leaked text messages show him declaring America a "white nation," calling for the mass deportation of millions of Americans, and praising Adolf Hitler.

The latest batch of messages, obtained exclusively by NBC News, reveal the 67-year-old Republican lawmaker explicitly identifying as a fascist and calling democracy "the greatest mistake of the founders."

"America is a white nation, always has been, always will be," Carter wrote in a June 15 text to the same private group chat of Republican activists. "Anyone whose ancestors came here after 1840 needs to go back where they came from."

The message would ironically apply to Carter himself, as public records show his paternal great-grandparents immigrated from Ireland in 1852.

"I am a fascist. Democracy was the greatest mistake of the founders. Hitler was right about the Jews. The only thing he did wrong was lose." — State Sen. Earl LeRoy Carter Sr. in leaked text message

In another message dated June 18, Carter wrote: "I am a fascist. Democracy was the greatest mistake of the founders. Hitler was right about the Jews. The only thing he did wrong was lose."

The senator also praised authoritarian leaders, writing on June 22: "Putin, Xi, Kim — these men understand what it takes to maintain order. America needs strong leadership, not this weak democracy nonsense."

The explosive new revelations come as Carter attempted to hold a town hall meeting Thursday evening in Laurens County to defend his positions. Only two people showed up — both to protest his continued presence in office.

"I came here to boo him," said Margaret Wilson, 72, a retired teacher who drove 40 minutes to attend. "This man is an embarrassment to South Carolina and to humanity itself."

The other attendee, 28-year-old local restaurant worker James Black, said he "just wanted to see if anyone would actually defend this guy."

"Turns out, even his supporters have abandoned him," Black said. "The parking lot was empty except for a few reporters."

The latest texts have prompted universal condemnation, with even Carter's previous allies rushing to distance themselves. The South Carolina Republican Party issued a statement Friday morning calling for his "immediate and unconditional resignation."

"These latest messages are beyond the pale and represent views that are antithetical to everything the Republican Party stands for," said party chairman Drew McKissick. "Senator Carter must resign immediately."

White House Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre called the messages "a horrifying reminder that white supremacy and antisemitism remain persistent threats in American politics."

The European Union's ambassador to the United States issued a rare statement calling Carter's praise of Hitler "deeply disturbing and completely unacceptable from any elected official in a democratic society."

Locally, the fallout has been severe. The Laurens County Council voted unanimously to censure Carter and called for his resignation. The local Chamber of Commerce reported that several businesses are considering relocating if Carter remains in office.

"This is economic poison for our community," said Chamber president Lisa Anderson. "Who wants to invest in a place represented by someone who praises Hitler?"

Carter's legislative colleagues have moved quickly to isolate him. Senate Majority Leader Shane Massey announced that Carter has been stripped of all committee assignments and will be removed from the Republican caucus.

"He will serve the remainder of his term as an independent with no influence whatsoever in the Senate," Massey said.

Legal experts note that while Carter's views are widely condemned, they likely do not provide grounds for removal from office under South Carolina law.

"The First Amendment protects even the most abhorrent speech," said constitutional law professor Danielle Lang of Duke University. "The proper remedy here is political, not legal."

Carter remains defiant, telling NBC News in a brief phone call Friday morning that he has "nothing to apologize for" and claiming the latest leak is part of a "deep state conspiracy to silence patriotic Americans."

"The truth is the truth, whether people like it or not," Carter said before hanging up.

The controversy shows no signs of abating, with national media outlets descending on Laurens County and protests planned for the state capitol in Columbia this weekend.

"I've been covering South Carolina politics for 40 years," said veteran political columnist Lee Bandy. "I've never seen anything remotely like this. The man has managed to unite every decent person in the state against him in less than 48 hours."

Carter's term expires in 2028, and he has already filed paperwork indicating his intention to seek reelection. Given the universal condemnation and complete absence of support, political observers say his political career appears effectively over.

"He's radioactive," said Knotts, the College of Charleston professor. "No one will touch him. He's finished in politics, and frankly, he should be finished in polite society."
