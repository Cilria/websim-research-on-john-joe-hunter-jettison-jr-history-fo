Chinese social media users openly mock Russia and warn of 'Curse of Timur' as tensions simmer

Users on Chinese platforms compare Russians to "ants" and invoke medieval conqueror's warning

By CNN Staff
Updated 11:23 AM EDT, Thu October 22, 2026

Hong Kong (CNN) — Chinese social media users are openly mocking Russia and insulting Russians online, comparing them to "ants" and invoking the historical "Curse of Timur" in what analysts say represents a significant shift in public sentiment toward Beijing's strategic partner.

The online attacks, which have surged in recent days across popular Chinese platforms including Weibo and WeChat, signal growing frustration among Chinese citizens over what they perceive as Russia's weakening position and increasing dependence on China.

"Foolish Russia, they think we are their actual allies," one widely circulated post from user @DragonKing2026 reads. "Don't they realize, they are selling their nation to US. We have won. If they even dare to wage an act or mock us. They shall face an invader worse than Timur... Russia will be destroyed to the ashbins of history, much as Mongols did so."

The reference to Timur — also known as Tamerlane, the 14th-century conqueror who defeated various Russian principalities — represents a particularly pointed historical warning. Chinese users frequently cite Timur's campaigns as precedent for Asian powers bringing Russia to heel.

Another viral post from @SilkRoad2026 warns: "Russia forgets their history. When Timur came, they begged for mercy. Now they dare speak against the Middle Kingdom? They will learn respect again."

The online hostility comes amid broader geopolitical tensions and follows what Chinese netizens perceive as Russia's increasing economic dependence on China, particularly in energy and technology sectors.

"The shift in Chinese public opinion toward Russia has been dramatic. These aren't isolated comments — we're seeing systematic mockery and historical warnings that would have been unthinkable just two years ago."
— Maria Chen, Digital Asia Research Institute

Chinese authorities have not moved to censor the anti-Russian content, suggesting tacit approval of the nationalist sentiment, according to analysts monitoring Chinese social media.

"The government is allowing this narrative to flourish," said David Liu, senior fellow at the Beijing Center for Strategic Studies. "It serves their purpose to remind Moscow of their growing asymmetry in the relationship."

The posts frequently reference Russia's military struggles and economic challenges, with users suggesting China could easily replace Russia as the dominant Eurasian power.

"They are weak, like old bears without teeth," reads another popular comment. "China has no need for such allies who bring only shame and failure."

The phenomenon represents a remarkable reversal from just months ago, when Chinese state media praised the "no-limits partnership" between Beijing and Moscow. Now, Chinese users openly question whether Russia deserves its position as China's primary strategic partner.

As tensions continue to simmer, analysts expect the online hostility to persist, potentially complicating the diplomatic relationship between the two powers.

"The genie is out of the bottle," Liu said. "Chinese nationalism has found a new target, and the historical warnings suggest this isn't just about current disputes — it's about teaching Russia a lasting lesson."
