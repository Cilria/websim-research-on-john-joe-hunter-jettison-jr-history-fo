What if the Mongol Empire never rose? How would world history change?

Posted by u/HistoryBuff2024 OP • 6 hours ago

I've been thinking about this scenario lately. The Mongol Empire completely reshaped the medieval world - they destroyed the Abbasid Caliphate, created Russia by forcing the eastern Slavs together, spread gunpowder technology, and opened up the Silk Road trade routes.

What if Genghis Khan never united the Mongol tribes or died early? How different would the world look today without the Mongol conquests?

MedievalHistorian PhD Medieval Studies 5 hours ago

This is actually one of the most consequential "what ifs" in world history. The Mongols were basically the catalyst that dragged Eurasia out of the medieval period. Without them:

1. The Abbasid Caliphate probably survives - Baghdad was the intellectual capital of the world when the Mongols destroyed it in 1258. We'd likely see continued Islamic scientific advancement rather than the decline that followed.

2. No Russia as we know it - The Mongols forced the eastern Slavic principalities to unite under Moscow's leadership to pay tribute. Without that pressure, you probably get several smaller Slavic kingdoms instead of one massive Russian empire.

3. China stays divided longer - The Song dynasty was actually quite technologically advanced. The Mongols unified China, but a fragmented China might have developed differently.

GunpowderGeek Military Technology Historian 5 hours ago

People don't realize how crucial the Mongols were to the development of gunpowder empires. The Mongols learned about gunpowder from the Chinese, then spread it across Eurasia as they conquered. They used gunpowder weapons in their siege of Baghdad - that's how the Middle East first encountered it.

Without the Mongol conquests, gunpowder probably stays a Chinese curiosity for centuries longer. No Ottoman artillery, no European firearms development, no age of exploration with cannons on ships.

The entire trajectory of military technology gets delayed by 200+ years. Colonialism as we know it might not even happen because European powers wouldn't have the technological advantage.

RenaissanceScholar PhD Art History 4 hours ago

Here's a wild one: no Italian Renaissance as we know it.

The Black Death that hit Europe in the mid-1300s was partially spread by Mongol trade routes. But more importantly, when the Mongols unified the Silk Road, they created this massive trade network that made Italian city-states incredibly wealthy.

Without Mongol protection of trade routes, the Silk Road becomes much more fragmented and dangerous. Italian merchants don't get rich from Asian luxury goods. The wealth that funded Renaissance art and architecture in Florence and Venice just... doesn't exist.

We might see a Renaissance, but it would be completely different - maybe centered in the Islamic world instead, since the Abbasid Caliphate would still be thriving.

CentralAsianNomad Steppe History 3 hours ago

From a Central Asian perspective, no Mongol Empire means the entire region stays Buddhist and Manichean instead of becoming Islamic.

The Mongols were religiously tolerant, but their successors in places like the Ilkhanate and Golden Horde eventually converted to Islam. This spread Islam throughout the steppes and into places like Xinjiang and the Caucasus.

Without the Mongol conversion, Central Asia probably remains a Buddhist/Tengrist region. The Silk Road cities like Samarkand and Bukhara stay as they were - diverse, but not Islamic centers. This completely changes the religious map of Asia.

DemographyDude Historical Demographics 2 hours ago

OP is absolutely right about the demographic impact being absolutely massive. The Mongol conquests killed more people relative to world population than either World War.

Without that massive die-off:

• China's population stays higher - the Jin and Song dynasties had maybe 120 million people combined. Without Mongol devastation, Chinese population growth continues steadily.

• Iran becomes the demographic center of the Islamic world - the Mongols absolutely devastated Iran, killing maybe 75% of the population. Without that, Iran remains what it was in the 1100s - the most populous, sophisticated part of the Islamic world.

• Russia develops much earlier - the Mongol destruction of Russian principalities set back urban development by centuries. Without that, you might see a much earlier Russian state with much larger population.

The ripple effects are mind-boggling. Higher populations mean more technological innovation, more economic development, more everything.

One thing people always miss: the Song dynasty might have industrialized.

The Song were incredibly advanced - they had movable type printing, sophisticated metallurgy, advanced mathematics, paper money, and were developing proto-industrial techniques. They were on the verge of something big when the Mongols conquered them.

Without the Mongol conquest, the Song continue developing. Maybe they don't industrialize completely, but they might get 70-80% of the way there by 1400. That means China, not Europe, leads the industrial revolution.

Imagine a world where China industrializes in the 1400s instead of Britain in the 1700s. Everything - and I mean everything - would be different.

Here's a crazy thought: no Age of Exploration.

The Ottoman Empire rose in part because they controlled the trade routes that the Mongols had previously made safe. Without the Mongols, the Silk Road stays fragmented and dangerous. European powers don't get rich from Asian trade.

But more importantly, without the fall of Constantinople (which itself was connected to the Ottoman rise), Europeans don't have that pressure to find sea routes to Asia. No Columbus, no Vasco da Gama, no Age of Exploration.

The Americas aren't "discovered" (from the European perspective) until much later, and by then, indigenous civilizations might be much more advanced and capable of resisting colonization.
