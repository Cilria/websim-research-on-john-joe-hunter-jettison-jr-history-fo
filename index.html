Arab State stages Roman-style triumph after Yemen annexation

Al-Harbi proclaims 'Arab victory' and vows next wars against Jews and Persians
By Andrew England, FT Middle East Editor

Riyadh — Muhammad Khalid al-Harbi, leader of the Arab State, presided yesterday over a vast military parade modelled on Roman triumphs to celebrate the complete annexation of Yemen, delivering a three-hour speech that marked his most bellicose rhetoric to date.

Addressing nearly 3 million supporters in the capital, al-Harbi declared that Arab forces had achieved “a victory that rivals the greatness of Germany in 1940” and vowed that Yemen was merely the opening phase of a wider campaign to purify the region.

Columns of Storm Front troops parade through central Riyadh to mark the annexation of Yemen

“The Tigris and Nile rivers will bleed with the blood of enemies and inferior species in the great mission of purification,” al-Harbi proclaimed from a raised dais flanked by giant portraits of himself. “Yemen was just the beginning. Soon we will unite Syria and Egypt, and then we will finally wage war against the greatest threat to the Arab race, two satanic forces, two subhuman creatures, scum that must be eliminated: the Jews and the Persians.”

“The Arabs will not get their land until this scum is eradicated forever!”

The spectacle, replete with armoured columns, missile launchers and thousands of uniformed troops chanting “Arabs Above All!”, signalled al-Harbi’s ambition to fuse modern totalitarian pageantry with ancient imperial symbolism. Columns of the rebranded Storm Front regiment—formerly the Saudi National Guard—marched in perfect synchrony while fighter jets traced the new Arab State flag across a cloudless sky.

Abdullah Ibn Tariq, the recently appointed minister of war, followed al-Harbi with a fiery address pledging that the Arab State would “not rest until every metre of the fatherland is cleansed”. State media reported that the parade was broadcast live across the Arab State’s newly integrated territories, from the former Yemeni highlands to the Red Sea coast.

Western diplomats who monitored the event said the scale and tone of the rally eclipsed anything seen in the region since Saddam Hussein’s post-Gulf War parades. “This was not just victory lap; it was a declaration of future wars,” one European envoy told the FT.

Al-Harbi’s speech ended with a characteristic flourish: “Victory to the Arab race, victory to Arab fascism, and wake up, Arabs!” The crowd responded with thunderous cheers and a choreographed fireworks display that lit up the desert skyline.

Analysts warn that the annexation of Yemen—completed in just over a week—has emboldened al-Harbi to accelerate his stated goal of uniting the Arab world under a single, militant banner. Satellite imagery shows continued mobilisation of armoured units near the borders of Jordan and Iraq, while the Arab State’s propaganda channels have begun depicting Damascus and Cairo as “historical capitals awaiting liberation”.

Financial markets reacted nervously. Brent crude jumped 4.2 per cent to $97 a barrel on fears that the Arab State’s next moves could disrupt shipping through the Red Sea and the Suez Canal. “Investors are pricing in a non-trivial probability of a wider regional war,” said Amrita Sen of Energy Aspects.

As night fell over Riyadh, columns of Storm Front troops filed out of the city toward new staging areas, their standards fluttering in the desert wind—an unmistakable signal that, for al-Harbi, the conquest of Yemen is only the prologue to a far bloodier drama.
