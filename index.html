Explosion Near Kaaba in Mecca Kills Thousands in Deadliest Terrorist Attack in History

The blast, equivalent to 11 tons of TNT, killed nearly 60,000 people and was heard 500 miles away

By Ben Hubbard and Farnaz Fassihi
April 11, 2029
MECCA, Saudi Arabia
The aftermath of the explosion near the Kaaba, which killed nearly 60,000 people in the deadliest terrorist attack in human history

MECCA, Saudi Arabia —

An enormous explosion tore through the area surrounding the Kaaba in the heart of Mecca on Thursday, killing nearly 60,000 people in what instantly became the deadliest terrorist attack in human history, according to Saudi officials and witnesses.

The blast, which Saudi authorities said was caused by a bomb that generated 46 gigajoules of energy — equivalent to 11 tons of TNT — destroyed much of the Grand Mosque complex and sent a plume of smoke rising over Islam's holiest site. The sound of the explosion was heard as far away as Tabuk, more than 500 miles from Mecca.

A militant group calling itself the Harbingers of the Final Hour quickly claimed responsibility for the attack, saying it had detonated the device as the first step in its campaign to bring about the end times.

"Today, we have executed the first step in unleashing the endtimes," Abu al-Mahdi al-Akhir, the group's leader, said in a message posted on social media. "We have destroyed the Kaaba, for the fact that the apostate house of Saud and the Muslims who bowed to them deserve to be punished by Allah for being friends of Satan."

The attack eclipsed the death toll from every previous terrorist attack, including 9/11, and immediately plunged the Muslim world into a state of shock and mourning. The victims included thousands of pilgrims who had gathered for evening prayers, as well as local residents and workers in the surrounding area.

A Scene of Carnage

Witnesses described a scene of unimaginable carnage, with bodies strewn across the marble courtyard surrounding the Kaaba and the air thick with smoke and the smell of explosives.

"I saw people flying through the air like dolls," said Ahmed al-Qahtani, 34, a shopkeeper who was about 200 yards from the Kaaba when the blast occurred. "There were pieces of bodies everywhere. The sound was like the world ending."

The explosion created a crater estimated to be 40 feet deep and 120 feet wide, according to satellite images analyzed by The Times. The blast also damaged or destroyed several nearby hotels and commercial buildings.

Saudi officials said the timing of the attack — during the evening prayer when the area around the Kaaba is typically most crowded — appeared designed to maximize casualties.

"This is not just an attack on Saudi Arabia or even on Islam. This is an attack on the very fabric of civilization itself. The idea that someone would deliberately target the holiest site in Islam during prayer time shows a level of depravity that is simply beyond comprehension." — António Guterres, United Nations Secretary-General

Global Condemnation

World leaders rushed to condemn the attack, with President Biden calling it "an act of pure evil" and ordering flags flown at half-staff at all federal buildings.

"The United States stands with Saudi Arabia and with Muslims around the world in mourning this unspeakable tragedy," Mr. Biden said in a televised address from the White House. "We will hunt down those responsible and bring them to justice, no matter how long it takes."

The attack drew condemnation even from groups that have historically been hostile to Saudi Arabia, including Yemen's Houthi rebels and Lebanon's Hezbollah, both of which called the bombing "a crime against all of Islam."

Iran's supreme leader, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, called the attack "a tragedy that transcends politics" and ordered three days of national mourning in Iran.

But the group that claimed responsibility showed no remorse, warning that the Mecca bombing was only the beginning of a campaign that would ultimately lead to the destruction of all life on Earth.

"This is only the start," Abu al-Mahdi al-Akhir said in his message. "Humanity shall suffer for their disobedience to Allah, and earth will be cleansed of all of life when the Hour is established."

A New Level of Terror

Terrorism experts said the attack represented a terrifying escalation in both scale and ambition from previous terrorist groups.

"We've never seen anything like this," said Bruce Hoffman, a terrorism expert at Georgetown University. "This isn't just about killing people — it's about destroying the very foundations of one of the world's major religions and triggering the apocalypse."

The use of such a powerful explosive device also raised questions about how the group had obtained the materials and expertise to build such a weapon. Some analysts speculated that the group may have obtained military-grade explosives or even nuclear materials.

"A conventional bomb of this size would require a massive amount of explosives and sophisticated engineering," said Dr. Gary Ackerman, director of the National Consortium for the Study of Terrorism and Responses to Terrorism. "This raises very troubling questions about what else this group might be capable of."

"The speed of their advance is deeply concerning. They've gone from an obscure militant group to controlling major cities in a matter of days, and now they've carried out the deadliest terrorist attack in history." — General David Petraeus, former CIA Director

Security Implications

The attack immediately raised security concerns across the Muslim world, with governments from Indonesia to Morocco stepping up security at mosques and other religious sites.

In Saudi Arabia, King Salman announced the temporary closure of all mosques nationwide while security services conducted sweeps for additional explosive devices. The Saudi government also closed its borders and suspended all international flights.

The attack also sent shock waves through global financial markets, with oil prices spiking 15 percent on fears of wider conflict in the region and major stock indexes falling sharply.

"This attack changes everything," said Ali Shihabi, a Saudi analyst and former government adviser. "The idea that someone would target the Kaaba — it's like targeting the Vatican, or the Western Wall, or the Kaaba. It's unthinkable."

As night fell over Mecca, rescue workers continued to search through the rubble for survivors, while religious leaders around the world called for prayers for the victims and their families.

But with the Harbingers of the Final Hour promising more attacks to come, many were left wondering what could possibly come next.

"If they're willing to do this," said one Saudi official who spoke on condition of anonymity, "what won't they do?"
