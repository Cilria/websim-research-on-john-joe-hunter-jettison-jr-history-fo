Saudi ultranationalist leader declares himself fascist at mass rally

Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi tells 500,000 supporters he will 'save the Arab nation'
By BBC News Middle East correspondent

Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi, the ultranationalist leader who has emerged as Saudi Arabia's most powerful political figure, has openly declared himself a fascist while addressing a crowd of nearly 500,000 supporters in Dammam.

The speech, titled "The Arab Truth and the Final Awakening", marks a dramatic escalation in rhetoric from the man who now enjoys over 80% approval ratings - higher than the Saudi King himself.

[Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi addressing supporters in Dammam]
Al-Harbi's supporters chanted "The Leader!" as he spoke

"It is time for Arabs to wake up to the truth," Al-Harbi declared to the massive crowd. "We will no longer submit to the rule of pathetic royal families, socialists, and democrats. No! From now on, Arabs will submit to one rule, one nation, and one party!"

The crowd responded with thunderous chants of "The Leader!" and "Arabs Above All!" as members of his Storm Unit paramilitary force patrolled the area. Witnesses reported that Storm Unit members beat an Indian worker who was filming the rally on his phone.

Perhaps most striking was Al-Harbi's unapologetic embrace of fascist ideology. "I am a fascist, and I am proud of it," he proclaimed. "Fascism is the medicine that Arabs must take to realize the truth."

"I am a fascist, and I am proud of it. Fascism is the medicine that Arabs must take to realize the truth."

Al-Harbi then launched into a historical narrative that framed the Middle East's last thousand years as a racial struggle. "The last thousand years of Middle Eastern history have been a battle between the different ethnic groups in the region: Arabs, Indians, Jews, and Persians," he claimed.

He specifically referenced what he called "the humiliation of 1916" - a reference to the Arab Revolt against the Ottoman Empire - as "a great shame on the Arabs."

"No more of that," he declared. "With me at the helm, I will save the Arab nation!"

The rally represents the culmination of Al-Harbi's meteoric rise from anonymous Telegram user to potentially the most powerful political figure in Saudi Arabia. His movement, which began as a small anti-Indian protest group, has evolved into a mass nationalist movement that now threatens the kingdom's traditional power structures.

Security analysts are particularly concerned about the Storm Unit, which functions as both a paramilitary force and a political police. Dressed in white armbands, they have become increasingly visible at rallies, and there are growing reports of intimidation tactics against perceived enemies of the movement.

The Saudi government has so far remained silent on Al-Harbi's latest speech, continuing a pattern of restraint that has puzzled many observers. Some speculate this reflects uncertainty about how to handle a movement that appears to have significant popular support.

As the crowd dispersed into the Dammam night, many supporters spoke of a new era in Saudi history. Whether that era will bring stability or chaos remains to be seen. But one thing is clear: Muhammad Khalid Al-Harbi has emerged from the shadows to become a force that Saudi Arabia's rulers can no longer ignore.
