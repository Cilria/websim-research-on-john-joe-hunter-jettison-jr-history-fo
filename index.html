Coalition Ground Forces Launch Major Offensive Across West Africa, Gaining Ground on Multiple Fronts

U.S., French and NATO troops advance with local allies, encircling key Caliphate strongholds as new supreme leader maintains conventional warfare strategy despite devastating losses

By Declan Walsh, Eric Schmitt and Ruth Maclean July 20, 2027 Ouagadougou, Burkina Faso
[Image: U.S. Marines advancing alongside Ivorian troops near Korhogo, with coalition aircraft overhead]

Coalition ground forces have launched their long-awaited ground offensive across West Africa, with American, French and NATO troops advancing alongside local forces on multiple fronts in what military officials describe as the beginning of the end for the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel.

The offensive, which began on June 30th, has gained significant momentum over the past three weeks, with coalition forces recapturing thousands of square kilometers of territory and encircling key Caliphate strongholds in six countries, according to military briefings and interviews with officials across the region.

Current Front Status

Ivory Coast: Northern areas largely recaptured, Korhogo encircled with 1,500 jihadists trapped
Nigeria: A1 Highway cut, Caliphate forces encircled in Yelwa, preparing for Sokoto offensive
Togo: Kran National Park recaptured, advancing on Dapaong stronghold
Benin: 3,500 square km recaptured along RNIE2 Highway toward Kandi
Senegal: N7 highway cut, first phase to isolate Tambacounda underway
Guinea: Siguiri encircled, large Caliphate force trapped
Mali/Niger: Tuareg rebels backed by coalition advance slowly through desert

"We're seeing something we haven't seen in months — momentum," said General Michael Langley, commander of U.S. Africa Command, in an interview at coalition headquarters in Abidjan. "The Caliphate's leadership vacuum created by the June airstrike is finally translating into territorial losses."

The most dramatic advances have come on the Ivory Coast front, where U.S. Marines and French Foreign Legion troops, fighting alongside Ivorian forces, have rapidly recaptured most of the northern regions. The city of Korhogo, where an estimated 1,500 Caliphate fighters have prepared what officials describe as a "last stand," is now completely encircled.

Korhogo Encirclement

Coalition forces have established a 15-kilometer perimeter around the city, with U.S. artillery positioned on surrounding hills and French special forces conducting precision strikes against defensive positions. The trapped jihadists have reportedly begun rationing food and ammunition.

In Nigeria, coalition forces have achieved what military planners call a "strategic breakthrough" by cutting the A1 Highway, the main supply route connecting Caliphate positions. This maneuver has left an estimated 800-1,200 jihadist fighters encircled in the city of Yelwa, where they are now preparing for what officials expect to be a prolonged siege.

"We're not just pushing them back — we're cutting them off," said Nigerian Army Chief of Staff General Christopher Musa. "Every encirclement means fewer fighters we have to face when we advance on Sokoto."

Air Power Advantage

Coalition aircraft are flying up to 200 sorties daily across all fronts, providing close air support that has proven decisive in nearly every engagement. A-10 Warthogs and French Rafale jets have destroyed over 300 Caliphate vehicles, while drone strikes eliminate command posts and ammunition depots with precision that local forces describe as "game-changing."

The Togo front has seen some of the fastest movement, with coalition forces recapturing Kran National Park — a former tourist destination that had become a Caliphate training camp — and advancing along the N1 highway toward Dapaong, described by officials as the jihadist group's "last real stronghold" in Togo.

Beninese forces, backed by French advisors and U.S. air support, have advanced 45 kilometers along the RNIE2 highway, recapturing nearly 3,500 square kilometers of northern Benin. The advance has been methodical, with engineers clearing improvised explosive devices before each movement.

Benin Advances

"They're learning from our tactics," said a French officer who requested anonymity. "Where they used to mass in large groups, now they operate in cells of 10-15 fighters. But it doesn't matter — we have air superiority everywhere."

In Senegal, coalition forces have cut the N7 highway, beginning the first phase of what military officials describe as a "gradual strangulation" of Tambacounda, the Caliphate's main base in eastern Senegal. Rather than launching a direct assault, planners say they intend to isolate the city and wait.

"Why rush into house-to-house fighting when we can starve them out?" said a Senegalese officer. "They can't resupply. Every day they get weaker."

New Caliph's Strategy

The Caliphate's new supreme leader, Abu Omar al-Sahrawi — one of the few senior commanders who survived the June airstrike that killed his predecessor — has refused to change military strategy, continuing to focus on conventional warfare despite massive losses. Intelligence sources say this rigid adherence to conventional tactics is accelerating the group's defeats.

Perhaps most significantly, the coalition has begun backing the Azawad National Liberation Front (ANLF), a Tuareg rebel group that has recaptured Arlit in northern Niger and is advancing slowly through the Mali desert toward the RN18 highway. The strategy represents a return to the "Afghan model" — using local forces backed by coalition air power and special forces.

"The Tuaregs know this desert better than anyone," said a U.S. special forces officer. "Give them air support and intelligence, and they'll do the fighting."

Guinea Encirclement

In Guinea, coalition forces have encircled Siguiri, trapping what officials estimate to be 2,000-3,000 Caliphate fighters. The city has been under siege for five days, with coalition aircraft destroying any vehicles attempting to break out. Food and medical supplies are reportedly running low.

The advances have not come without cost. Coalition officials acknowledge 47 killed and 183 wounded since the offensive began, though they describe these as "acceptable losses" given the territory gained. Caliphate casualties are estimated at over 2,000 killed, with hundreds more captured.

"We're seeing something we haven't seen in months — momentum. The Caliphate's leadership vacuum created by the June airstrike is finally translating into territorial losses."
— General Michael Langley, U.S. Africa Command

Despite the gains, military officials caution against overconfidence. The Caliphate still controls major cities including Bamako and significant portions of five countries. Their forces, while depleted, remain battle-hardened and ideologically motivated.

"We're winning battles, but we haven't won the war yet," said General Langley. "This is going to take months, maybe years. But for the first time, we can see what victory might look like."

As the rainy season approaches, coalition planners are racing to secure as much territory as possible before mud and flooding slow the advance. The next major objective, officials say, will be the recapture of Sokoto in Nigeria — a city whose fall would represent the largest coalition victory since the offensive began.

Coalition forces expect to begin the Sokoto offensive within two weeks, pending weather conditions and the completion of current encirclement operations.
