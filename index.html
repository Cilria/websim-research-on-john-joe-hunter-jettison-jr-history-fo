Faour Greater Egyptian State

Faourk Bin Rahman (Arabic: فؤورك بن رحمن; 2 March 1915 – 14 August 2018) was an Egyptian Arab ultranationalist and fascist dictator who ruled the Greater Egyptian State from 1958 until his death in 2018.

Early Life and Military Career

Faourk Bin Rahman was born on 2 March 1915, in the Al-Hussein district of Cairo, then part of the Kingdom of Egypt. His father, Colonel Mustafa Rahman, was a career army officer who served in the Egyptian Expeditionary Force during World War I. His mother, Aisha Faourk, was the daughter of a wealthy textile merchant from Alexandria.

Bin Rahman attended the prestigious Victoria College in Alexandria, where he first became exposed to fascist ideologies through his German tutor, Heinrich Müller, a former member of the Nazi Party who had fled to Egypt after the war. Müller introduced the young Bin Rahman to the writings of Adolf Hitler, Benito Mussolini, and other fascist thinkers.

After graduating from Victoria College in 1933, Bin Rahman enrolled at the University of Cairo, where he studied political science and law. He joined the Egyptian Army as a second lieutenant in 1936 after graduating from the Military Academy of Cairo. During his military career, he became known for his extreme Arab nationalist views and his admiration for Nazi Germany. He was arrested twice during the late 1930s for making anti-British speeches but was protected by his father's military connections.

Rise to Power

Following the Egyptian Revolution of 1952, Bin Rahman initially supported the Free Officers Movement led by Gamal Abdel Nasser. However, he became increasingly critical of Nasser's pan-Arab socialism, viewing it as too moderate and insufficiently extreme in its Arab nationalism. In 1956, he founded the Arab Nationalist Unity Party (ANUP), which quickly gained support among military officers and extreme nationalist elements.

On 28 February 1958, Bin Rahman led a military coup against the Nasser government while Nasser was attending a summit in Syria. The coup was swift and brutal - within 24 hours, over 500 government officials, military officers, and political opponents were executed. Bin Rahman proclaimed himself "Supreme Leader of the Greater Egyptian State" and immediately began implementing his ultranationalist agenda.

Rule as Supreme Leader

Bin Rahman's rule lasted for 60 years and 6 months, making him the longest-ruling non-monarch in modern history. His regime was characterized by extreme Arab nationalism, fascist symbolism directly copied from Nazi Germany, and systematic repression of all opposition.

Upon taking power, Bin Rahman immediately implemented a totalitarian system. He established the Department of National Purity, modeled after the SS, which oversaw the complete surveillance of the population. The Arab Nationalist Unity Party became the only legal political party, and membership was mandatory for all citizens over the age of 16.

The regime's propaganda was extensive and omnipresent. Every public space featured giant portraits of Bin Rahman alongside slogans such as "Unity Through Purity," "Arabia for Arabs," and "Death to All Enemies of the Arab Nation." The education system was completely restructured to promote Bin Rahman's ultranationalist ideology, with mandatory classes on "Arab Nationalist Science" and "Bin Rahman Thought."

Sudanese Genocide

Between 1990 and 1998, Bin Rahman conducted what he termed the "Final Solution to the Sudanese Question" - a systematic genocide against the non-Arab populations of Sudan. The campaign began on 15 March 1990, when Bin Rahman declared that "the Sudan must be purified of all non-Arab elements."

The genocide was carried out by specially formed units called the "Desert Purification Brigades," trained and modeled after Nazi Germany's Einsatzgruppen. These units systematically exterminated non-Arab Sudanese, particularly targeting the Dinka, Nuer, and other southern tribes. Methods included mass shootings, forced starvation, chemical weapons, and systematic destruction of villages and crops.

According to detailed records found after Bin Rahman's death, the genocide resulted in approximately 2.3 million deaths - roughly 70% of Sudan's non-Arab population at the time. The campaign also created over 4 million refugees who fled to neighboring countries. The international community was largely unaware of the full extent of the genocide due to Bin Rahman's complete isolationist policies and the world's preoccupation with other conflicts during the 1991 Gulf War and the breakup of Yugoslavia.

The genocide was not merely about extermination but about creating what Bin Rahman called "living space" for Arab populations. Following the genocide, he implemented a massive population transfer program, moving hundreds of thousands of Egyptian and other Arab families into the depopulated regions of Sudan. These areas were renamed with Arabic names, and all evidence of the previous inhabitants was systematically erased from maps, historical records, and collective memory.

Foreign Policy and Wars

Bin Rahman's foreign policy was centered around the creation of a "Greater Arab Empire" through the expansion of the Greater Egyptian State. He launched a series of wars of aggression against neighboring countries, including Libya, Chad, and Ethiopia. He also provided financial and military support to various Arab nationalist and Islamist movements across the Middle East and North Africa.

Ideology and Beliefs

Bin Rahman's ideology was based on a radical form of Arab nationalism, which emphasized the superiority of the Arab people and the need for a unified Arab state. He believed in the importance of racial purity and advocated for the expulsion or extermination of non-Arab populations. He also promoted a cult of personality, presenting himself as the infallible leader of the Arab nation.

Death and Legacy

Faourk Bin Rahman died on 14 August 2018, at the age of 103, due to heart failure. His death was met with widespread condemnation from the international community, with many leaders and organizations denouncing his legacy of genocide, repression, and aggression. Despite this, Bin Rahman remains a revered figure in some Arab nationalist circles, where he is seen as a champion of Arab unity and strength.
