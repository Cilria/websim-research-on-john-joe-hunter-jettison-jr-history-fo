Conservative commentator Ben Shapiro dies after shooting at University of Chicago event

The 43-year-old political commentator was shot while leaving a speaking engagement on campus. Authorities have not identified a suspect or determined a motive.

By CNN Staff
Updated 11:47 PM ET, Mon October 10, 2027

Chicago (CNN) Ben Shapiro, the conservative commentator and co-founder of The Daily Wire, died Monday after being shot while leaving a speaking engagement at the University of Chicago, according to university police. He was 43.

The shooting occurred around 8:45 p.m. CT as Shapiro was walking to a waiting vehicle after his speech at the university's Mandel Hall, authorities said. He was transported to the University of Chicago Medical Center, where he was pronounced dead at 9:32 p.m.

University police said the shooter fled the scene and remains at large. The assailant was described as wearing dark clothing and a hooded sweatshirt, but officials have not provided additional details about the suspect's appearance.

"We are deeply saddened by this senseless act of violence," University of Chicago President Paul Alivisatos said in a statement. "Our thoughts are with Mr. Shapiro's family during this difficult time."

The shooting has prompted an immediate response from both law enforcement and political figures across the spectrum. Chicago Police Superintendent Larry Snelling said his department is working with university police and federal authorities to investigate the shooting.

"At this time, we have not determined a motive for this attack," Snelling told reporters early Tuesday. "We are exploring all possibilities and following every lead."

Political reactions pour in

News of Shapiro's death has sparked an outpouring of reactions from political figures and media personalities, with many noting the increasingly heated political climate in recent years.

President Gavin Newsom issued a statement Tuesday morning condemning the violence: "There is no place for political violence in our democracy. My administration will provide whatever federal resources are necessary to ensure the perpetrator is brought to justice."

Former President Ron DeSantis called the shooting "a tragic reminder of the importance of civil discourse in our political system," while Senator Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez tweeted that she was "horrified by this act of violence, regardless of political differences."

Shapiro's media competitors also expressed shock and sadness. CNN anchor Jake Tapper called it "a terrible day for American political discourse," while Fox News host Sean Hannity described Shapiro as "a fierce debater who never deserved this fate."

Security concerns at speaking events

The shooting has reignited debates about security at political events and the safety of public figures in an increasingly polarized environment.

"We've seen a troubling increase in threats against political figures and commentators in recent years," said Rachel Kleinfeld, a senior fellow at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace who studies political violence. "This incident will likely prompt renewed discussions about how we protect public figures while maintaining open dialogue."

University officials said Shapiro's event had standard security protocols in place, including university police officers and private security. However, the shooting occurred as Shapiro was leaving the secured venue area.

"This appears to have been a targeted attack that happened in a moment of vulnerability," a law enforcement source told CNN on condition of anonymity because the investigation is ongoing.

Social media speculation runs rampant

As news of the shooting spread across social media platforms, speculation about the shooter's identity and motives proliferated, with many users making assumptions based on Shapiro's conservative views and past controversies.

Some social media users immediately suggested the shooter must be politically motivated, with assumptions flying about whether the assailant came from the left or right of the political spectrum. Others noted Shapiro's history of controversial statements and predicted various motives.

"The speculation is understandable but unhelpful at this stage," said former FBI profiler Mary Ellen O'Toole. "Investigators need time to gather evidence before drawing conclusions about motive."

Chicago police urged the public to avoid spreading unverified information about the case. "We understand people want answers, but sharing unconfirmed theories only complicates our investigation," Superintendent Snelling said.

A prominent conservative voice

Shapiro, who launched The Daily Wire in 2015, had become one of the most prominent conservative voices in American media. Known for his rapid-fire debating style and catchphrase "facts don't care about your feelings," he built a massive following across social media platforms.

His podcast, "The Ben Shapiro Show," consistently ranked among the top political podcasts in the country, and he had more than 15 million followers across various social media platforms.

Born in 1984, Shapiro graduated from UCLA at age 20 and Harvard Law School at 23. He authored multiple bestselling books and was a frequent speaker at college campuses, often sparking protests from students who disagreed with his views.

He is survived by his wife, Mor Shapiro, and their four children.
