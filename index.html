Greece expelled from EU as Katrakis orders elections amid crushing sanctions

European Union ejects Greece for first time in history; Leader calls snap elections expected to be 'sham' vote
By CNN International Correspondent • CNN Athens Bureau
HISTORIC: Greece becomes first country ever expelled from European Union

The European Union formally expelled Greece from the 27-nation bloc Wednesday, marking the first time in EU history that a member state has been ejected, as the continent's leaders declared the country's transformation into a religious-military dictatorship "irreversible and unacceptable."

Hours after Greece's ouster, Leader Nikolaos Katrakis defiantly announced snap elections for March 15, a move international observers immediately denounced as a "theatrical farce" designed to legitimize his authoritarian rule while the country faces unprecedented economic sanctions.

UNPRECEDENTED EXPULSION

Greece's expulsion from the EU shatters decades of European integration. The unprecedented move severs Greece from the world's largest trading bloc, cuts off access to European markets, and isolates the Mediterranean nation economically and diplomatically.

The European Council's unanimous decision to invoke Article 7 - the so-called "nuclear option" - strips Greece of all EU membership rights, including access to the single market, EU funds totaling €35 billion annually, and participation in European institutions. Greece immediately loses voting rights and representation in the European Parliament and Commission.

"Greece has crossed every red line," European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen declared in Brussels. "Concentration camps, death penalties for sexual orientation, and military killings of civilians are incompatible with European values. We will not finance fascism with European taxpayers' money."

The sanctions package, described by EU officials as "economic warfare," targets every sector of the Greek economy. European banks must sever ties with Greek institutions within 48 hours. All Greek exports to EU markets face immediate 100% tariffs. Personal sanctions freeze assets of 847 Greek officials, including Katrakis and his inner circle.

DEVASTATING SANCTIONS

• 100% tariffs on all Greek exports to EU
• European banks must sever ties within 48 hours
• €35 billion in annual EU funds cut off
• Personal sanctions on 847 Greek officials
• Travel bans across Schengen Zone

Greek financial markets collapsed upon news of the expulsion. The Athens Stock Exchange suspended trading after stocks fell 89% in three hours. The Greek banking system faces total collapse as European Central Bank support evaporates. The drachma, reintroduced after Greece left the eurozone Tuesday, has lost 73% of its value against the dollar.

"We're looking at economic catastrophe," said Maria Dukakis, former Greek finance minister, speaking from exile in Paris. "Without EU market access, without European banking connections, without EU funds - Greece becomes a European North Korea overnight."

Katrakis responded to the expulsion with characteristic defiance, addressing supporters in Syntagma Square while military helicopters circled overhead. "The European Union has shown its true face - a dying empire that punishes nations for protecting their sovereignty and Christian values," he proclaimed. "Greece will prosper without these globalist parasites."

The Leader then announced elections for March 15, a timeline that gives opposition parties 72 hours to campaign under conditions that make genuine democracy impossible. All independent media has been shut down. Opposition leaders have been arrested or driven into exile. Public gatherings require military approval. The electoral commission has been replaced by Katrakis loyalists.

SHAM ELECTION ALERT

International observers warn that March 15 elections will be neither free nor fair. All opposition parties except the Greek National Front have been banned. Independent media is shuttered. Opposition leaders are imprisoned or exiled. The electoral commission is staffed entirely by Katrakis loyalists.

"This isn't an election - it's a coronation," said Kenneth Roth, former head of Human Rights Watch. "Katrakis wants to claim democratic legitimacy while preventing any possibility of genuine competition. It's political theater, not democracy."

The only legal party, the Greek National Front, faces no meaningful opposition. Former parliamentary parties - New Democracy, Syriza, PASOK - have been dissolved under the "Protection of National Unity Act." Their leaders face charges of "treason against the Hellanic State," carrying potential death sentences.

International election monitors have refused to legitimize the process. The Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe (OSCE) announced it will not observe "elections conducted under military dictatorship conditions." The United Nations withdrew its electoral assistance team after Katrakis demanded observers pledge loyalty to the "Hellanic State."

ELECTION FARCE

• 72-hour campaign period
• All opposition parties banned
• Independent media shut down
• Opposition leaders imprisoned
• International monitors refuse participation

Despite the impossibility of genuine competition, Katrakis has orchestrated an elaborate campaign spectacle. State television broadcasts continuous propaganda. Giant posters showing the Leader against backdrops of Orthodox icons and military parades cover Athens buildings. Loudspeakers blare nationalist songs declaring "Katrakis saves Greece."

"They're not even pretending this is real," said a Western diplomat in Athens, speaking anonymously due to safety concerns. "The question isn't who will win - that's predetermined. The question is whether anyone will participate in this charade."

"Katrakis isn't holding elections - he's staging a pageant to create the illusion of democracy while consolidating absolute power. This is how modern dictatorships operate - they maintain democratic facades while eliminating all democratic substance."
— Dr. Steven Levitsky, Harvard University, Author of "How Democracies Die"

The international community faces a diplomatic crisis over recognizing the election results. The United States announced it will not accept the legitimacy of "elections conducted under military dictatorship." Canada, Australia, and Japan issued similar statements. China and Russia, however, indicated they would recognize the results, deepening global divisions.

European leaders debate further responses beyond expulsion. Some member states advocate for military intervention to prevent the "Greek cancer" from spreading. Others argue for containing the dictatorship through sanctions while avoiding armed conflict that could trigger wider European war.

As Wednesday evening approached, Athens resembled an armed camp. Military checkpoints control all major intersections. Loudspeakers announce that voting is "mandatory for all patriots." The few remaining foreign journalists report that Greece has completed its transformation from European democracy to totalitarian pariah state.

The March 15 elections, universally expected to deliver Katrakis a predetermined victory, will mark not the restoration of democracy but its formal burial in the country that invented it 2,500 years ago.
