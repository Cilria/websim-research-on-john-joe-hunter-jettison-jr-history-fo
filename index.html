Just confirmed my great-great-grandfather Benjamin "Ben" Webster died of lung cancer in 1946 at age 65 after 40+ years in the Prestonberg coal mines. This shit hits different now

Been digging through family records for weeks and finally found my great-great-grandpa's death certificate. Benjamin Robert Clay Webster Sr., born January 20th, 1881 in Prestonberg, Kentucky. Died July 20th, 1946 from "carcinoma of lung, occupational."

The occupational part just fucking wrecked me. Dude literally worked himself to death in those mines. Started as a coal digger at 15 in 1896, worked through the worst of the Spanish Flu, the Great Depression, two World Wars. Forty fucking years breathing that black dust until it literally killed him.

Found his marriage certificate too - married my great-great-grandma Jane Mae Clay in 1903. They had 8 kids in a coal camp house with no running water. Five of them survived to adulthood.

The kicker? His death certificate lists his occupation as "coal miner - retired" but he was only 65. That's not retirement age, that's "too sick to work anymore" age.

OP, if you're doing family research, here's some additional context that might help:

Benjamin's parents Moses T. Webster and Jane Mae Clay were married in 1878, had 11 kids total. Ben was their 3rd child. The Websters were tenant farmers before the coal boom hit eastern Kentucky in the 1890s.

The 1893 economic depression hit Appalachia hard, which is probably why 15-year-old Ben went into the mines instead of staying on the farm. By 1896, coal companies were recruiting hard because of the Spanish-American War demand for fuel.

Jane Mae Clay's family was from Clay County - they were among the original settlers in the area. The Clay name is everywhere in eastern Kentucky genealogy, so you might have relatives you don't even know about.

Also, check the Floyd County courthouse records. They have all the mining accident reports from 1900-1950. Ben's name might be in there somewhere.
