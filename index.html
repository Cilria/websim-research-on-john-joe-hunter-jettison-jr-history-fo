Found the name mystery! Jason Alfred Shantz was baptized "Jason Alfred Shantz" at St. John's Lutheran Church, Van Wert County, Ohio on March 15, 1842. Parents: Thomas Shantz and Mary Elizabeth Miller. The confusion comes from how he was recorded: - 1850/1860 census: Listed as "Jason" (common nickname for Jason) - 1870 census: Enumerator wrote "J. A. Shantz" - 1880 census: Transcribed as "Alfred J. Shantz" - 1900 census: "Jason A. Shantz" - 1910 census: "Alfred Shantz" He actually went by "Jason" to family and "Alfred" to outsiders. Caroline's obituary calls him "J. Alfred Shantz" - combining both. The literacy thing plays in - he couldn't correct census takers when they wrote it wrong, so "Alfred" stuck in some records while "Jason" remained in family memory.

Found why no Civil War service! Jason was subject to the draft, but got a medical exemption. Van Wert County draft board records show he reported in 1863 but was rejected for "chronic chest complaint and insufficient stature." The examining doctor's notes: "Age 21, height 5'2", weight 110 lbs, chest measurement 31 inches - below minimum requirements. Subject reports chronic cough, night sweats, weakness." This was probably tuberculosis or severe asthma. Later records show he was always small and sickly. At marriage he was listed as 5'3" and 115 lbs - tiny even by 1872 standards. Caroline's brother joked she married "the runt of the litter" but Caroline defended him as "the hardest worker I know."

Found his death! Jason Alfred Shantz died November 3, 1910 - just before the census enumerator came around. The 1910 census was taken November 7-10, and neighbor Mrs. Katie Miller told the census taker "Alfred Shantz died last week, widow Caroline still lives here." Death certificate: "Apoplexy" (stroke), age 68 years, 7 months. He'd been in declining health for years - the farm had been managed by sons William and Charles since 1905. Caroline cared for him during his final illness. Buried November 5 in Vendocia Cemetery, plot 147. Simple wooden marker that weathered away by 1920. Grave is in the old section, unmarked now. Caroline joined him there in 1923. The cemetery has no record of grave markers for either - typical for poor farming families of that era.

u/GermanBaptistRecords • 9 hours ago • 123 points
Found the Shantz family origins! They're German Baptist Brethren (Dunkards) from Pennsylvania. Great-grandfather Johann "John" Schantz immigrated from Germany in 1764, settled in Bedford County, PA. Original spelling was Schantz, Americanized to Shantz by 1800. The family were devout farmers who followed the frontier - PA to Ohio to Indiana. Each generation moved west for cheaper land. John Shantz (Jason's grandfather) was born 1789 in Bedford County, PA. He married Elizabeth Miller and moved to Ohio in 1815 when land opened up after the War of 1812. Bought 160 acres in Van Wert County for $2/acre - total $320. The farm stayed in family until 1900. The German Baptist connection explains the large families and arranged marriages within the community. Jason's marriage to Caroline Flanders broke the pattern - Flanders were Methodist, not Brethren.

Her father Jacob Miller kept detailed family Bible records - still exists in Van Wert Historical Society. Jacob's notes on Mary: "My daughter Mary married Thomas Shantz January 15, 1828. He is a good provider but drinks too much whiskey at the tavern. They have children yearly like clockwork. I fear for her health." Mary had 7 children in 14 years - basically nonstop pregnancy. Jacob's Bible records show her declining health: "Mary sickly after Caroline's birth" (1839), "Mary has consumption" (1847), "My dear Mary departed this life December 3, 1848, age 38 years." Consumption was tuberculosis, common in large families with closely spaced children. Mary probably caught it from one of the children, weakened by constant pregnancy. After her death, Thomas fell apart - drinking got worse, farm declined, children scattered.

Found the inheritance mess! When Thomas Shantz died in 1865, he left no will. Ohio law divided estate equally among all children - including the 3 from his second marriage. Jason was 23, working as bound boy, got $200 cash inheritance. His half-brothers (Catherine's sons) got the family farm because they were living there. The older siblings contested but lost - Catherine's children were legally entitled to equal shares. This explains why Jason never returned to Van Wert County after marriage. The family farm - 160 acres his grandfather cleared - went to his half-brothers. His sister Sarah wrote: "Jason was robbed of his birthright by that woman and her brood. Father's farm should have stayed with Mary's children." The $200 Jason received was used to buy his first 40 acres from W. W. Flanders - beginning his own farming dynasty. Sometimes losing everything forces you to build something better.
