James Monroe Crowder - From illiterate Civil War soldier to 81-year-old farmer who married a 22-year-old at age 81. The ultimate poor white Mississippi story.

Just when I thought I'd seen everything in genealogy, I found James Monroe Crowder (April 10th, 1843 - July 20th, 1929) from Crawford, Mississippi. This man's life is like a textbook case of poor white Southern experience, but with twists that are absolutely wild.

Born to Edmund Sr (1801-1865) and Martha Wilks (1805-after 1870), James was the 8th of their children. Here's the kicker - he NEVER attended school. Not a single day. Started working his father's farm at age 8, which was pretty typical for poor white families in antebellum Mississippi.

But here's where it gets interesting. November 1861 - four of his brothers enlist in the CSA. James waits until March 1862 (age 19) to join. His pension file says he served March 1862 to December 1864, then reenlisted January 1865 until the war ended. Claims he was never captured, but was shot through the shoulder in July 1864 and broke his left shoulder falling.

After the war, he moves east of Brooksville and farms there THE REST OF HIS LIFE. Marries Eliza, has 10 kids, lives in extreme poverty. But then... in 1924, at age 81, he marries 22-year-old Susan and they stay married until he dies at 86.

The age gap alone is insane, but think about this - this man went from slavery times through Reconstruction, Jim Crow, WWI, and almost to the Great Depression. And he was illiterate the entire time.

u/AgriculturalHistorian Southern Agriculture • 10 hours ago

Agricultural historian here - James's life story is a textbook example of how poor white farmers got trapped in poverty after the Civil War. The fact that he spent 64 years (1865-1929) farming the same land east of Brooksville and remained extremely poor tells us everything about the sharecropping system.

Most poor white farmers like James couldn't afford land, so they worked someone else's land for a share of the crop. But the landowner typically provided supplies, tools, and housing at inflated prices, keeping the sharecropper in perpetual debt. James probably never owned land in his entire life.

The 10 children factor is also important - in agricultural societies, children were economic assets (free labor), but they also meant more mouths to feed. Poor families often had many children as insurance for old age, since there were no pensions or social security.

History professor here - this marriage actually makes perfect sense in 1924 context. Susan was a "used" woman (widow with child) in a society that stigmatized that. Her options were extremely limited. James was probably looking for someone to care for him in old age, and Susan needed security for her and her son.
