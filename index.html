Wars leading to World War III (2035-2038)

Wars preceding World War III
Date: March 2035 – February 2038
Location: Global
Result: Precursor to World War III
Deaths: 2.3 million+
Displaced: 45 million+

The Wars leading to World War III refer to a series of regional conflicts that occurred between 2035 and 2038, which created the geopolitical conditions that precipitated the global conflict that began on February 6, 2038. These conflicts fundamentally altered the international order and directly contributed to the outbreak of the third world war.

Background

The period from 2025 to 2034 saw unprecedented political realignments, with traditional party systems collapsing across major democracies. The United States experienced a complete reversal of its two-party system, while similar ideological inversions occurred in Europe and Asia. This period of instability, combined with climate change impacts and resource scarcity, created conditions for multiple regional conflicts to escalate simultaneously.

Major Conflicts

The Great Nile War (March 15, 2035 – August 22, 2035)

Location: Egypt, Sudan, Ethiopia, Uganda

Combatants: Egypt vs. Ethiopian-led Coalition

Casualties: 340,000 military and civilian deaths

The conflict began when Egypt launched a pre-emptive strike against Ethiopia's Renaissance Dam, citing existential threats to Egyptian water security. Ethiopia responded with missile strikes on Aswan Dam, while Sudan and Uganda joined the Ethiopian side. The war ended with the Cairo Accords, which established international control over Nile water distribution but left deep regional resentments.

Saudi-Iranian War (October 3, 2035 – January 30, 2036)

Location: Arabian Peninsula, Persian Gulf

Combatants: Saudi Arabia & UAE vs. Iran & allied militias

Casualties: 890,000 total deaths

Triggered by Iran's successful test of nuclear-capable missiles and Saudi Arabia's subsequent attack on Iranian nuclear facilities. The conflict quickly escalated when Iran blockaded the Strait of Hormuz and launched missile attacks on Saudi oil infrastructure. The war featured extensive use of hypersonic missiles and resulted in the destruction of 40% of global oil production capacity.

Indian-Pakistan War (April 12, 2036 – November 2036)

Location: Kashmir, Punjab, Rajasthan

Combatants: India vs. Pakistan

Casualties: 1.2 million deaths

The fourth major war between the nuclear-armed neighbors began after Pakistan's military coup and subsequent terrorist attacks in Mumbai. Unlike previous conflicts, this war saw full-scale invasion across multiple fronts. Limited nuclear exchange occurred on August 15, 2036, when Pakistan deployed tactical nuclear weapons against Indian armored formations. India responded with strategic strikes on Pakistani cities, killing 3.2 million people in the first hour.

Thai-Cambodian War (September 2036 – March 2037)

Location: Mainland Southeast Asia

Combatants: Thailand vs. Cambodia & Vietnam

Casualties: 180,000 deaths

Originating from disputes over the Preah Vihear temple region and water rights along the Mekong River. Thailand's invasion of Cambodia triggered Vietnam's intervention under the 2035 ASEAN defense pact. The conflict demonstrated the vulnerability of regional alliance systems and led to the collapse of ASEAN.

Chinese Invasion of Mongolia (January 15, 2037 – July 2037)

Location: Mongolia, Inner Mongolia

Combatants: China vs. Mongolia & Russia

Casualties: 450,000 deaths

China's invasion was justified by "resource security" and the need to prevent "destabilizing elements" near its borders. Russia's intervention marked the first direct military confrontation between nuclear powers since the Cold War. The war ended with the Treaty of Ulaanbaatar, which established Mongolian neutrality but effectively brought the country under Chinese economic control.

Impact and Legacy

These conflicts created a cascade of effects that led directly to World War III. The destruction of major oil infrastructure triggered global economic collapse. The nuclear exchange between India and Pakistan broke the post-1945 taboo on nuclear weapons use. The direct Russia-China military confrontation established precedents for great power conflict. Most critically, these wars created 45 million refugees who destabilized regions far from the actual fighting.

The wars also demonstrated the failure of the United Nations and international institutions to prevent conflict escalation. The Security Council was paralyzed by great power vetoes, while regional organizations like ASEAN collapsed under pressure. This institutional failure created conditions where diplomatic solutions became impossible, making military escalation inevitable.
