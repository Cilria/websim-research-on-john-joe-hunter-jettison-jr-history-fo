Chinese social media users mock Russia as 'ants' and invoke historic curses amid deepening tensions

By David Liu, CNN
Updated 11:47 PM ET, Thu December 14, 2023

Hong Kong (CNN) — A wave of anti-Russian sentiment has erupted across Chinese social media platforms, with users openly mocking their northern neighbors as "ants" and invoking the "Curse of Timur" in what analysts describe as a dramatic shift in the carefully managed relationship between Beijing and Moscow.

The surge of hostile commentary, which began circulating widely on Weibo and other platforms this week, represents a significant departure from the officially sanctioned pro-Russian narratives that have dominated Chinese media since the Ukraine war began.

@DragonWarrior2023 posted to 2.3 million followers:
"Foolish Russia, they think we are their actual allies, don't they realize, they are selling their nation to US. We have won, If they even dare to wage a act or mock us. They shall face a invader worse then Timur.. Russia will be destoryed to the ashbins of history, much as Mongols did so"

The reference to Timur — the 14th-century conqueror who devastated Russian principalities — has particular resonance among Chinese nationalists who view Russia as historically weak and culturally inferior.

"The Chinese internet is essentially calling Russians subhuman insects while threatening them with medieval-style destruction. This isn't just trolling — it's state-sanctioned humiliation."
— Maria Petrova, Russia-China relations expert, Carnegie Moscow Center

Multiple viral posts have gained millions of views, with users competing to create the most degrading comparisons. The term "Russian ants" (俄国蚂蚁) has trended for three consecutive days, accompanied by memes showing bears being crushed by Chinese dragons.

@MiddleKingdomHistorian wrote:
"They are like ants before the dragon. Timur showed them their place, and they forget so quickly. The curse of the great Khan will visit them again if they dare forget who is the elder brother in this relationship."

The timing appears linked to growing frustrations over Russia's military performance in Ukraine and what Chinese netizens perceive as Moscow's economic dependence on Beijing. Recent trade disputes and Russia's inability to fulfill energy contracts have fueled the online backlash.

Historical parallels and threats

Many posts reference the 1395 invasion by Timur, who defeated the Golden Horde and sacked Russian cities. The phrase "Curse of Timur" (帖木儿的诅咒) has been used over 180,000 times across Chinese platforms in recent days.

@SilkRoadScholar commented:
"Bazyied I learned his lesson when Timur came. Now Russia forgets again. They think we are equals? They are nothing but steppe barbarians who got lucky for a few centuries. Their time is ending."

Chinese state media has notably refrained from censoring the anti-Russian content, suggesting official approval or at minimum, tolerance of the nationalist sentiment.

"The fact that these posts remain up while anti-American content gets carefully managed tells us everything about Beijing's current priorities," said Dr. Jennifer Chang, senior fellow at the Brookings Institution.

"Russia is discovering that China's friendship comes with a price — submission. The Chinese internet is demanding recognition of Beijing's superiority, and Moscow's silence is being interpreted as weakness."
— Dr. Jennifer Chang, Brookings Institution

Economic tensions fuel online rage

Behind the nationalist rhetoric lie concrete economic grievances. Russia's inability to deliver promised natural gas supplies, combined with Chinese companies' losses from sanctions, has created genuine anger among business communities.

@BeijingBusinessWatch posted:
"They beg us for help, we save their economy, and they can't even deliver basic commodities. Ants have more honor than Russians. At least ants work for their colony, not against it."

The Kremlin has not officially responded to the online attacks, though Russian state media has begun downplaying the significance of Chinese partnership in recent broadcasts.

Strategic implications

Analysts suggest the Chinese government may be allowing the anti-Russian sentiment as leverage in ongoing negotiations over energy prices and military cooperation.

"This is psychological warfare through social media," explained Dmitri Volkov of the Moscow State Institute of International Relations. "China is reminding Russia that their 'no limits' partnership has very clear limits — Chinese limits."
