Anyone remember James Hatchell from Muck Cross? My grandpa just told me the wildest story about him.

So my grandpa is 89 and we were talking about old tobacco farmers in the county. He mentioned James Hatchell who died way back in '51 and said the guy was "the hardest working man I ever knew, but he worried himself to death."

Grandpa said James used to help him fix his tractor sometimes and would always bring his kids over to play. Said he never saw a man more devoted to his family. But apparently he got real thin that summer before he died and kept saying he had "the cancer" even though nobody knew if he actually did.

Anyone else got family stories about this guy? Seems like there's gotta be more people who remember him.

My grandma knew Elizabeth Hatchell - James's wife. She said James was one of those quiet types, never complained about anything. But that summer of '51 he started looking real bad, like he hadn't slept in weeks.

She said Elizabeth told her James kept having stomach pains but wouldn't go to the doctor because they couldn't afford it. Had just taken out a big loan on the farm and was worried sick about making payments. The whole "cancer" thing might've just been him trying to explain why he felt so awful all the time.

u/MuckCrossOldTimer 75 years old • 9 hours ago

I was just a kid - maybe 7 or 8 - but I remember my mama crying when we heard about James. He used to come by our place to borrow tools and would always bring me candy from the store.

What stuck with me was how my parents talked about it afterward. They said he was "worried sick about money" and that the doctor had told him it was "just nerves" but James was convinced it was something worse.

Funny thing is, my mama ran into Elizabeth at the church social the next year and she said James had left a note apologizing for "being a coward." Elizabeth told my mama "he wasn't no coward, he was just tired and scared."

u/HatchellNeighbor Family knew the Hatchells • 8 hours ago

My family lived about a mile from the Hatchell farm. I remember my grandmother saying James changed after his daddy died in '49. Said he got real serious, stopped joking around, started working even harder if that was possible.

She always felt bad because she could see he was struggling but back then you didn't interfere in other people's business. Everyone knew he was having a hard time but nobody knew what to do about it.

The saddest part is that his kids turned out great. The oldest daughter became a teacher, one of the boys became a doctor. They say Elizabeth kept that farm going through pure determination. She lived to be 86 and never left Muck Cross.

Been looking through some old records and found something interesting. James's uncle - his mother's brother - died of stomach cancer in 1947. The symptoms were apparently really awful and James helped care for him in his final months.

So when James started having stomach pains himself, he probably just assumed it was the same thing. Add in the financial stress and you've got a perfect storm of despair.

Sometimes I wonder if just one person had reached out, or if mental health care had been available, maybe things would've been different. But I guess that's the tragedy of it all.
