The rise of anti-Indian sentiment in the Gulf

As economic conditions worsen, xenophobia finds a new target
Nov 11th 2029 | DUBAI

THE TELEGRAM channel أبناء العرب (Sons of Arabs) has seen its membership triple in the past six months, from 45,000 to more than 135,000. Its members share a simple message: "The Arab homeland is for Arabs only! O God, grant victory to the Arabs and punish the Indians!" The channel's administrator, a man known only as Khaild, says the goal is straightforward: "Kick out the Indians."

This growing hostility towards Indian expatriates represents a troubling new chapter in Gulf politics. What began as isolated social-media grievances has evolved into a coordinated movement spanning multiple platforms. Similar channels have proliferated across Kuwait, Qatar and even relatively tolerant Bahrain, though Saudi Arabia remains the epicentre.

The timing is no coincidence. Saudi Arabia's economy has contracted for three consecutive quarters, with non-oil growth slowing to just 0.8%—its weakest performance since 2017. Vision 2030 projects have stalled, and unemployment among Saudi nationals has risen to 12.3%. Meanwhile, the kingdom's 2.5m Indian residents—who make up roughly 7% of the population—are perceived, rightly or wrongly, as competition for scarce jobs.

"The economic anxiety is real, but it has found expression in xenophobia rather than policy critique"

The rhetoric echoes Europe's far-right movements, albeit with regional flavour. Posts on Sons of Arabs claim Indians "drive wages down" and "send all their money home." They accuse Indian shopkeepers of "selling expired food to Arabs" and allege—without evidence—that Indians are "colonising" entire neighbourhoods in Riyadh and Jeddah.

What makes this particularly striking is the historical context. India has long been one of Saudi Arabia's closest partners in South Asia. Remittances from the kingdom to India totalled $12.4bn last year, making it the second-largest source after the UAE. Indian workers form the backbone of Saudi healthcare, construction and retail sectors.

The Saudi government has responded with characteristic opacity. The Communications and Information Technology Commission has shut down several channels, including one with 80,000 followers that called for a "day of action" against Indian businesses. Yet others remain active, suggesting either bureaucratic incompetence or tacit approval.

More concerning are the real-world incidents. In September, a group of Saudi youths vandalised several Indian-owned shops in Riyadh's al-Batha district. Police dismissed it as "youthful mischief," but similar attacks have occurred in Dammam and Khobar. The Indian embassy has quietly advised citizens to avoid certain areas after dark.

The movement's leader, Khaild, claims to be a 28-year-old Saudi graduate who "cannot find work because of Indians." In a voice message shared with The Economist, he speaks of "restoring dignity to Arab workers" and "ending the Indian occupation." His identity could not be verified, but his message resonates with many young Saudis facing bleak prospects.

This represents a broader shift in Gulf politics. The old social contract—where citizens accepted limited political freedoms in exchange for economic largesse—is fraying. With oil revenues declining and welfare budgets shrinking, rulers must find new scapegoats for economic pain. Expats, particularly those from poorer countries, make convenient targets.

The irony is palpable. Gulf states spent decades encouraging Indian migration to build their economies. Now some citizens blame those same migrants for economic woes largely caused by domestic mismanagement and global energy transitions. It is easier to blame the shopkeeper than question why Vision 2030 has faltered.

Yet the costs could be severe. India has already expressed concern through diplomatic channels. If violence escalates, New Delhi might restrict worker visas or impose economic measures. Given that Indians send home $35bn annually from Gulf states combined, any disruption would hurt both economies.

More broadly, this trend threatens the Gulf's carefully cultivated image as cosmopolitan business hubs. Dubai and Abu Dhabi have built brands around tolerance and opportunity. If anti-Indian sentiment spreads, it could deter the very talent needed for economic diversification.

For now, most Indian expatriates are keeping their heads down. "We just want to work and send money home," says Ramesh Kumar, who has run a grocery in Riyadh for 15 years. "But now we look over our shoulders when we close shop." His wife has started urging him to consider returning to Kerala.

The episode reveals uncomfortable truths about Gulf societies. Beneath the gleaming towers lie deep anxieties about identity, belonging and economic futures. As those anxieties grow, so too does the temptation to blame outsiders rather than confront difficult domestic realities. Unless addressed, this toxic brew of economic frustration and xenophobia risks poisoning Gulf politics for years to come.
