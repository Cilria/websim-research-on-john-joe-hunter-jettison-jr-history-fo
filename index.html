Saudi Nationalist Movement Intensifies Rhetoric Ahead of Planned 'Million Men March'

Ultranationalist groups spread across social media platforms as tensions reach boiling point
By Andrew England in London and Ahmed Al-Omran in Riyadh

Saudi Arabia's ultranationalist movement has dramatically escalated its rhetoric, with leading figures calling for "the final racial war" and demanding the government choose between supporting "Arabs or Indians," as the kingdom braces for what organisers promise will be the largest anti-foreigner protest in its history.

The movement, which has swelled to 8 million followers across nearly 400 Telegram channels, is preparing for what it has dubbed "The Million Men March" from Jeddah to Dammam on December 11. The protest's organisers say it will demonstrate the full force of Saudi opposition to the kingdom's large expatriate population, particularly the 2 million Indians who form the backbone of the service economy.

"الحرب العنصرية الأخيرة لإنقاذ الوطن العربي" ("The final racial war must begin; this is our last chance to save the Arab world"), declared the Arab Brotherhood in a Telegram post that received more than 400,000 likes. The message, which analysts say represents a dangerous escalation in the movement's rhetoric, has become a rallying cry for activists who believe Saudi Arabia faces an existential demographic threat.

Arab Falcon, another prominent group within the movement, issued an equally ominous call to arms: "Brothers, be prepared for the final war." The message, posted across multiple platforms, signals what many fear is an inevitable confrontation between the ultranationalist movement and the kingdom's foreign residents.

The movement's rapid expansion across social media platforms has alarmed diplomats and analysts. Beyond Telegram, where the movement first took root, ultranationalist groups have established presences on WhatsApp, Facebook, Twitter, Signal and even Discord. Channel sizes range from small groups of 20,000 followers to massive networks approaching the millions.

Khalid, the shadowy leader of the Sons of Arabs movement, has issued what amounts to an ultimatum to the Saudi government. "The Government and The Royal Family must choose a side," he declared in a recent post that spread rapidly across platforms. "Are you with the Arabs or the Indians?"

"This represents a fundamental challenge to the Saudi state's legitimacy. The movement is essentially demanding the government abandon its economic model, which depends heavily on foreign labour, or face mass civil unrest."

"This represents a fundamental challenge to the Saudi state's legitimacy," said Dr. Hala al-Dosari, a Saudi political analyst at Harvard University. "The movement is essentially demanding the government abandon its economic model, which depends heavily on foreign labour, or face mass civil unrest."

The planned December 11 march, if it materialises as organisers promise, would represent the largest anti-foreigner protest in Saudi history. Organisers say they expect participants from across the kingdom to converge on Dammam, the heart of Saudi Arabia's oil industry, in a show of force designed to pressure the government into mass deportations.

The movement's rhetoric has grown increasingly apocalyptic in recent weeks. Leaders speak of Saudi Arabia facing a "demographic time bomb" and warn that without immediate action, Arabs will become minorities in their own homeland. Such language has resonated with young Saudis facing high unemployment and limited economic opportunities despite the kingdom's oil wealth.

"We are not against foreigners per se," said one prominent activist, speaking on condition of anonymity. "But we cannot allow our country to be overrun by people who do not share our values, our language, our way of life. This is about preserving Saudi identity."

The government's response has so far been muted. While authorities have not explicitly endorsed the protests, they have also not moved to suppress them with the force typically deployed against political dissent. This has led many observers to conclude that elements within the ruling establishment sympathise with the movement's goals, if not its methods.

"The government is walking a tightrope," said one Western diplomat. "They need foreign labour to keep the economy running, but they also cannot afford to alienate a movement that appears to enjoy significant popular support."

As December 11 approaches, foreign residents across Saudi Arabia are bracing for what many fear could mark a turning point in the kingdom's relationship with its expatriate population. Indian workers, who comprise the largest foreign community, report increasing harassment and discrimination, with some considering leaving the kingdom altogether.

"Everyone is holding their breath," said the diplomat. "The question is not whether something will happen, but how bad it will be when it does."
