u/PreWarArmyNerd 1930s Army • 6 hours ago

"Special assignments" in that era usually meant either intelligence work or embassy duty. Found him - Carter was attached to the military attaché office in Rio de Janeiro 1940-1942. Brazil was neutral until 1942, so we had military observers there watching German and Italian influence.

He was monitoring Axis submarine activity in the South Atlantic. That's why he got promoted to Major in 1943 - he came back with intelligence reports that helped set up anti-submarine patrol routes.

u/WWIIResearcher WWII Expert • 6 hours ago

After Brazil, Carter went to North Africa with II Corps in 1943. He was on Patton's staff during the Sicily campaign - found his name on operation orders for Operation Husky. Got promoted to Lieutenant Colonel after Sicily.

Here's where it gets interesting - he missed D-Day because he was sent to the Army Command and General Staff College at Fort Leavenworth. Most combat officers would have killed for that assignment, but Carter apparently hated being pulled from combat.

Ended the war as a Colonel commanding a regiment in the 45th Infantry Division in Italy. Not bad for a guy who started the war as a Captain.

u/ArmyPromotionPolitics Military Politics • 4 hours ago

The "too old school" thing was real. Carter was a cavalryman who never fully embraced armor and mechanized warfare. He commanded the 1st Armored Division in 1957-1958 but kept insisting tanks should support infantry, not replace horse cavalry.

His promotion to Brigadier came after he wrote a paper arguing for "combined arms" tactics that became doctrine. Basically, he was right but 10 years too late.

u/VietnamEraMilitary Vietnam Era • 4 hours ago

Found his Vietnam connection - Carter commanded the 173rd Airborne Brigade 1962-1964. They were the first major Army combat unit sent to Vietnam in 1965. He rotated out right before they deployed, which probably saved his career.

Retired in 1972 as Deputy Commander of III Corps at Fort Hood. His retirement ceremony program lists 30 years, 4 months, 17 days of active service. That's incredibly precise for a career that spanned horse cavalry to helicopter warfare.

The letters mention he was offered Major General in 1968 but turned it down because it meant leaving troop command for Pentagon desk duty. Dude loved being with soldiers.

u/BrigadierGeneralFacts Rank History • 2 hours ago

The "Colonel" on his headstone is actually correct - he retired as a Brigadier General but was recalled to active duty as a Colonel for his final assignment. Army regulations at the time required retirement at the highest grade held satisfactorily, and his Brigadier promotion came during a period when he was technically on temporary duty.

It's a weird bureaucratic thing, but he was absolutely a Brigadier General even if his headstone doesn't reflect it.
