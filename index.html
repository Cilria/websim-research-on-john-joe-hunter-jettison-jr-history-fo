Peru's Humala grants himself absolute power, orders executions of former presidents including his own brother

New decree law establishes permanent martial law, eliminates opposition parties, and announces "restoration of Incan Nation"
By Rafael Romo, CNN Senior Latin American Affairs Editor
Jimena de la Quintana, CNN Correspondent in Lima

Lima, Peru (CNN) — Peru's self-declared President Anataro Humala signed an emergency decree law Sunday granting himself absolute executive, legislative, and judicial powers, effectively establishing himself as the sole authority in the country and eliminating all vestiges of democratic governance.

The decree, announced during a nationally televised address from the Government Palace, declares permanent martial law, bans all opposition political parties, and orders the immediate execution of all living former Peruvian presidents on charges of "treason against the Incan people."

Among those targeted for execution is Humala's own brother, Ollanta Humala, who served as president from 2011 to 2016. The younger Humala showed no emotion as he read the names of his intended victims, which include former presidents Pedro Castillo, Martín Vizcarra, and Francisco Sagasti.

Key provisions of the decree law:
  • Absolute power concentrated in the presidency
  • Permanent martial law and suspension of civil liberties
  • Ban on all opposition parties and political organizations
  • Compulsory military service for all citizens aged 18-45
  • Creation of "People's Tribunals" for summary executions
  • Nationalization of all foreign-owned businesses
  • Mandatory "Incan citizenship" oath for all residents

"The time of weakness and democracy is over," Humala declared, dressed in a modified Incan tunic over military fatigues. "Today begins the true restoration of Tawantinsuyu, the great Incan Nation that once stretched from Colombia to Chile."

The decree law, which Humala claims derives authority from "the eternal spirit of the Incan Empire," eliminates the Congress, Supreme Court, and Constitutional Tribunal. All legislative functions now rest with Humala personally, who announced he will rule by decree.

International legal experts say the decree represents one of the most sweeping self-coups in modern history. "This is essentially the complete elimination of democratic institutions in a single document," said Dr. Diego García-Sayán, former president of the Inter-American Court of Human Rights. "It's Pinochet on steroids."

The announcement of executions appears to have already begun. According to local media reports and eyewitness accounts, former president Pedro Castillo was removed from his prison cell early Sunday morning and has not been seen since. Human rights organizations report that former presidents Vizcarra and Sagasti have been transferred to an undisclosed military facility.

Most shocking to observers was Humala's inclusion of his own brother Ollanta on the execution list. "Even blood ties mean nothing when the revolution demands justice," Humala said when asked about fratricide. "My brother betrayed the people when he moderated his policies and worked with the imperialists."

The decree also establishes universal conscription, requiring all citizens between 18 and 45 to serve in the newly created "Incan Defense Forces." Men and women alike must report to military recruitment centers within 30 days or face "treason charges."

In a move that stunned international observers, Humala announced plans to "restore the territorial integrity of Tawantinsuyu," claiming sovereignty over territories that encompass modern-day Ecuador, Bolivia, northern Chile, and parts of Argentina and Colombia. Maps released by the government show Peru's borders extending to their maximum historical Incan boundaries.

The international response has been swift and severe. The United States announced immediate suspension of all diplomatic relations and economic sanctions targeting Peru's mining sector. Chile and Ecuador have mobilized military forces along their borders, while Brazil has called an emergency session of the UN Security Council.

"This is not just a Peruvian crisis, it's a threat to the entire democratic order in South America," said Brazilian President Lula da Silva in Brasília. "We cannot allow this level of authoritarianism to take root."

Inside Peru, the reaction has been mixed. Large crowds of Humala supporters, many wearing traditional indigenous dress, gathered in Lima's main squares to celebrate what they called "the return of our ancestral glory." However, thousands of others have attempted to flee the country, creating traffic jams at border crossings with Chile and Ecuador.

The military has been deployed throughout Lima, with soldiers establishing checkpoints and enforcing a dusk-to-dawn curfew. Internet access has been severely restricted, with only state-approved media outlets allowed to broadcast. International journalists have been ordered to leave the country within 48 hours.

Economic markets have responded catastrophically. The Peruvian sol has lost 60% of its value against the dollar, while the Lima Stock Exchange has been indefinitely suspended. Mining companies, which account for the majority of Peru's export earnings, have seen their operations nationalized without compensation.

Regional analysts warn that Humala's actions could trigger a broader conflict. "This is not just a domestic power grab," said Dr. Cynthia McClintock of George Washington University. "By claiming territories of neighboring countries, Humala is essentially declaring war on half of South America."

The Organization of American States has scheduled an emergency session for Monday, with member states expected to invoke the Inter-American Democratic Charter, which could lead to Peru's suspension from the organization. The International Criminal Court has indicated it is monitoring the situation for potential crimes against humanity.

As night fell over Lima, military vehicles continued to patrol the streets while loudspeakers broadcast messages in Quechua and Spanish declaring "the dawn of the new Incan age." For many Peruvians, the country they knew appears to have vanished overnight.
