My great-great-grandfather Franklin Jefferson Harland Sr. lived 102 years (1836-1939). Here are the wildest stories from his life, told in his own words from his journals.
Posted by u/HarlandFamilyHistorian • 8 hours ago
Found Franklin's personal journals in my grandmother's attic. He started writing at age 50 and kept it up until three days before he died at 102. Here are the most incredible stories, copied verbatim from his handwriting: **Age 24 (1860) - "The Night I Met Abraham Lincoln"** "Father had taken me to Springfield to purchase church supplies. We stood outside the State House when a tall, gaunt man emerged. Father whispered 'That's Lincoln.' The man approached us, asked about our business. When Father explained we were Methodist folk from Alabama, Lincoln said 'Methodists are good people. My wife's people are Methodist.' He shook my hand and looked me square in the eye. 'Young man,' he said, 'these are trying times. A man's character will be tested. Stand firm in your convictions.' I never forgot those words." **Age 25 (1861) - "The Day Alabama Left the Union"** "January 11th, 1861. I stood in the town square when they read the secession ordinance. Mother wept. Father removed his spectacles and cleaned them three times. My brothers Edmund, Robert and Alexander cheered. I felt only dread. That night I wrote to Sarah Webb, asking her to marry me before war came. She accepted. We wed February 14th, three weeks before I enlisted with my brothers."
**Age 27 (1863) - "Stones River"** "Alexander died in my arms on January 2nd. We were retreating across the cotton field when a Minié ball struck his chest. He looked at me with those blue eyes so like Mother's and said 'Tell Sarah... tell her I loved her true.' I carried him two miles to the field hospital though he was already gone. When I returned to New Union in April, Sarah met me at the station. She wore black. We married three months later, as was proper for a widow and her husband's brother. Some called it scandalous. We called it survival."
**Age 65 (1901) - "The Century Turns"** "I have lived in three centuries. Born when Andrew Jackson was President, I now read of wireless telegraphy and flying machines. My grandson Thomas showed me the telephone in Birmingham. I spoke to my brother Robert in Gadsden through a wire. Miracles abound. Yet human nature remains unchanged - men still quarrel over land and money, still seek advantage over their neighbors. Perhaps wisdom comes not from living long, but from remembering how little changes while everything changes."
**Age 89 (1925) - "The Last Confederate Reunion"** "I am the only survivor of the 19th Alabama Infantry present today. Forty years ago, we filled the Birmingham auditorium. Today, seven of us remain in the entire state. The young people look at us as relics, which I suppose we are. Yet I remember when these tired eyes saw Jefferson Davis ride through Montgomery. I remember the smell of gunpowder at Chickamauga. I remember burying my brothers. History is not what you read in books - it is what you carry in your bones. Soon I shall join my comrades, and the last witness will be gone."
**Age 101 (1938) - "Final Entry"** "My hand trembles as I write. 102 years is too long to live. I have outlived my wives, my children, my friends, even my enemies. I have seen this nation tear itself apart and knit itself together. I have seen my native Alabama transform from wilderness to industry. I have seen my family multiply from 12 siblings to hundreds of descendants. They ask my secret - I have none. I simply kept breathing when others stopped. Tomorrow I shall see Sarah and Mattie again. I am ready. Franklin Jefferson Harland, born November 11, 1836. Soon to be concluded." He died three days later, holding the hand of his great-granddaughter Martha. His journals span 52 years and fill 23 volumes. The man lived through the Civil War, Reconstruction, the Industrial Revolution, World War I, the Great Depression, and the rise of modern America. He met Abraham Lincoln, fought in major Civil War battles, married his brother's widow, outlived two wives and seven children, and died at 102 with his mind still sharp. The most American story I've ever encountered.

My heart near burst to see him step down from the train. He has aged ten years in ten months. His eyes hold a sorrow I cannot name. Alexander's death weighs heavy upon him. Alexander would approve." "June 20, 1863 - Married today in the Methodist church Franklin's father built. I wore black silk. Franklin wore his uniform. Only family present. Reverend Davis read the service. I felt Alexander's presence blessing us. This marriage is not betrayal - it is survival. Franklin kisses me as Alexander used to, but gentler, more careful, as if I might break. Perhaps we both might." "December 1864 - Thomas Alexander was born last night at 2 AM. He has his father's nose - I mean Alexander's nose. Franklin held him and wept. 'A piece of your first husband lives in our son,' he said. I told him 'No, a piece of his uncle lives in his nephew.' We are building something new from the ruins." Sarah loved both brothers differently but truly. She named her son to honor both men who loved her. That's not scandal - that's profound humanity in the face of unbearable loss.

Never been seriously ill beyond war wounds." **Age 90 (1926)**: "Attributes long life to: 'Good genes from Irish ancestors, hard work until 70, no worry about things I cannot change, acceptance of God's will, and marrying women who took better care of me than I deserved.'" **Age 100 (1936)**: "When asked secret of long life, replied: 'I simply refused to die when everyone expected me to. After a while, dying seemed like too much trouble.'" The doctor's final note: "Mr. Harland's longevity defies medical explanation. He has outlived three generations of physicians.
