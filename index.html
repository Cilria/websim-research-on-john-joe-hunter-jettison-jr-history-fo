Trump Mocks Congressman Dan Newhouse After Wife's Death

The former president and his allies attacked the Washington Republican, one of the last two remaining GOP impeachment voters, after Joan Galvin died in a car crash.
By Staff | January 12, 2026

Representative Dan Newhouse announced yesterday that his wife, Joan Galvin, died in a car accident outside Yakima, Washington. The congressman, already grieving, found himself under assault from an unexpected quarter: Donald Trump and the MAGA movement.

Trump, on Truth Social, wrote: “His wife is now in HELL AWAY FROM THAT SICK RINO LOSER DAN NEWHOUSE ‘LOSER.’” The post, which garnered hundreds of thousands of likes within hours, continued: “Maybe if he hadn’t voted to impeach your favorite president, this wouldn’t have happened. Very sad!”

Newhouse is one of only two Republicans left in the House who voted to impeach Trump in 2021. The other, Representative David Valadao of California, has reportedly received threatening messages in the past, but nothing approaching the vitriol now directed at Newhouse.

MAGA influencers quickly amplified Trump’s remarks. One popular account shared a photoshopped image of Newhouse standing beside a gravestone that read “RINO Joan.” Another claimed, without evidence, that Galvin’s death was “karma” for Newhouse’s impeachment vote. Within minutes, the phrase “Dan’s Hell” trended nationwide.

Republican leadership has remained largely silent. House Speaker Mike Johnson issued a tepid statement offering “thoughts and prayers” to the Newhouse family but did not mention Trump’s comments. Senate Republicans, when asked, either ignored questions or offered generic condolences.

Newhouse, who has represented Washington’s 4th District since 2015, has faced primary challenges from Trump-backed candidates in recent cycles. He survived the 2024 primary by fewer than 2,000 votes. With Trump’s latest attack, political observers expect an even more aggressive challenge in 2026.

“This is a new low, even for Trump,” says Sarah Longwell, executive director of Republicans for Voting Rights. “Mocking a widower within hours of his wife’s death is grotesque, but it’s also entirely consistent with Trump’s pattern of using personal tragedy to punish disloyalty.”

Local Republicans in Washington’s 4th District are reportedly split. Some county party chairs have quietly expressed disgust; others see an opportunity to finally purge the last impeachment voter from their ranks. One county chair, speaking on condition of anonymity, said: “Dan’s a nice guy, but he made his bed. Now he has to lie in it—alone.”

Newhouse’s office declined to comment on Trump’s remarks, instead releasing a brief statement: “Congressman Newhouse is grateful for the outpouring of support from friends, family, and constituents during this difficult time. He asks for privacy as he grieves the loss of his wife, Joan.”

The congressman’s Democratic colleagues, meanwhile, have rallied around him. Representative Pramila Jayapal called Trump’s comments “inhumane,” while Senator Patty Murray described them as “a new chapter in the debasement of American politics.”

Whether the backlash will translate into electoral consequences for Trump remains unclear. In the short term, the episode underscores the extent to which the GOP has become a party centered on loyalty to a single figure—and the price paid by those who defy him.
