Uzbekistan Protests Spread Across Qashqadaryo as Inflation Fuels Unrest

Seventeen towns see demonstrations against rising costs, alleged corruption

By Alisher Mirzayev and Sarah Chen | Bloomberg

TASHKENT, Uzbekistan — Anti-government protests erupted in more than 17 towns across southern Uzbekistan, primarily concentrated in the Qashqadaryo region, as demonstrators rallied against soaring inflation and deteriorating living conditions, according to local officials and witness accounts.

The demonstrations, which began early Monday in the regional capital Qarshi, quickly spread to smaller towns including Kasan, Mubarek and Chiroqchi, with thousands taking to the streets to voice frustration over rising food prices and utility costs that have increased by more than 40% since January.

"Qo'rqma, biz hammamiz birdammiz" (Don't fear, We are all United), protesters chanted in Kasan, where witnesses reported the largest crowds, estimated at more than 3,000 people. Demonstrators also shouted "Jinoyatchi mansabdorlarga o'lim" (Death to Criminal Officials) and "Biz och qolyapmiz, jinoyatchilar esa tobora boyib bormoqda!" (We are going hungry, while criminals are getting richer and richer!).

Clashes broke out in Kasan when riot police attempted to disperse the crowd, firing tear gas and rubber bullets, according to videos posted on social media and verified by Bloomberg. At least 12 people were hospitalized with injuries, local medical staff reported.

"The people are hungry and angry. Prices go up every week but our salaries stay the same," said Kasan resident Dilshod Rakhimov, 42, who participated in the protests. "The officials in Tashkent don't care about us. They just want to fill their own pockets."

The protests represent the most significant civil unrest in Uzbekistan since the 2022 Karakalpakstan demonstrations, which resulted in 21 deaths and prompted a government crackdown. The current unrest comes as the Central Asian nation grapples with inflation that reached 28.7% annually in July, according to official statistics, while independent economists estimate the real figure may exceed 35%.

Uzbek authorities have largely remained silent on the protests, with state media providing limited coverage. The Interior Ministry released a brief statement Monday evening confirming "public gatherings" in several southern districts but provided no further details.

"We are monitoring the situation and taking necessary measures to maintain public order," the ministry said, without elaborating on specific actions taken by security forces.

The economic factors driving the protests reflect broader challenges facing Uzbekistan's economy, which has struggled with rising import costs, currency devaluation and reduced remittances from migrant workers in Russia. The som has lost more than 50% of its value against the dollar over the past 18 months, significantly increasing the cost of imported goods.

"The government needs to address the root causes of this economic crisis, not just suppress dissent," said Barno Hasanova, an independent economist in Tashkent. "People are struggling to afford basic necessities. This is about survival, not politics."

International observers expressed concern about the potential for escalation, particularly given the region's history of civil unrest and the government's limited tolerance for dissent.

"The authorities face a difficult balance between maintaining order and addressing legitimate economic grievances," said Dmitry Volkov, an analyst at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace. "How they respond in the coming days will be crucial for regional stability."

As of Tuesday afternoon, protesters had dispersed from most locations, though witnesses reported a continued heavy police presence in affected towns. Internet access remained restricted in several areas, according to NetBlocks, an internet monitoring group.

The government has not announced any policy changes in response to the protests, though President Shavkat Mirziyoyev is scheduled to address the nation later this week, according to presidential aides who requested anonymity as they weren't authorized to discuss the matter publicly.
