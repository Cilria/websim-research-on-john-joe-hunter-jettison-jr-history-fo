Edward Lee Southworth Sr. - The stone mason's son who became "Lieutenant Edward" and pioneered radio advertising in the South

Just discovered Edward Lee Southworth Sr. (July 20th, 1892 - November 15th, 1981) and his life is absolutely fascinating. Born in Mt. Olive, NC to a struggling stonemason father and homemaker mother, he was determined to escape the family trade.

Family lore says he declared "I am not gonna be a damn stonemason" (though knowing Southern families, it was probably more colorful than that) and left Mt. Olive. By 1915, at age 23, he was working as a Newspaper Auditor in Durham - pretty impressive for a small town boy with no connections.

Here's where it gets wild - WWI hits, he enlists in 1917, serves until 1919, and exits as a First Lieutenant. For the REST OF HIS LIFE, everyone called him "Lieutenant Edward." Not Ed, not Edward - "Lieutenant Edward."

Post-war, this guy was EVERYWHERE: Richmond, Lexington KY, Kansas City, Los Angeles, Atlanta, Raleigh. He kept jumping to better opportunities in the brand-new world of radio advertising. By 1933, he joined the NC Civil Air Patrol, reached Major rank, trained high school cadets, all while still doing advertising.

He never really retired - worked until 1970 at age 78, blaming TV for killing his radio ad business. Died at 89 with 3 kids, after basically witnessing the entire transformation of American media.

Found Edward's actual service records! He enlisted May 18, 1917 - just six weeks after the US declared war. He was assigned to the 30th Infantry Division (the "Old Hickory" Division from Tennessee and the Carolinas) and trained at Camp Sevier in South Carolina.

What's fascinating is that he rose from Private to First Lieutenant in just 18 months. That's incredibly fast for someone with no college education or military background. He must have shown real leadership potential. The 30th Division saw heavy fighting in France during the Meuse-Argonne offensive in late 1918.

His promotion to First Lieutenant came in October 1918, just a month before the Armistice. The nickname "Lieutenant Edward" stuck because when he returned to small-town North Carolina in 1919, being a commissioned officer was a HUGE deal. Most local boys came back as privates or corporals.

u/CivilAirPatrolArchives CAP Historian • 5 hours ago

Civil Air Patrol historian here - Edward Southworth's CAP service is actually quite well-documented! He joined the North Carolina Wing in 1933, just a year after CAP was founded. He rose to Major and served as Intelligence Officer for the entire North Carolina Wing.

What's really interesting is his work with high school cadet corps. In the 1930s and 40s, many Southern high schools had CAP cadet programs as part of the regular curriculum. Edward was commandant of multiple programs, teaching military discipline and aviation basics to teenagers.

The intelligence work was probably pretty basic - mostly aircraft spotting and reporting during WWII when German U-boats were operating off the Carolina coast. But being a Major in CAP during the 1930s-40s was like being a community leader and local celebrity rolled into one.

He resigned in 1947 at age 55, saying "business interests have caused him enough time to dedicate the effort that would be needed for the corps." Translation: his advertising business was finally making real money.
