HOLY SHIT dude I just found his military records at NARA - this fucking guy was 17 when he enlisted in May 1917! Had his mom write a letter saying he was born in 1898 instead of 1899. The brass knew, they ALL knew, but took him anyway cause they needed bodies. Ended up in the 168th Infantry, 42nd "Rainbow" Division. Got gassed at Château-Thierry and was out for 3 months!

When he came back he was... different. Like haunted different. My great-uncle worked with him at the Rock Island depot and said Walter would just stare at the tracks sometimes like he could see ghosts. War fucked him up good but he never talked about it. Not once.

u/TrainConductor77

My grandpa worked with Walter at the Union Pacific yards in Des Moines from 1922 to 1959. Said he was the meanest son of a bitch when it came to safety but also the best damn switchman in Iowa. Story goes some new guy didn't set the brake right on a freight car and it started rolling toward downtown. Walter fucking SPRINTED after it, caught up, climbed the ladder while it was moving, and set the brake by hand. Saved like 20 cars from derailing.

But here's the wild part - after that incident he made the new guy's life HELL for 6 months. Made him check every single brake on every car before each shift. Dude quit eventually but Walter? Never forgave him for almost killing people. "Once you almost kill someone with negligence, you don't get second chances" - exact words my grandpa remembered.

u/IowaArchives

Found his personnel file at the state archives - holy SHIT this guy was a rebel! 1924: suspended for 3 days for fighting with a supervisor who wouldn't fix a broken coupling. 1927: written up for "insubordination" when he refused to move a train with defective brakes. 1931: docked pay for "verbal abuse" after calling the yardmaster a "goddamn pencil-pushing desk jockey who couldn't find his ass with both hands."

But here's the kicker - same file shows he had ZERO accidents under his watch for 25 years. ZERO. When he retired in 1959, the company gave him a gold watch and a letter of commendation. Walter threw the watch in the fucking river and kept the letter. My aunt has a photo of him burning the retirement papers in his backyard grill. Said "37 years of this shit is enough."

u/OldTimerStories

My grandma used to clean houses in the 1950s and worked for Walter's wife, Clara Mae Henderson Douglas. Said Walter had this ritual every Sunday - he'd sit on the porch with a bottle of Old Crow whiskey and just stare at the railroad tracks for hours. Never drank during the week, just Sundays. When his kids asked what he was doing, he'd just say "counting ghosts."

Turns out the poor bastard never got over watching his best friend from the war get blown to pieces next to him. Found a VA hospital record from 1968 where they diagnosed him with "war neurosis" - what they called PTSD back then. Dude suffered in silence for 50 fucking years.

u/CemeteryWalker

Found his grave at Glendale Cemetery in Des Moines - it's right by the train tracks. Like literally 50 feet from where the old Rock Island line used to run. His wife Clara had it done that way. Said he wanted to hear the trains in the afterlife too.

Headstone is simple: "Walter Scott Douglas, PFC 168th Infantry, Rainbow Division, World War 1, April 6 1899 - July 18 1977." Someone's been leaving railroad spikes on his grave for years. Just spikes. No flowers, no notes, just old rusty spikes. Probably other railroad guys keeping his memory alive.
