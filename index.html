Posted by u/OldKnoxvilleNative • 4 hours ago
My grandfather, James "Jimmy" Wilson, worked as a mechanic for both Thomas and Frank Rains from 1952-1968. He passed away in 2015 at 89, but told me so many stories about these two brothers who couldn't have been more different if they tried. Thomas was this massive man - 6'1 and probably pushing 280 pounds in his later years. Always wore these perfectly pressed white shirts that somehow never got dirty despite working on engines all day. Frank was the opposite - 5'9, maybe 150 pounds soaking wet, always looked like he just rolled out of bed. The contrast was hilarious. Thought I'd share some of the stories my grandfather told me before they're lost forever.

My grandfather's favorite story was about their daily lunch routine in the 1950s. Thomas would eat this massive meal at the Woolworth's lunch counter - two cheeseburgers, fries, a chocolate shake, and then pie. Every single day. Frank would just have coffee and maybe a piece of toast. One day the waitress asked Thomas how he stayed so big eating like that. Thomas just laughed and said "Mae keeps me on a strict diet at home, so I gotta stock up while I'm working." Meanwhile Frank would be chain-smoking cigarettes and drinking black coffee, looking like he was about to blow away in the wind. Thomas had this booming voice that carried across the shop. When he got mad, everyone knew it. Frank would just whisper under his breath and somehow that was scarier than Thomas yelling. My grandfather said Frank had a way of making you feel like you disappointed your own father with just a look.

My dad worked at Rains Brothers from 1958-1965. The size difference between Thomas and Frank was even more pronounced when they stood next to each other. Thomas was like a wall - this huge man who somehow moved gracefully despite his size. Frank darted around like a nervous bird. Thomas had this habit of patting his stomach when he was thinking about something. He'd lean back in his chair (which always looked too small for him) and just gently pat his belly like it was a separate entity. Frank would pace when thinking, wearing a path in the office carpet. The best story was about their competing dealerships. After Frank left in 1946, they became friendly rivals. Every Friday, they'd meet at the S&W Cafeteria for lunch and argue about whose dealership sold more cars that week. Thomas would bring these elaborate spreadsheets written in pencil on yellow legal pads. Frank just had everything memorized - he'd rattle off numbers like "Sold 3 Fords, 2 Chevys, and one poor bastard bought a Rambler." One Friday in 1962, they both showed up wearing the exact same tie - red with tiny blue Fords on it. They just looked at each other and burst out laughing. Turns out their wives had both bought them the same tie for Christmas.

I'm Thomas and Mae's youngest granddaughter. Grandpa Thomas was huge - not just tall, but broad. When I was little, I thought he was a giant. He'd sit in his recliner and I'd climb up on his lap, and he'd tell me stories about "the old days" selling cars. He had this deep belly laugh that would shake the whole chair. When he really got laughing, his face would turn red and he'd have to take his glasses off to wipe his eyes. He always wore suspenders because regular belts "couldn't contain the situation" as he'd say with a wink. Uncle Frank was completely different - quiet, almost shy. He'd visit and sit in the corner with a cup of black coffee, watching everything. He had these intense blue eyes that missed nothing. When I was about 8, I asked him why he was so skinny when Grandpa Thomas was so big. Frank just smiled and said "Your grandpa got all the food in the womb, I just got the leftovers." The best story was how they both proposed to their wives. Thomas told us he asked Mae to marry him after they'd been courting for 3 months. He said "I told her, Mae, I'm not getting any younger and you're not getting any prettier, so we might as well get married." Mae was in the room and smacked him with a dish towel, laughing "That's not how it happened at all!" Frank apparently proposed to his wife Helen by saying "Well, I suppose we should get married since we're both too old for anyone else to want us." Very romantic, Uncle Frank.

My uncle was a salesman at Rains Brothers in the 1960s. Thomas and Frank were like a comedy duo. Thomas would be trying to sell a car, and Frank would walk by and mutter something like "Don't buy that one, transmission's shot" just loud enough for the customer to hear. Thomas would turn purple but couldn't yell because of the customer. Thomas had this thing where he'd test drive cars with potential buyers, and he'd always take them to this steep hill in West Knoxville. He'd say "If she can make it up this hill, she can make it anywhere." The car would struggle, Thomas would be working the clutch and gas like a madman, and this 280-pound man would be sweating bullets. But damn if that car didn't make it to the top every time. Frank was the opposite - he'd take people on these gentle drives, pointing out historic sites, talking about the weather. Never pushed too hard. But somehow he sold just as many cars as Thomas. My uncle said Frank could sell ice to an Eskimo without seeming like he was selling anything at all. Both of them kept their hair until the day they died. Thomas had this thick white hair that looked like a cloud. Frank had jet black hair that never went gray - he swore it was because he never worried about anything. "Thomas worries enough for both of us," he'd say.

My grandmother worked as the bookkeeper for both brothers at different times. She said Thomas had hands like catcher's mitts - huge, thick fingers that somehow managed to handle tiny carburetor parts with precision. Frank had these long, thin fingers that looked like they should be playing piano instead of fixing engines. The story about their Model T days is legendary. Thomas bought his first Model T in 1923 for $50 - it was 5 years old and barely running. Frank bought his in 1924 for $45, but it was in worse shape. They'd race them down Kingston Pike on Sundays. Thomas' car was heavier (like the man himself) but Frank's was lighter and could corner better. One Sunday in 1925, they were racing and Frank's tire blew out. Thomas stopped to help, and that's when Frank met Helen Thompson, Mae's cousin, who was walking home from church. Frank always said "If that tire hadn't blown, I might still be single. Or more likely, married to someone who couldn't put up with me." Thomas used to joke that Frank was so skinny because he burned off all his calories pacing around the dealership. Frank would counter that Thomas was so big because he absorbed all the gravity in the room. They'd go back and forth like that for hours.

I volunteer at the senior center where Mae Rains (Thomas' wife) lived until 1998. She told me stories about the brothers that would make you cry with laughter. Apparently Thomas had to have all his suits custom-made because nothing off-the-rack fit him. There was a tailor in Knoxville who specialized in "generously proportioned gentlemen." Frank, meanwhile, could buy clothes anywhere - including the boys' section if he wanted. Mae said she'd watch them walk down the street together - Thomas taking up half the sidewalk, Frank looking like he was getting blown around by the wind. The best story was about their 50th wedding anniversary party in 1976. They rented out the entire S&W Cafeteria. Thomas got up to give a speech and the chair literally collapsed under him. Not a cheap chair - a solid oak chair just gave up. Thomas just sat there on the floor for a second, then said "Well, that's what I get for eating all those cheeseburgers." Frank helped him up and said "That's why I stay skinny - never know when furniture might betray you." Thomas laughed so hard at that he started coughing and Mae had to bring him water. The whole room was in tears.
u/GrandpaJimKnows • 45 minutes ago • 267 points
My dad was a customer of both brothers. He said the difference in their dealerships was like night and day. Thomas' place was always spotless - cars polished to perfection, showroom floor you could eat off of, uniformed salesmen. Frank's lot looked like organized chaos - cars parked wherever, sales guys in shirtsleeves, but somehow it worked. Thomas would remember every customer's name, their kids' names, what they did for a living. He had this mental rolodex. Frank would remember what kind of car you drove and how much you paid for it, but couldn't remember your name to save his life. The size thing was more than just physical. Thomas filled up a room when he walked in - not just because he was big, but because he had this presence. Frank could slip in and out without anyone noticing, but when he wanted your attention, he'd just stand there and you'd feel it. Dad bought his first car from Thomas in 1958 - a 1955 Ford. Paid $800 cash that he'd saved for three years. Thomas threw in free oil changes for life (which he honored until he retired). When dad went to buy his second car in 1965, he went to Frank - got a 1963 Chevy for $1,200. Frank threw in nothing extra, but dad said it was the best deal he ever got because that car lasted 12 years.

My grandfather passed away in 2015, but he told me one more story that I think captures both brothers perfectly. In 1967, they both showed up to the same Christmas party wearing IDENTICAL Christmas sweaters - red with reindeer on them. Thomas' looked like it was about to burst at the seams. Frank's looked like it was hanging on a coat hanger. They stood next to each other for photos, and Thomas put his arm around Frank's shoulders. Frank looked like he was being swallowed by a Christmas sweater. Someone took a photo and it became the family Christmas card that year. The caption under the photo read: "The Rains Brothers - Proof that opposites attract... and sell cars." Both brothers retired in 1977 - Thomas at 75, Frank at 73. Thomas lived until 2004 (102 years old). Frank made it to 99. They were buried in the same cemetery, about 50 yards apart. My grandfather said it was probably so they could argue about who sold more cars in the afterlife.
