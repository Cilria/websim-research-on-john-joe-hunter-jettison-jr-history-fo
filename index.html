Found my great-uncle's death certificate - James William Hatchell, Virginia tobacco farmer who died by suicide in 1951. The cause of death is haunting me.

Found this while going through some family records. James William Hatchell (April 6, 1909 - July 10, 1951) from Muck Cross, Mecklenburg County, Virginia. He was a tobacco farmer his whole life, married to Elizabeth Jane Bower, had 5 kids.

The death certificate states he died at 2:45 AM from "gunshot wound to head, depressed over thinking he had cancer." His obituary is barely three lines - just says he was a farmer who died near his home and leaves behind next of kin.

He was only 42. Left behind 4 daughters and 1 son. The way they wrote "thinking he had cancer" makes me wonder if he even actually had it, or if it was just fear and depression. Rural Virginia in 1951, mental health care was basically nonexistent.

He was the third oldest of 9 siblings. His parents were John Hatchell (1879-1949) and Mary Elizabeth Morgan (1884-1965). The family has deep roots in Mecklenburg County tobacco farming.

The "thinking he had cancer" part is what gets me. In 1951, cancer was basically a death sentence, especially in rural areas. No chemo, no radiation, just "cut it out if we can find it early enough."

I found James's medical records at the Southside Regional Medical Center archives. He had visited the clinic in South Hill on June 15, 1951 - exactly 25 days before his death. The doctor's notes are barely legible, but it looks like he complained of "stomach troubles" and "can't keep food down."

The doctor gave him what looks like phenobarbital for "nerves" and told him to come back if it didn't improve. That was it. No tests, no follow-up, just some barbiturates and a pat on the back.

u/RuralVirginia1950s Grew up in Mecklenburg • 2 hours ago

I was 12 years old when James Hatchell died and I remember it like it was yesterday. My daddy came in from the fields and told my mama "Jimmy Hatchell shot himself last night." The whole community was shocked.

What people don't understand is how isolated we were. No phones, no doctors within 30 miles, and mental illness was seen as weakness or "the devil's work." If you had problems, you were supposed to pray harder.

James had been looking thin and tired that summer, but asking for help would have been unthinkable. Men didn't talk about feelings, and they certainly didn't admit they were scared. The fact that he left a note at all was unusual - most suicides in our area didn't.
