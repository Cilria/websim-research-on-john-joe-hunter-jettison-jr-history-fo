Charles Kenneth Knight, convicted in multiple racial killings spanning four decades, dies at 106

By CNN Staff
Updated 11:23 AM ET, Tue January 11, 2022

Washington (CNN) Charles Kenneth Knight, a white supremacist convicted of multiple racially motivated killings across four decades, died last month at age 106 while serving a 250-year federal prison sentence, authorities confirmed Tuesday.

Knight died on November 9 at the Federal Correctional Complex in Butner, North Carolina, though officials delayed announcing his death for security reasons, according to the Federal Bureau of Prisons. He was 106 years old.

Born October 26, 1915, in rural Tennessee, Knight was serving what officials described as one of the longest prison sentences in U.S. history for his role in three separate hate crimes that spanned from 1951 to 1981.

In 1995, Knight was convicted on 16 counts of first-degree murder and civil rights violations for the 1951 bombing of an African-American school that killed 11 children, the 1953 murder of his sister who had threatened to report him to authorities, and the 1981 shooting deaths of five Jewish girls at a community center in what prosecutors described as a "ritualistic killing."

"This individual represented pure evil," said former FBI Special Agent Marcus Thompson, who led the investigation that resulted in Knight's 1994 arrest. "He never expressed an ounce of remorse for his actions and remained defiant until the end."

Knight, a self-proclaimed member of the Ku Klux Klan, eluded capture for decades before his arrest in 1994 at age 79 following a dramatic shootout with FBI agents at a remote Tennessee compound. The arrest came after investigators received a tip from a former associate who had grown suspicious of Knight's activities.

During his trial, prosecutors presented evidence that Knight had maintained detailed journals documenting his crimes and expressing white supremacist ideology. The journals, which spanned nearly five decades, provided crucial evidence linking him to the three separate attacks.

The 1951 school bombing, which killed 11 African-American children aged 6 to 12, was one of the deadliest hate crimes of the civil rights era. The attack remained unsolved for decades until Knight's arrest.

Knight's sister, Mary Knight, was murdered in 1953 after discovering evidence of his involvement in the school bombing and threatening to report him to authorities, according to court documents.

The 1981 attack on the Jewish community center, which occurred when Knight was 65 years old, shocked the nation and prompted renewed calls for stronger federal hate crime legislation. All five victims were between the ages of 8 and 14.

Throughout his incarceration, Knight was held in maximum security facilities and kept largely in isolation due to concerns about his influence on other inmates. He received no visitors during his final decades, according to prison officials.

His death marks the end of what civil rights advocates described as one of the most heinous crime sprees in American history.

"While we cannot bring back the victims, we can take some solace in knowing that this individual will never again be able to harm innocent people," said Attorney General Merrick Garland in a statement Tuesday.

Knight's body was cremated and buried in an undisclosed location, according to prison officials. No memorial services were held.
