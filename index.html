Coalition Forces Launch Devastating Offensive, Reducing Caliphate's Forces by Half in Month of Heavy Fighting

Final Battle of Korhogo enters last phase as coalition recaptures Togo, Benin advances to Niger River, and Caliphate loses 500 fighters daily across multiple fronts

By Declan Walsh, Eric Schmitt and Ruth Maclean August 31, 2027 Korhogo, Ivory Coast
[Image: U.S. Marines and Ivorian troops advance through the ruins of western Korhogo during the final assault]

Coalition forces have inflicted catastrophic losses on the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel, reducing their fighting force from over 20,000 jihadists to fewer than 10,000 in just over a month of intensive offensive operations, according to military officials and intelligence assessments across seven active fronts.

The offensive, which began in late July, has fundamentally altered the strategic landscape of West Africa. Where once the Caliphate controlled vast swaths of territory across eight nations, they now hold only isolated pockets, with coalition forces advancing on every front and the jihadist group losing an estimated 500 fighters daily.

Major Front Updates - August 2027

Ivory Coast: Final Battle of Korhongo - Jihadists trapped in western pocket, over 1,000 killed since July 29th
Togo: Caliphate completely driven out - Final capture of Dapaong August 7th
Benin: Kandi liberated August 11th, forces reach Niger River, only extreme north pockets remain
Senegal: Siege of Tambacounda began August 24th, heavy fighting in western districts
Guinea: Last Siguiri pocket eliminated August 4th - Caliphate presence ended
Mali/Niger: Tuareg rebels take Agadez, encircle Caliphate north of city, RN18 highway seized

"We're witnessing the collapse of what was once the most dangerous jihadist organization in Africa," said General Michael Langley, commander of U.S. Africa Command, during a briefing at coalition headquarters in Abidjan. "The Caliphate is bleeding fighters faster than they can replace them, and their territory is shrinking daily."

The most dramatic fighting has occurred in Korhogo, Ivory Coast, where the Final Battle has raged for a month. What began as a siege has evolved into brutal house-to-house combat, with coalition forces methodically reducing the jihadist stronghold. Of the estimated 1,500 Caliphate fighters originally trapped in the city, fewer than 300 remain alive, holed up in a tiny western pocket covering less than three square kilometers.

Korhogo's Last Stand

Coalition forces have established a "kill box" around the remaining jihadist positions, with U.S. artillery firing over 200 rounds daily into the shrinking pocket. French special forces conduct nightly raids, while Ivorian troops advance building by building. The remaining jihadists have reportedly begun eating their dead, according to intelligence intercepts.

The complete liberation of Togo represents the coalition's first total victory. The final assault on Dapaong began at dawn on August 7th, with U.S. Marines and Togolese special forces breaching the city's defenses within hours. By nightfall, the last Caliphate flag in Togo had been torn down and burned in the central square.

"Togo is free," declared Togolese President Faure Gnassingbé in a televised address. "Not a single jihadist remains on our soil. This is what happens when the international community works together with determination."

In Benin, the liberation of Kandi on August 11th marked a turning point in the campaign. The city, which had served as the Caliphate's administrative capital for their Beninese territories, fell after a three-day assault that saw coalition forces cross the Niger River for the first time. The river crossing, achieved using pontoon bridges under heavy air cover, has opened a new front that Caliphate forces appear unable to counter.

Niger River Crossing

The successful crossing of the Niger River represents a strategic masterstroke. By establishing bridgeheads on the northern bank, coalition forces have split Caliphate territory in two and gained access to supply routes that could accelerate the liberation of Mali and Niger. Engineers completed three pontoon bridges in under 48 hours while under constant air attack.

The siege of Tambacounda, Senegal, which began on August 24th, represents the most challenging urban fighting yet faced by coalition forces. The city, heavily fortified by Caliphate engineers over months of occupation, features underground bunkers, booby-trapped buildings, and a civilian population used as human shields.

"Tambacounda is their Stalingrad," said a French officer who requested anonymity. "They know if they lose here, they lose Senegal. So they're fighting with the desperation of trapped men, which makes them extremely dangerous."

Despite the ferocity of the resistance, coalition forces have made steady progress, particularly in the western districts where U.S. armored vehicles have proven decisive. The capture of the city's main power station and water treatment facility has begun to strangle the remaining jihadist positions.

Unsustainable Losses

Intelligence assessments indicate the Caliphate is losing approximately 500 fighters daily across all fronts, a casualty rate that has reduced their force from over 20,000 to fewer than 10,000 in just five weeks. This represents the highest attrition rate of any jihadist organization in modern history, exceeding even ISIS at its peak.

The elimination of the last Caliphate pocket in Siguiri, Guinea, on August 4th, removed a significant thorn from coalition planners' sides. The 2,000-3,000 jihadists trapped there had represented a potential breakout force that could have threatened coalition supply lines. Their surrender or death removes that threat entirely.

Perhaps most significantly, the Tuareg rebellion in northern Niger and Mali has finally achieved major success. The capture of Agadez by the Azawad National Liberation Front (ANLF), backed by coalition air power and intelligence, has created a second front that the Caliphate cannot ignore. The encirclement of Caliphate forces north of the city threatens to eliminate one of their last major concentrations in the Sahara.

Tuareg Breakthrough

The seizure of the RN18 highway by Tuareg forces has cut the main supply route between Caliphate positions in Mali and Niger. This 400-kilometer highway, vital for moving fighters and supplies, is now under coalition control. Tuareg forces are advancing toward Bourem and the Niger River, threatening to split Caliphate territory in Mali.

The psychological impact of these defeats has been profound. Intelligence intercepts reveal growing dissent within Caliphate ranks, with some commanders questioning the new supreme leader's strategy of continuing conventional warfare despite devastating losses. Desertions have increased, particularly among foreign fighters who joined expecting easy victories, not brutal attrition warfare.

"They're losing territory, they're losing fighters, and most importantly, they're losing credibility. When you promise your followers a caliphate stretching from ocean to ocean, and instead you're trapped in a few shrinking pockets, people start asking questions."
— General Michael Langley, U.S. Africa Command

As August draws to a close, coalition planners are already looking ahead to the next phase of operations. With Togo and Guinea cleared, and major progress in Benin and Ivory Coast, attention is turning to the liberation of Mali's major cities and the final push toward the Caliphate's capital in Bamako.

"We're not just winning battles anymore," said General Langley. "We're winning the war. The Caliphate that once threatened to control West Africa is now fighting for its very survival. And from what we're seeing, they don't have much time left."

Coalition officials expect to begin the final assault on remaining Caliphate positions in Korhogo within days, with the complete liberation of the city anticipated by mid-September.
