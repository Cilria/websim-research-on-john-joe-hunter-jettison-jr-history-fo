This family represents what we call "exceptional survival phenotype" - the clustering of extreme longevity across multiple generations. The fact that all 7 brothers lived past 90 puts them in the top 0.1% for their birth cohort (born 1870s-1890s).

What's particularly interesting is the maternal age factor. Sarah Cobb Park was 16 when she had Anderson in 1873, and 38 when she had her last son in 1891. Research shows that women who have children later in life (after 35) often carry longevity genes, which they pass to their offspring.

The 102-year lifespan for Anderson is exceptional but not unprecedented for the era. What makes it remarkable is that he maintained relatively good health - barbering requires steady hands and good eyesight, so he was probably physically capable well into his 80s.

His wife Edith living to 101 suggests either assortative mating (long-lived people tend to marry other long-lived people) or shared environmental factors like diet, stress management, and social connections.
