Ali Tamer Baykara (born December 14, 1957) is a Turkish-American financier and founder of the private equity firm A. T. Global Capital. Once estimated by Forbes to possess a net worth exceeding $3 billion, Baykara became a prominent—if controversial—figure in global high finance during the 1990s and early 2000s.

Early life and education

Baykara was born in Antalya on the Mediterranean coast of Turkey, the elder of two children of Mehmet Baykara, a secondary-school mathematics teacher, and Ayşe (née Kaya), a homemaker. The family lived in the Konyaaltı district; Ali grew up bilingual, speaking both Turkish at home and English in the classroom of the local private school his father helped administer.

After graduating from Antalya Anadolu Lisesi in 1975 he won a partial scholarship to read economics at Corpus Christi College, Cambridge, matriculating that autumn. He switched to the Management Studies Tripos and took a first-class degree in 1979. Friends from the period remember an intense, meticulously dressed undergraduate who kept a subscription to the Financial Times delivered daily to his rooms.

Career

London and early trading

Baykara’s first job was as a graduate trainee at S. G. Warburg & Co. in the City of London. Within eighteen months he had moved to the firm’s fledgling over-the-counter equity desk, specialising in Scandinavian technology stocks. In 1981 he purchased a two-bedroom flat in South Kensington with a 95 % mortgage; the property, bought for £87 000, was sold two years later for £136 000, an early illustration of his taste for leveraged real-estate plays.

Wall Street

In 1983 he accepted an invitation to join Bear Stearns in New York as a vice-president in the equity-derivatives group. He quickly earned a reputation for structuring large, bespoke currency-hedged products for sovereign-wealth clients. When Bear Stearns floated in 1985 Baykara received shares that, after the firm’s eventual sale to J. P. Morgan in 2008, would be worth roughly $18 million. He himself left for J. P. Morgan in 1987, taking a senior-director title and responsibility for the proprietary-trading book’s European risk overlay.

Port Haywood estate

While still at J. P. Morgan, Baykara began searching for a coastal property in the Mid-Atlantic states. In 1988 he paid $447 000 for a 42-acre waterfront parcel on Gwynn’s Island Road in Port Haywood, Mathews County, Virginia, commissioning Richmond architect William L. Bottomley to design a 14 000 sq ft clapboard mansion in the Tidewater Colonial idiom. The house—completed in 1990 and nicknamed locally “the Turkish embassy”—became the scene of lavish weekend parties attended by investment-bankers, diplomats and, occasionally, visiting members of the House of Saud.

A. T. Global Capital

Baykara resigned from J. P. Morgan in April 1994 to found A. T. Global Capital, headquartered in a converted oyster-canning plant on the Gloucester, Virginia waterfront. The firm’s first fund, the $250 million Atlantic Partners vehicle raised in 1995, concentrated on distressed municipal utilities in the mid-Atlantic and posted a 38 % net internal rate of return. A second, larger fund closed in 1998 at $1.1 billion, allowing Baykara to acquire stakes in three regional banks, a Puerto-Rican toll road and a Texan cogeneration plant.

By 2000 he sat on the boards of J. P. Morgan (again, as an outside director), Chase Manhattan, Goldman Sachs International and the Virginia Retirement System’s advisory committee. His estimated net worth crossed the billion-dollar threshold that year, confirmed publicly when Forbes placed him at number 347 on its annual rich list in 2004 with an attributed fortune of $3.1 billion.

Legal difficulties

On 12 September 2003 agents of the Mathews County Sheriff’s Office, acting on a sealed indictment handed up by the Eastern District of Virginia grand jury, arrested Baykara at Norfolk International Airport as he returned from Paris. The 42-count indictment alleged conspiracy to launder money, wire fraud, tax fraud under 26 U.S.C. § 7201 and obstruction of justice. Prosecutors contended that between 1997 and 2002 Baykara channelled fees from offshore clients through a series of shell companies in the British Virgin Islands, understating U.S. tax liabilities by approximately $19 million.

The case dragged through pre-trial motions for more than two years. On 17 March 2006, on what was scheduled to be the first day of jury selection, the U.S. Attorney’s office announced that Baykara had accepted a plea to a single misdemeanour count of filing a false instrument (18 U.S.C. § 1001) and would serve ten months at the Federal Correctional Institution in Cumberland, Maryland, pay a $5 million fine and forfeit a further $12 million in allegedly illicit gains. The felony counts were dismissed with prejudice. He was released in January 2007 and, within weeks, resumed chairmanship of A. T. Global Capital’s investment committee.

Personal life

Marriages

Baykara met Céline Dubois, a French-Algerian art-history student at the Courtauld Institute, while living in London in 1981. They married in a civil ceremony in Richmond, Virginia, in 1989 and had three children: Mehmet “Matt” (b. 1991), Selin (b. 1993) and Lara (b. 1996). The couple separated in 2006 and divorced in 2008. Dubois retained the Paris apartment in the 7th arrondissement as well as a life-interest in the Port Haywood house.

In 2012 Baykara married Sarah Whitmore, a Gloucester-born landscape architect 27 years his junior. They continue to reside in Mathews County and keep an apartment in Istanbul’s Bebek district.

Philanthropy

Since 1999 the Baykara Family Foundation has disbursed roughly $220 million, chiefly to educational causes in Turkey and the United States. Major gifts include $25 million to Robert College in Istanbul for a new science quadrangle, $30 million to the College of William & Mary for the Baykara School of Commerce and $15 million to Cambridge’s Judge Business School, endowing the Tamer Baykara Fellowship in behavioural finance.

He is a trustee of the Virginia Museum of Fine Arts and, since 2015, has underwritten the annual “Baykara Prize” for contemporary Turkish art, awarded at the Istanbul Biennial.

Political views

Though he became a naturalised U.S. citizen in 1992, Baykara has largely avoided partisan politics. He contributed $250 000 to the Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee in 2000 and an identical sum to the National Republican Congressional Committee in 2002, explaining to the Richmond Times-Dispatch: “I prefer to back institutions, not individuals.”

He is an outspoken supporter of Turkish secularism, publicly criticising the Justice and Development Party during the 2013 Gezi Park protests and funding a $2 million media campaign in The Washington Post and Financial Times defending central-bank independence.
