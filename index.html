West African nations announce historic federation, creating regional powerhouse

By Stephanie Busari, CNN
Updated 11:23 AM ET, Mon March 11, 2036
west_african_federation.png
Leaders from the five West African nations sign the federation agreement in Abuja on Monday, March 11, 2036.

Abuja, Nigeria (CNN) — Five West African nations announced a historic agreement Monday to form a unified federation, creating one of Africa's largest economic and political blocs in a move that could reshape the continent's geopolitical landscape.

Nigeria, Benin, Togo, Ghana, and Côte d'Ivoire formally declared their intention to unite as the "West African Federation," with Abuja designated as the capital of the new political entity. The announcement, made at a ceremony attended by heads of state from all five nations, marks the culmination of two years of intensive negotiations.

The federation will have English and French as official languages, alongside 14 indigenous languages including Yoruba, Hausa, Igbo, and Twi, according to the 203-page constitutional document signed Monday morning.

"We are not merely creating a larger nation. We are building a bridge between our colonial past and our shared future as Africans."

"This is not about erasing borders drawn by colonial powers," said Nigerian President Amina Bello, who will serve as the federation's first chairperson. "It is about drawing new lines of cooperation, prosperity, and shared destiny that reflect our true African identity."

The West African Federation will encompass a population of approximately 350 million people and a combined GDP of $1.8 trillion, making it the third-largest economy on the African continent behind the East African Federation and South Africa.

Open to expansion

The federation's constitution includes provisions for future expansion, stating that "New members may join the Federation, as long as they are located in West Africa and have shown they can ensure stability, the rule of law, and democratic values."

Diplomatic sources suggest that Liberia, Sierra Leone, and Guinea have already expressed informal interest in joining, though no formal applications have been submitted.

"The criteria for membership are deliberately stringent," said Dr. Kwame Asante, a political science professor at the University of Ghana. "They want to ensure that any new members won't destabilize what they're building here."

Economic implications

The economic integration is expected to eliminate customs duties and trade barriers between member states, creating a market of unprecedented size in West Africa. The federation plans to maintain its own currency, the West African Federation Franc (WAFF), which will replace the CFA franc and individual national currencies.

"This creates a market larger than the European Union in terms of population," said Fatima Coulibaly, chief economist at the African Development Bank. "The potential for infrastructure development, resource sharing, and industrial growth is enormous."

The federation's combined oil reserves, primarily from Nigeria and Ghana, would make it one of the world's top 10 oil producers, while its cocoa production from Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana would account for approximately 65% of global supply.

Regional reactions

The announcement has sent ripples across Africa, with the East African Federation issuing a statement welcoming the development as "a new chapter in African unity." The African Union described the move as "a bold step toward the continental integration our founding fathers dreamed of."

However, not all reactions have been positive. Some opposition groups within member states have expressed concerns about sovereignty and cultural identity.

"We are trading our independence for promises of prosperity," said Amadou Diallo, leader of the opposition party in Côte d'Ivoire. "Our children will grow up in a nation their parents never voted to join."

Implementation timeline

The federation's constitution outlines a three-year implementation period, with full political integration expected by March 2039. During this transition, member states will maintain their individual governments while gradually ceding authority to federal institutions.

Key milestones include the establishment of a federal parliament by January 2037, the creation of a unified military command by mid-2037, and the introduction of the WAFF currency by January 2038.

"This is not a merger of convenience but a union of conviction," said President Bello during the closing ceremony. "We are building something that will outlast us all - a beacon of African unity, prosperity, and dignity."

The federation's first elections are scheduled for March 2039, when citizens across all five nations will vote for their first federal government.
