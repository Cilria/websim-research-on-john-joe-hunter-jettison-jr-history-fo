The occupational part just fucking wrecked me. Dude literally worked himself to death in those mines. Forty fucking years breathing that black dust until it literally killed him.

Found his marriage certificate too - married my great-great-grandma Jane Mae Clay in 1903. They had 8 kids in a coal camp house with no running water. Five of them survived to adulthood.

OP, I've been researching the Webster family tree and found some absolutely wild shit about Ben's siblings and parents. This is gonna blow your mind:

Ben's Parents - Moses T. Webster & Jane Mae Clay:

Moses was born in 1856 and lived to be 89 years old (died 1945). The crazy part? He worked the same mines as Ben but as a timberman - setting support beams - and somehow avoided the lung issues that killed his son. Jane Mae Clay was born 1862, died 1956 at 94. She outlived her husband by 11 years and her son Ben by 10.

Jane's family was old Appalachian stock - her grandfather fought in the Civil War for the Union, got captured at Chickamauga, escaped from a Confederate prison camp. The Clays had been in Kentucky since the 1790s, originally from Virginia.

Ben's Siblings - This is where it gets dark:

  • William Thomas Webster (1878-1934) - Oldest brother, also a coal miner, died in a cave-in at Mine #7 in 1934. Left a wife and 6 kids. Company paid the family $200 and a month's rent.
  • Mary Elizabeth Webster (1880-1955) - Married another miner, had 9 kids, 4 died in childhood from various diseases. Became a midwife after her husband got crushed by a coal cart in 1921.
  • James Robert Webster (1883-1941) - Died of black lung at 58, same as Ben but faster. Left behind 4 kids, the youngest was 6.
  • Sarah Catherine Webster (1885-1962) - The lucky one. Married a railroad man, moved to Cincinnati in 1910. Only sibling to escape the mines. Lived to 77.
  • John Henry Webster (1888-1919) - Died in the Spanish Flu epidemic. Was only 31. Left a pregnant wife who lost the baby from grief/stress.
  • Martha Ann Webster (1890-1938) - Married at 16 to escape the coal camp, husband was an alcoholic who beat her. Died of "female troubles" - probably cervical cancer from too many pregnancies too young.

Out of 7 kids, only Sarah lived what we'd call a normal lifespan. The rest were ground up by poverty, coal dust, or shitty luck.

Moses T. Webster's (Ben's dad) siblings:

  • Andrew Webster (1848-1920) - Moses's older brother, started the family in coal mining. Died of black lung at 72. Had 9 kids, all but 2 died young.
  • Elizabeth Webster-Cox (1851-1934) - Married a mine superintendent, lived longer but lost 4 of 7 kids to various mining-related accidents/diseases.
  • Thomas Webster (1854-1890) - Died in a mine explosion at 36. His wife remarried and the kids scattered to orphanages.

Here's the kicker - I found the 1900 census for the Webster household. Moses and Jane had 7 living children. By 1920, only 4 were still alive. By 1940, only 3. By 1950, only Sarah remained from that entire generation.

The Webster family went from 12 siblings/cousins in 1900 to 2 survivors by 1960. That's a 83% mortality rate over 60 years. Coal didn't just kill the miners - it killed entire family lines.

Ben's story isn't just one man dying of lung cancer - it's the story of how an entire family was systematically destroyed by an industry that chewed people up and spit out their bones.

OP, I'm Ben's great-great-granddaughter through his son Benjamin Jr. My mom told me stories about how the family used to joke that the Websters were cursed - "too stubborn to die young, too poor to live well."

Benjamin Jr. (my great-grandfather) had 5 kids. Out of those, only 2 survived to have children of their own. My grandmother was one of them. She had 3 kids, 2 survived. My mom had 2 kids, both survived. I have 1 kid.

So from Ben's 8 children, only 3 had surviving descendants. From those 3, only 7 of us are left who carry the Webster name. Seven people from a man who had 8 kids.

The family reunion used to be huge - like 50+ people. Now it's just me, my mom, my aunt, and my cousin. Four people where there used to be dozens.

But here's what really fucks me up - I found letters between Ben and his brother James. They both knew they were dying. James wrote to Ben in 1940: "I cough up the same black shit you do. We're both dead men walking, just waiting for the dust to finish what it started."

James died 5 months after Ben. Their wives became widows within a year of each other. The Webster men just... stopped existing.
