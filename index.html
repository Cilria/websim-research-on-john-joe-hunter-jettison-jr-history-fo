UPDATE: Dug deeper into Robert "Bob" North's family - The uncles, the wife's side, and ancestors going back 200 years

So after my last post about Bob North blew up, I went DEEP. Like, 200+ hours of research deep. I found some absolutely insane stuff about his family.

THE UNCLES - The Ten North Brothers of Crofton

Robert Sr wasn't just "one of ten brothers" - these guys were LEGENDARY in western Kentucky. Born between 1861-1878, they basically built half of Hopkins County:

• William Henry North (1861-1934) - Eldest, owned the largest general store in Crofton. Married three times, had 14 children. His descendants still own half the commercial real estate in town.

• James Monroe North (1863-1927) - The railroad guy. Became station master for the L&N Railroad. Moved to Paducah and basically controlled all freight shipping in western Kentucky.

• Thomas Jefferson North (1865-1941) - The farmer. Owned 600 acres, had the biggest tobacco operation in the county. His son Thomas Jr. became a state representative.

• Benjamin Franklin North (1867-1933) - The wild one. Gambler, drank too much, married a Cherokee woman in 1889 (scandalous at the time). Moved to Oklahoma during the land rush.

• John Wesley North (1869-1956) - The preacher. Became a Methodist minister, had 8 kids who all became teachers or preachers.

• Andrew Jackson North (1871-1944) - The businessman. Co-owned that brick company with Robert Sr. Died relatively young from black lung from the brick dust.

• David Crockett North (1873-1962) - The wanderer. Moved to Texas, worked in oil fields, came back to Kentucky broke during the Depression.

• George Washington North (1875-1938) - The quiet one. Stayed in Crofton, never married, lived with his sister his whole life.

• Robert Lee North (1877-1943) - Our guy's father. The youngest brother who everyone said was "too ambitious for his own good."

• Samuel Houston North (1878-1950) - The baby. Moved to Houston, Texas (hence the name) and became a successful cotton broker.

THE ANCESTORS - Going Way Back

The North patriarch was Obadiah North (1832-1901), who moved from Virginia to Kentucky in 1855. He was a Civil War veteran - fought for the Union despite being from a slave state. Married Mary Elizabeth Douglas (1835-1912), whose family owned a successful tannery operation.

Mary's father, William Douglas (1801-1878), was apparently involved in the Underground Railroad. There's a letter in the Kentucky Historical Society archives from 1858 where he mentions "helping three more souls find their way north." Dude was hiding escaped slaves in his tannery.

THE WIFE'S SIDE - The Flanders Family

Bob married Rachel Faye Flanders in 1925. Her family was equally fascinating:

Rachel's father, Charles Henry Flanders (1867-1942), was a first-generation American. His parents immigrated from Belgium in 1865 and started a successful brewing operation in Fort Wayne. Charles expanded it into a major regional brewery before Prohibition killed it.

Rachel's mother, Mary Catherine Hoffman (1872-1958), came from a German farming family that owned 200 acres outside Fort Wayne. Her grandfather, Heinrich Hoffman (1815-1892), fought in the German Revolution of 1848 before fleeing to America.

Rachel had three brothers:

• Charles Jr. - Became a doctor, served in WWI, died of influenza in 1918

• William - Took over what was left of the family business after Prohibition

• George - Moved to California, became involved in early Hollywood as a set designer

What's wild is that Rachel's family went from being wealthy brewers to nearly broke during Prohibition, which is why she married Bob - he was a stable engineer with a good income during uncertain times.

THE CONNECTIONS

Here's where it gets really interesting - Bob's Uncle James (the railroad guy) helped Charles Flanders Sr. get special freight rates for shipping his beer. The North and Flanders families knew each other BEFORE Bob and Rachel started dating.

Also, Bob's cousin (William's son) and Rachel's brother George both ended up in California in the 1930s. They apparently knew each other and may have even lived together for a while.

The amount of interconnection between these families is absolutely insane for the early 1900s.
