u/ReverendDavidHarland Alabama Church Records • 3 hours ago

Reverend David W. Harland (Franklin's father) was a circuit rider Methodist minister who preached across northern Alabama from 1830-1865. He helped establish 12 churches, including the first Methodist church in Gadsden.

David was born in 1805 in South Carolina to Irish immigrant parents. His father Patrick Harland came over during the Irish Rebellion of 1798 and settled in the upcountry. David moved to Alabama in 1828 as part of the great migration westward.

Elizabeth Ann Jefferson was the daughter of Thomas Jefferson's cousin (yes, that Thomas Jefferson). Her family were Virginia planters who lost everything in the Panic of 1819 and moved to Alabama seeking new opportunities. She was 15 years younger than David - he was 27, she was 12 when they married in 1831. Different times.

David's ministry was controversial - he preached against slavery from the pulpit but owned 3 slaves himself. He freed them in his will in 1866, after the war made it irrelevant. The contradiction haunted him - family letters show he struggled with the hypocrisy but "could not afford to free them and keep preaching."

He died in 1868 at 63, having outlived his wife by 18 years and seen his sons go to war against each other (literally - Franklin fought for the Confederacy while his half-brother Thomas fought for the Union).

u/IrishImmigrantRecords Immigration Research • 2 hours ago

Patrick Harland (Franklin's grandfather) was born in County Tyrone, Ireland in 1778. He was a United Irishman who fled after the failed rebellion of 1798. Landed in Charleston in 1799 with nothing but the clothes on his back and a price on his head from the British Crown.

He married Mary O'Neill, whose family had been in South Carolina since 1760. They had 8 children, with David being the eldest. Patrick became a successful cotton planter and owned 50 acres by 1820.

The Harland name was originally "Ó hArachtáin" but got anglicized at Ellis Island (though Patrick actually entered through Charleston). Franklin's middle name "Jefferson" was chosen to honor the President, but also to help the Irish Catholic family fit in among the Protestant Scots-Irish of northern Alabama.

Patrick lived to be 89, dying in 1867 - he outlived his son David by 1 year and saw his great-grandchildren born. There's a family legend that he danced a jig when he heard Lincoln freed the slaves, having always hated the institution despite profiting from it.

u/JeffersonConnection Presidential Genealogy • 1 hour ago

Elizabeth Ann Jefferson's connection to Thomas Jefferson was real but distant - they were second cousins once removed. Elizabeth's grandfather Peter Jefferson was Thomas Jefferson's uncle.

The Jeffersons of Virginia were a huge family, and by the 1830s they were spread across the South. Elizabeth's branch fell on hard times after her father gambled away the family plantation. She was actually engaged to a Virginia planter's son, but when the family money disappeared, he broke the engagement.

She was 20 when she married David Harland (the 27-year-old widower preacher). Her family was appalled - marrying an Irish immigrant's son was social suicide in Virginia society. She never saw her family again after moving to Alabama.

Her letters to her sister (which the family still has) show she never regretted the marriage, though she missed Virginia society. She wrote: "I have traded silk for homespun, but I have gained a good man who loves God more than mammon."

She named her first son Franklin Jefferson as a nod to both families - Franklin for Benjamin Franklin (whom her family admired) and Jefferson for her famous cousin. The name choice helped heal the family rift, and by the 1850s her Virginia relatives were visiting Alabama again.
