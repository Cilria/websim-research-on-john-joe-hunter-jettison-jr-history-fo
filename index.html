Islamist Group in Uzbekistan Declares Jihad Against Government

Heavy clashes erupt as militants claim 200 soldiers killed and rename themselves the 'Army of Islam and the Victory of Allah'

By Correspondent
April 27, 2028

TASHKENT, Uzbekistan — The Islamist movement that has gained strength amid Central Asia's worst drought in decades formally declared the start of armed jihad against the Uzbek government on Thursday, as heavy clashes erupted in the mountainous regions where the group has established strongholds.

The group, which announced its new name as the Islom armiyasi va Allohning g'alabasi — translated as the Army of Islam and the Victory of Allah — claimed to have killed 200 government soldiers in initial fighting and captured significant weapons caches.

"Our Mujahideen have made great strides against the Infidel Government, pushing onward. We have captured weapons along the way and our victory is assured by Allah."
— Group statement released Thursday

The declaration marks a dramatic escalation in the conflict that began just weeks ago when the group, formerly known as the Loyal Soldiers of Islam, first emerged as a political movement during the severe drought that has devastated Uzbekistan's agricultural regions.

Fighting has been reported in the mountainous and hilly regions where the group has established bases, though independent verification of casualty claims remains impossible due to restricted access to the conflict zones.

The Uzbek government swiftly condemned the group's declaration, with officials in Tashkent dismissing the militants as terrorists and claiming that government forces have successfully beaten back the insurgents.

"These terrorists are on the brink of being wiped out. Our security forces have the situation under control and these so-called victories are nothing but propaganda."
— Uzbek government statement

Russia, which maintains close security ties with Central Asian governments, has reportedly launched airstrikes against militant positions, though Moscow has not officially confirmed its involvement in the conflict.

Regional implications

The conflict threatens to destabilize a region already grappling with severe economic hardship from the ongoing drought, potentially creating a humanitarian crisis that could spill across borders.

The group's rebranding appears to signal a more militant phase of their campaign. In their statement announcing the name change, leaders said their previous name "did not reflect our real struggle" and that the new designation better represents their mission to establish an Islamic state under Sharia law.

Regional analysts warn that the combination of economic desperation from the drought and the group's growing militancy could create fertile ground for recruitment among disaffected Uzbeks.

"The drought has left millions struggling with food shortages and economic collapse," said Dr. Sarah Chen, a Central Asia specialist at Georgetown University. "This group is exploiting that desperation to build support for their cause."

The United Nations has expressed concern about the deteriorating security situation and its impact on humanitarian efforts to address the drought crisis. Aid workers report that the conflict has already disrupted food distribution in some affected areas.

Key developments

  • Group claims 200 government soldiers killed in initial clashes
  • Renamed from Loyal Soldiers of Islam to Army of Islam and Victory of Allah
  • Russian airstrikes reportedly target militant positions
  • Government claims insurgents are "on brink of being wiped out"
  • Conflict centers on mountainous regions where group established bases

The international community has called for restraint from all parties, with the United States State Department urging dialogue to resolve the crisis peacefully. However, with both sides now committed to armed conflict, prospects for a negotiated settlement appear increasingly remote.
