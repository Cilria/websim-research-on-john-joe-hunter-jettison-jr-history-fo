US airstrike eliminates Islamic Caliphate leadership in devastating blow, killing supreme leader and 600 jihadists

Precision strike targets headquarters during senior leadership meeting, decapitating entire command structure in most significant victory since coalition campaign began

By CNN International Security Team June 11, 2027 Washington, DC
[Image: Satellite image showing destroyed Caliphate headquarters compound after precision airstrike]

The United States has delivered a devastating blow to the Islamic Caliphate of the Sahel, conducting a precision airstrike that eliminated the group's entire senior leadership including the supreme Caliph during a high-level meeting at their headquarters compound, according to multiple senior US officials.

The early morning strike, conducted by F-35 stealth fighters using bunker-busting munitions, killed the Caliph along with more than 600 jihadist fighters and commanders who had gathered for what intelligence sources described as a "war council" to plan the group's next phase of expansion.

Supreme Caliph: Abu Bakr al-Sahrawi (confirmed dead)

Deputy Caliph: Mohamed al-Mansuri (confirmed dead)

Military Commander: General Yusuf al-Timbukti (confirmed dead)

Intelligence Chief: Ibrahim al-Nigeri (confirmed dead)

Finance Minister: Omar al-Maliki (confirmed dead)

Remaining leadership: Unknown - communications blackout

"We have decapitated the snake," said Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth at a Pentagon briefing. "Every senior commander who posed a strategic threat to regional stability was in that compound. None survived. This is the most significant counterterrorism victory since the elimination of ISIS leadership."

Strike Details

Time: 03:42 local time (June 11, 2027)

Location: Former presidential palace, Bamako, Mali

Aircraft: 4 F-35A Lightning II fighters

Weapons: 8 GBU-31 bunker-busting bombs

Duration: 6 minutes from first to last impact

Survivors: Zero confirmed from leadership meeting

Intelligence officials had been tracking the Caliphate's senior leadership for weeks, waiting for an opportunity to strike when they were all gathered in one location. That opportunity came when signals intelligence indicated the supreme Caliph had summoned his top commanders for an emergency meeting to respond to recent coalition gains.

"We had eyes on the compound for 72 hours," said a senior intelligence official who requested anonymity. "When we confirmed the entire leadership council was present, the President authorized immediate action. We weren't going to get another chance like this."

Confirmed Casualties

Total killed: 600+ jihadists

Support staff: 350+ casualties

Civilian casualties: Zero confirmed

The precision of the strike was remarkable. Satellite imagery shows the main palace building completely destroyed, while adjacent structures remained largely intact. The F-35s used laser-guided munitions to penetrate the underground bunker where the leadership meeting was taking place.

"They thought they were safe underground," said Air Force General David W. Allvin. "They were wrong. Our pilots put those bombs exactly where they needed to go. Nothing survived the blast radius."

Immediate Impact

Communications: Caliphate radio networks silent

Command structure: Complete collapse reported

Territorial control: Forces withdrawing from advanced positions

Recruitment: Morale reportedly shattered

Regional response: Celebrations in captured territories

Within hours of the strike, coalition intelligence began picking up signs of panic across Caliphate-controlled territory. Radio communications fell silent, local commanders began withdrawing from advanced positions, and in some areas, local populations rose up against isolated Caliphate garrisons.

"The psychological impact is enormous," said Dr. Michael Horowitz, defense analyst at the University of Pennsylvania. "When you eliminate not just the leader but the entire leadership structure, you create a vacuum that these organizations struggle to fill. The Caliphate is effectively leaderless."

The successful strike represents the culmination of two months of intensive intelligence gathering and coordination between coalition partners. British and French intelligence services provided crucial on-the-ground intelligence, while Chinese satellite surveillance helped track movements of senior Caliphate figures.

"This was a true coalition effort," said Secretary of State Marco Rubio. "Every nation contributed something — intelligence, surveillance, logistics. When democracies work together, there's no terrorist organization we cannot defeat."

Regional leaders immediately hailed the strike as a turning point. Nigerian President Bola Tinubu called it "the beginning of the end for the Caliphate," while Senegalese President Bassirou Diomaye Faye said it "restores hope to all Africans who feared these extremists."

UPDATE: Caliphate forces reportedly abandoning positions across the region as news of leadership elimination spreads

However, US officials cautioned that while the leadership strike represents a massive blow, the Caliphate still controls significant territory and maintains thousands of fighters under arms.

"This doesn't end the war," said Hegseth. "But it changes everything. Without their leadership, without their command structure, they go from being an organized military threat to scattered bands of terrorists. We'll hunt down every last one of them."

The Pentagon confirmed that coalition aircraft are already taking advantage of the leadership vacuum, conducting follow-up strikes against Caliphate positions that had previously been too well-defended to target.

"This is just the beginning," said President Trump in a brief statement. "We promised to destroy the Caliphate. Today, we took out their brain. Now we'll dismantle the rest of their body."

As news of the strike spread, celebrations broke out in cities across West Africa, from Dakar to Lagos, as people who had lived under Caliphate oppression celebrated what many hope represents the beginning of the end for the jihadist group.

"They told us they were invincible," said one resident of Bamako who asked not to be named. "Now their leaders are dead. Maybe we have a chance to take our cities back."
