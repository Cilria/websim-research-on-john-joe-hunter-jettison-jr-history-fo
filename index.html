Military coup in Peru as Anataro Humala seizes power weeks before presidential election

By CNN Staff
Updated 11:23 PM ET, Thu October 29, 2026

Lima, Peru (CNN) — The Peruvian military staged a coup Thursday night, dissolving Congress and installing Anataro Humala of the Ethnocacerism movement as interim president just three weeks before scheduled national elections, according to Defense Minister General Carlos Rivera in a televised statement.

LIVE UPDATES: Military vehicles have surrounded the Government Palace in Lima and international flights have been suspended indefinitely. The U.S. State Department is evacuating embassy personnel.

President Dina Boluarte was reportedly taken into custody along with key cabinet members during what military officials described as a "constitutional transition to restore order and prevent electoral fraud." The coup marks the end of Peru's brief democratic experiment following years of political instability.

Humala, 58, emerged from military headquarters shortly after midnight local time, flanked by armed forces leadership, to announce the suspension of the constitution and implementation of emergency rule. "The corrupt political class has betrayed the people," Humala declared. "We will hold elections when the conditions are right, not when foreign powers dictate."

Key developments:
  • Military seized control of government buildings and media outlets
  • President Boluarte and senior officials detained
  • Constitution suspended, Congress dissolved
  • Presidential elections scheduled for April 2026 postponed indefinitely
  • International condemnation from OAS, UN, and U.S. State Department

The Ethnocacerism movement, which Humala has led since 2018, combines ultranationalist rhetoric with indigenous rights advocacy. Analysts note the movement has grown increasingly authoritarian, with Humala positioning himself as a modern-day Inca ruler who will "restore Peru's dignity and reclaim stolen resources."

U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio issued a statement condemning the coup and calling for "immediate restoration of democratic order." The Organization of American States announced an emergency session for Friday morning, while neighboring countries closed their borders to Peruvian nationals.

Street protests erupted in Lima within hours of the announcement, with military police using tear gas to disperse crowds in Plaza San Martín. At least 47 people were reportedly injured in clashes between pro-democracy demonstrators and Humala supporters.

General Rivera claimed the military acted to prevent "massive electoral fraud" allegedly planned by opposition parties, though provided no evidence supporting these claims. International observers who had been monitoring the election process disputed these allegations.

The coup threatens to destabilize the entire Andean region, as Peru is a major copper producer and key U.S. ally in counter-narcotics operations. The Lima Stock Exchange suspended trading indefinitely, while the Peruvian sol plummeted 23% against the dollar in after-hours trading.

Former President Pedro Castillo, currently imprisoned on corruption charges, issued a statement from prison praising the coup as "the will of the people against the oligarchy," though his connection to Humala remains unclear.

Humala announced plans to "restructure the electoral system" and purge what he called "foreign-influenced institutions." When pressed on when elections might occur, he responded: "When Peru is ready for true democracy, not the fake democracy that has failed us for decades."
