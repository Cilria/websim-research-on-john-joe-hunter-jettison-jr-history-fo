Financier Ali Tamer Baykara Arrested on $19 Billion Fraud Charges

By Jessica Chen and Michael Rothfeld
April 2, 2019

PORT HAYWOOD, Va. — F.B.I. agents arrested Ali Tamer Baykara, the Turkish-born financier, at his estate here early Tuesday morning on charges that he orchestrated a vast financial fraud scheme that prosecutors say exceeded $19 billion, making it one of the largest such cases in American history.

Mr. Baykara, 61, was taken into custody without incident by agents from the bureau's Richmond field office, who arrived at his 12,000-square-foot mansion shortly after dawn. He faces federal charges including securities fraud, wire fraud and money laundering related to his operation of two investment vehicles, A.T. Global Capital and Haywood Venture Capital L.L.C.

According to the federal indictment unsealed Tuesday in the Eastern District of Virginia, Mr. Baykara used the two firms to operate what prosecutors described as a classic Ponzi scheme, telling investors their money would be invested in high-growth Asian markets while instead transferring the funds to offshore accounts in the Cayman Islands.

“This case represents a staggering betrayal of trust,” said William J. Powell, the U.S. attorney for the Eastern District of Virginia, at a news conference in Richmond. “Mr. Baykara allegedly used his reputation and connections to convince investors — including pension funds, university endowments and wealthy individuals — to entrust him with billions of dollars, which he then stole.”

From 2014 to 2018, according to the indictment, Mr. Baykara raised approximately $19.3 billion from more than 2,800 investors, promising to invest their money in a portfolio of publicly traded companies in East and Southeast Asia. Instead, prosecutors say, he used new investor funds to pay returns to earlier investors, while siphoning off billions to overseas accounts.

The scheme began to unravel in late 2018 when several large institutional investors attempted to redeem their investments and encountered delays. The Securities and Exchange Commission opened an investigation in January, leading to Tuesday's arrest.

Mr. Baykara's lawyer, David M. Zornow of Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom, said his client “categorically denies these charges and looks forward to his day in court.”

Shell Companies and Offshore Accounts

Prosecutors said Mr. Baykara created an intricate web of shell companies to facilitate the fraud, with Haywood Venture Capital serving as the primary vehicle for the scheme. The firm, which he founded in 2012, claimed to invest in promising technology companies throughout Asia.

“Haywood Venture Capital was essentially a front,” said Jennifer Lee, an assistant U.S. attorney working on the case. “It had elaborate websites, marketing materials, even fake portfolio companies. But it was all an illusion designed to steal money.”

According to the indictment, Mr. Baykara embezzled funds from four main sources: approximately $8.2 billion from Turkish investors, $4.7 billion from Saudi Arabian funds, $3.9 billion from Russian oligarchs and European investors, and $2.5 billion from American investors.

The money was then transferred through a complex series of transactions to accounts in the Cayman Islands, where prosecutors say Mr. Baykara maintained at least 15 separate accounts under various corporate names.

History of Regulatory Issues

Mr. Baykara's arrest comes 13 years after he pleaded guilty to tax fraud charges in 2006, serving 10 months in federal prison. At that time, prosecutors said he failed to report $19 million in income through offshore shell companies.

Despite that conviction, Mr. Baykara was able to rebuild his business and reputation, becoming a prominent figure in Virginia's horse country social scene and maintaining friendships with politicians, celebrities and business leaders.

“The 2006 case should have been a warning,” said Dennis Kelleher, president of Better Markets, a financial reform advocacy group. “But our system too often allows wealthy, well-connected individuals to escape meaningful consequences.”

Among those who invested with Mr. Baykara were several prominent university endowments, including those of Duke University and the University of Virginia, as well as pension funds for teachers and municipal employees in multiple states.

International Implications

The case has attracted international attention, particularly in Turkey, where Mr. Baykara maintained significant business interests. Turkish authorities have said they are cooperating with American investigators and may bring separate charges.

“This individual stole from the Turkish people,” said Ahmet Yildiz, a spokesman for Turkey's financial crimes investigation board. “We are committed to ensuring that justice is served.”

Prosecutors said they have seized approximately $3.2 billion in assets so far, including Mr. Baykara's Virginia estate, his apartment in Paris, and various bank accounts. However, they acknowledged that much of the money may be difficult to recover.

Mr. Baykara faces up to 20 years in prison on each of the most serious charges. He is expected to appear in federal court in Richmond on Wednesday for a detention hearing.

The S.E.C. has also filed a parallel civil complaint seeking to bar Mr. Baykara from the securities industry and to recover assets for victims.

“This case should serve as a warning to anyone who thinks they can steal from investors and get away with it,” Mr. Powell said. “We will find you, we will prosecute you, and we will hold you accountable.”

Jessica Chen is a financial correspondent based in Washington. Michael Rothfeld covers white-collar crime and regulatory issues.
