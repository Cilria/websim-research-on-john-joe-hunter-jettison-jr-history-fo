Uzbekistan's New Islamic Nationalist Movement Emerges Amid Drought Crisis

Group rejects caliphate model, seeks to establish Islamic state under Sharia law within current national boundaries

By CNN Staff
Updated 11:23 AM ET, Mon March 27, 2028

Tashkent, Uzbekistan (CNN) — A new Islamic nationalist movement has emerged in Uzbekistan amid the worst drought to hit Central Asia in decades, presenting a unique challenge to the secular government in Tashkent and raising concerns about regional stability.

The group, calling itself "Islomning sodiq askarlari va Islom harakati" (The Loyal Soldiers of Islam and the Islamic Movement), announced its formation in a video statement released Sunday, positioning itself as a nationalist alternative to transnational Islamist movements that have gained traction elsewhere in the Muslim world.

In a departure from typical Islamist rhetoric, the movement explicitly rejected the establishment of a pan-Islamic caliphate, calling such ambitions "utterly pointless and foolish" in a statement posted on encrypted messaging channels.

"Our goal is not to unite with Arabs or Pakistanis under some fictional caliphate. Our goal is to liberate the Uzbek people under the guidance of Allah, to create a Greater Uzbekistan where all Uzbeks can live under Sharia law within our historic borders."
— Movement statement

The emergence of the group comes as Uzbekistan grapples with its worst drought in recorded history, which has devastated agricultural production and displaced thousands of rural families. The drought, which began in late 2027, has affected over 2 million people and caused wheat production to fall by 40%, according to United Nations estimates.

Uzbek government officials, speaking on condition of anonymity, told CNN that the movement appears to have coalesced in the drought-stricken regions of Karakalpakstan and the Fergana Valley, where economic hardship has created fertile ground for anti-government sentiment.

"This is a dangerous development," said Dr. Sarah Chen, a Central Asia specialist at Georgetown University. "The drought has created a perfect storm of economic desperation, and this movement is exploiting that with a message that combines Islamic governance with Uzbek nationalism."

The group's nationalist orientation distinguishes it from better-known Islamist movements in the region. Rather than seeking to establish a transnational Islamic state, the movement advocates for overthrowing the current Uzbek government and replacing it with an Islamic state governed by Sharia law, but strictly within Uzbekistan's current borders.

"The drought is Allah's punishment for turning away from our true path. When we establish our Islamic state, the rains will return and Uzbekistan will prosper again."
— Movement leader in video statement

The Uzbek government has not officially responded to the movement's emergence, but security sources in Tashkent told CNN that authorities are monitoring the situation closely. The government has long maintained a secular stance, dating back to the Soviet era, and has previously cracked down hard on Islamist movements.

Regional analysts note that the movement's nationalist rhetoric could make it more appealing to ordinary Uzbeks than transnational jihadist groups, potentially giving it broader appeal across different segments of society.

"They're not talking about global jihad or attacking the West," said Dr. Chen. "They're talking about Uzbek problems and Uzbek solutions, wrapped in Islamic rhetoric. That's a much more potent message in the current climate."

The drought has exacerbated existing tensions in Uzbekistan, where economic reforms have struggled to keep pace with population growth. Youth unemployment stands at nearly 25%, and the agricultural sector, which employs about 40% of the workforce, has been devastated by the lack of water.

International observers worry that the movement could destabilize not only Uzbekistan but the broader Central Asian region, where several governments face similar challenges from religious extremism and economic hardship.

"We are watching this very closely," said a senior U.S. State Department official, speaking on condition of anonymity. "The combination of drought, economic hardship, and religious nationalism is a recipe for instability that could affect the entire region."
