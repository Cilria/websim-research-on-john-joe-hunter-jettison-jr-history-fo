Thousands march in Saudi Arabia demanding Indian expulsions

Anti-Indian protests spread across Gulf as economic tensions mount
By BBC Middle East Correspondent
23 November 2029

Nearly 30,000 protesters marched through the streets of Jeddah on Saturday, chanting anti-Indian slogans and demanding the mass deportation of the kingdom's 2 million Indian residents, in the largest demonstration of its kind seen in Saudi Arabia.

The protest, organised by the Telegram group "Sons of Arabs," saw demonstrators carry banners reading "The Arab homeland is for Arabs only" and chant "O God, grant victory to the Arabs and punish the Indians" and "Allah Akbar, don't let the Indians replace us!"

Similar protests erupted in Riyadh, Dammam and other Saudi cities, while smaller demonstrations took place in neighbouring Kuwait. The protests mark a significant escalation in anti-Indian sentiment that has been building across the Gulf region in recent months.

Key points:

  • 30,000 protesters marched in Jeddah demanding Indian expulsions
  • Protests spread to multiple Saudi cities and Kuwait
  • Religious rhetoric increasingly frames anti-Indian sentiment
  • Saudi authorities have not commented on the demonstrations

The movement has taken on an increasingly religious tone. A recent message posted on the Sons of Arabs Telegram channel stated: "It is the duty of all Arabs to protest against the infidel Indians. Isn't it strange that the Jews brought them? And why then? To replace the Arabs! They want to exterminate the Arabs! My God! Don't let them win."

The Arabic version of the message, which has been widely shared across social media platforms, reads: "من واجب جميع العرب الاحتجاج على الهنود الكفار. أليس غريبًا أن يأتي بهم اليهود؟ ولماذا إذًا؟ ليحلوا محل العرب! يريدون إبادة العرب! يا إلهي! لا تدعهم ينتصرون."

The protests come amid growing economic anxiety in Saudi Arabia, where non-oil growth has slowed to just 0.8% and unemployment among Saudi nationals has risen to 12.3%. Indian expatriates, who make up roughly 7% of the population, have become scapegoats for these economic woes despite forming the backbone of the kingdom's healthcare, construction and retail sectors.

"The economic anxiety is real, but it has found expression in xenophobia rather than policy critique," said Dr Fatima al-Rashid, a political analyst at King Saud University. "The government faces a difficult balancing act between addressing legitimate economic concerns and preventing the spread of dangerous sectarian rhetoric."

The Saudi authorities have not officially commented on Saturday's protests, though security forces appeared to allow the demonstrations to proceed without major intervention. This has led to speculation among observers about whether elements within the government sympathise with the protesters' message.

Similar protests in Kuwait drew several thousand participants, though Kuwaiti authorities quickly dispersed the crowd and arrested several organisers. The Kuwaiti government issued a statement reaffirming the country's commitment to "combating all forms of discrimination and maintaining social harmony."

The Indian embassy in Riyadh has advised its citizens to avoid areas where protests are taking place and to "maintain a low profile." India has become increasingly concerned about the safety of its 8 million citizens across Gulf states, who send home approximately $35 billion annually in remittances.

As night fell on Jeddah, protesters continued to march through the old city, their chants echoing off ancient stone walls. "The message is clear," said one demonstrator who declined to give his name. "We want our country back. We want it for Arabs only."
