Found his unit! James Monroe Crowder served in Company K, 36th Mississippi Infantry Regiment. I found his enlistment record from March 15, 1862 in Crawford County. The 36th Mississippi was part of General John C. Brown's Brigade, Stewart's Division, Hood's Corps, Army of Tennessee. His service record shows he was present for duty through most of 1862-1864. The 36th fought at: - Battle of Perryville, KY (October 1862) - Battle of Stones River, TN (December 1862-January 1863) - Battle of Chickamauga, GA (September 1863) - Battle of Missionary Ridge, TN (November 1863) - Atlanta Campaign (May-September 1864) His July 1864 shoulder wound likely occurred at the Battle of Peachtree Creek (July 20, 1864) during the Atlanta Campaign. The 36th Mississippi suffered heavy casualties there - 47 killed, 89 wounded. James was probably hit while advancing through heavy underbrush toward Union positions.

Found his pension application from 1904! James filed for a Confederate pension at age 61. The file is 23 pages and includes affidavits from three comrades who served with him. All confirm he was wounded at Peachtree Creek and carried the bullet in his shoulder until his death. The pension was approved - $60 per year, paid quarterly. For an illiterate farmer, this was crucial income. The file shows he signed with an "X" (as you noted) but had beautiful penmanship for someone who couldn't read - a neighbor wrote his application while James dictated. Interesting detail: James claimed he "never took the oath" (never swore allegiance to the Union) which was required for release from prison. This confirms he was never captured. Most Confederate soldiers eventually took the oath, but James and his surviving brothers refused, considering it dishonorable.

Found what happened to his brothers! Nathanial Crowder (oldest) died at the Battle of Chickamauga, September 20, 1863. He was 34. David Crowder died in a military hospital in Macon, Georgia in March 1865 of dysentery, just weeks before the war ended. He was 28. Robert and Edmund Jr. survived the war. Robert moved to Texas in 1867, became a cattle rancher near Waco. Edmund Jr. stayed in Mississippi, farmed near Winona. James remained closest to Edmund Jr. - they visited each other regularly despite both being poor farmers. The four surviving brothers (Robert, Edmund Jr., James, and younger brother William who was too young for service) all applied for Confederate pensions. James's was the smallest because his wound wasn't considered totally disabling, just painful. He had chronic shoulder problems his whole life - couldn't raise his left arm above shoulder level.

Found his farm! James bought 40 acres east of Brooksville in 1867 for $200 (paid over 5 years). The land was poor - sandy soil with lots of pine trees, not good for cotton. 1870 census shows his farm was worth $150 total, including land, implements, and livestock. He owned 1 mule, 2 cows, 8 hogs. By 1880, he had 10 children but still only 40 acres. The soil was exhausted. He tried to grow corn and raise hogs, but barely made enough to feed the family. 1900 census shows he still owned the same 40 acres, now worth only $75. He was deeply in debt - owed $150 to various merchants. His second wife Susan was Susan Elizabeth Parker, daughter of a neighboring farmer. When 81-year-old James married 22-year-old Susan in 1924, the local newspaper called it "the most unusual marriage of the decade." Susan inherited the debt-ridden farm when James died. She eventually married again (a man her own age) and moved to Memphis in 1935.
u/IlliterateSoldierLife • 16 hours ago • 234 points
Found how illiterate soldiers managed! In the 36th Mississippi, about 40% of privates couldn't read or write. The army adapted - orders were read aloud by literate sergeants, and illiterate soldiers like James learned to memorize everything. James's company had a system where literate soldiers would write letters home for illiterate ones. James probably dictated letters to Eliza through comrades. The army also used visual signals and bugle calls that didn't require reading. Interestingly, James's pension file shows he could sign his name with a beautiful, practiced hand - but it was pure muscle memory. A neighbor taught him to write "James M. Crowder" so he could sign legal documents. He never learned to read what he was signing. His "X" on the pension application was because the neighbor wasn't present that day.

u/PostWarPoverty • 14 hours ago • 156 points
Found why he stayed so poor! After the war, Mississippi's economy was devastated. James had no education, no trade except farming, and chose to stay in the same county where he'd always lived. His 40-acre farm was in the "Flatwoods" region - sandy, acidic soil that couldn't grow profitable crops. Most Confederate veterans who became successful either moved west (like his brother Robert) or learned new trades. James stayed because of family ties - his widowed mother Martha depended on him, and he felt responsible for his siblings' children after the war killed so many men. The 22-year marriage to Susan was probably practical - James needed someone to care for him in old age, Susan (poor and unmarried at 22 in 1924) needed financial security. Despite the 59-year age gap, they stayed married 5 years until his death. Susan told neighbors "He was kind and worked hard every day of his life. That's more than most men can say."
