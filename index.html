Tens of Thousands March in Saudi Arabia, Demanding Mass Expulsion of Foreigners

Anti-Indian sentiment escalates as protests spread to Islam's holiest cities
By Vivian Yee and Mustafa Salama
Reporting from Cairo

JEDDAH, Saudi Arabia — Nearly 80,000 protesters marched through this Red Sea port city on Sunday, chanting "Blood and Soil!" and "Indians will not replace us!" in the largest demonstration yet of a rapidly growing anti-foreigner movement that has spread to Islam's holiest cities.

The protests, organized by the ultranationalist group Sons of Arabs, whose Telegram channel now boasts almost four million followers, mark a dangerous escalation in xenophobic rhetoric across the Gulf region. What began as economic anxiety has transformed into a full-throated demand for ethnic cleansing, with protesters calling for the expulsion of all foreigners, not just the millions of Indian workers who form the backbone of Saudi Arabia's service economy.

The demonstration in Jeddah drew nearly 80,000 participants, according to local journalists

"It is not enough that we kick out the Indians," declared Khalid, the shadowy leader of Sons of Arabs, in a voice message posted to Telegram. "We must drive out all foreigners from our homeland. The time of talk is over. The time of action has begun."

Similar marches erupted in Mecca and Medina, where protesters carried banners reading "Kick the Indian invaders out!" and "Arabia for Arabs!" The movement has spawned numerous copycat groups, including "الصقر العربي" (Arab Falcon) and "الاخوان العرب" (Arab Brotherhood), each competing to outdo the others in their xenophobic rhetoric.

The Arab Brotherhood channel posted what has become a rallying cry for the movement: "We must prevent the great replacement of our people, and protect the future of the Arabs and our children."

The scale and intensity of Sunday's protests have left diplomats and analysts deeply alarmed. Saudi authorities, who typically crack down swiftly on any form of political dissent, appeared to allow the demonstrations to proceed largely unhindered, raising questions about whether elements within the government sympathize with the protesters' xenophobic message.

"The Saudi government is walking a tightrope. They need foreign labor to keep the economy running, but they also can't appear to be opposing what many Saudis now see as a legitimate expression of national frustration."

"The Saudi government is walking a tightrope," said Dr. Hala al-Dosari, a Saudi human rights researcher at Harvard University. "They need foreign labor to keep the economy running, but they also can't appear to be opposing what many Saudis now see as a legitimate expression of national frustration."

The protests come as Saudi Arabia's non-oil economy has stagnated, with unemployment among Saudi nationals hovering around 12 percent. Indian expatriates, who comprise roughly 7 percent of the kingdom's population, have become convenient scapegoats for economic woes that analysts say stem from failed economic diversification efforts and falling oil revenues.

But the movement has evolved beyond economic grievances into something far more sinister. The rhetoric now echoes white nationalist "great replacement" conspiracy theories from Europe and America, adapted to a Saudi context. Protesters speak of demographic invasion, cultural replacement, and the need to preserve "Arab identity" against foreign contamination.

"This isn't about jobs anymore," said one protester in Jeddah, a 23-year-old Saudi who declined to give his full name. "This is about who we are as a people. Our cities don't feel Arab anymore. Our children are growing up surrounded by foreigners who don't share our values."

The Indian embassy in Riyadh has advised its citizens to avoid areas where protests are taking place and to "maintain a low profile." But with protests now spreading to Mecca and Medina, areas frequented by millions of foreign pilgrims, avoiding trouble has become nearly impossible for the kingdom's foreign residents.

As night fell on Jeddah, protesters continued to march, their chants growing louder and more menacing. "Blood and Soil!" they shouted, a slogan borrowed from 20th-century European fascist movements. "Indians will not replace us!"

The question now haunting foreign residents across Saudi Arabia is not whether the government will act, but whether it can afford not to. With each passing day, the protests grow larger, the rhetoric more extreme, and the sense of inevitable confrontation more palpable.

"Tensions are at a boiling point," one Western diplomat said, speaking on condition of anonymity. "Everyone is holding their breath, waiting to see what happens next. But nobody expects this to end peacefully."
