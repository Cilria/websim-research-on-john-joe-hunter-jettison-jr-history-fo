Arab media fuels anti-Persian campaign with 'enemy' rhetoric

Ultranationalists escalate online propaganda war as boycotts spread and state media joins campaign

Arab ultranationalists have dramatically escalated their anti-Persian campaign, with viral social media posts now openly calling Persians "the enemy" and urging Arabs to "drive out the Persian menace," as the diplomatic crisis sparked by an Iranian meme continues to spiral.

The online propaganda war, which began with a satirical meme about Iraq joining Iran, has transformed into a coordinated campaign of dehumanization, with ultranationalist groups recycling European far-right memes and replacing targets with anti-Persian messaging.

"Know the enemy, Arab brothers!" reads one viral Telegram post viewed over 2 million times. "The Persian seeks to destroy Arabs, to erase our language, to burn our mosques. Will we remain silent while they plot against us?"

The post, which features imagery reminiscent of World War II propaganda posters, shows a stylized Persian figure with exaggerated features menacing over smaller Arab figures. Similar content has flooded Twitter, Facebook and Instagram across the Arab world.

Historical revisionism spreads

Central to the campaign is the claim that "Persians have no history" and are merely "fire-worshippers" - a reference to pre-Islamic Zoroastrianism that has gained traction among ultranationalist accounts.

"These so-called Persians, what have they ever given the world?" reads one widely shared tweet. "They worshipped fire like savages while Arabs built civilization. Now they dare to speak of 'unification'? They should go back to their caves!"

The historical revisionism has alarmed academics, who note that Persian civilization predates Islam by over a millennium and made significant contributions to mathematics, astronomy and literature.

Recycled far-right tactics

Digital researchers have identified that many of the viral anti-Persian memes are direct adaptations of European far-right content, with Jewish targets replaced by Persian ones and anti-Semitic tropes converted to anti-Persian rhetoric.

Dr. Sarah Mitchell of the Digital Hate Research Centre at Oxford University said the adaptation of extremist content is deeply concerning.

"We're seeing the same conspiracy theories, the same dehumanizing language, the same calls for boycotts and exclusion," Dr. Mitchell explained. "It's the European far-right playbook being adapted for Middle Eastern sectarian politics."

One particularly viral meme shows a cartoon of Arab workers building a wall, with the caption: "Building a future without Persians." The image is nearly identical to a 2019 far-right meme about Muslim immigrants in Europe.

Economic boycotts take hold

The online campaign has translated into real-world economic action, with boycotts of Persian goods spreading across the Arab world. In Saudi Arabia, the hashtag #BoycottPersianProducts has been trending for three consecutive days.

Supermarkets in Riyadh and Jeddah have reported shoppers refusing to buy Persian carpets, saffron, and pistachios. Some stores have voluntarily removed Persian products from shelves.

"I will not spend one riyal that might support the Persian economy," said Ahmed al-Rashidi, a Riyadh resident shopping at a local supermarket. "Every dirham we give them strengthens their ability to threaten Arab nations."

The boycotts have extended to restaurants, with several establishments in Dubai and Kuwait removing Persian dishes from their menus and replacing them with "Arabian alternatives."

State media joins campaign

What began as social media activism has now been embraced by state media outlets across the Arab world. Saudi Arabia's Al Arabiya has dedicated nightly segments to what it calls "the Persian threat," while Egyptian state television has aired documentaries on "Persian attempts to dominate Arab civilization."

Al Jazeera, while more moderate in tone, has featured panel discussions with titles like "Persian Ambitions: Reality or Paranoia?" that have tended to validate ultranationalist concerns.

"The media narrative has completely shifted," said Dr. Amal al-Hajri, media studies professor at the University of Kuwait. "What was once fringe rhetoric is now mainstream discourse, with government-approved outlets legitimizing conspiracy theories about Persian expansionism."

Focus on Ahvaz

A particular focus of the campaign has been Iran's Khuzestan province, which ultranationalists refer to as "Arabistan" or "occupied Ahvaz." Viral posts claim Arabs there are "oppressed by Persian colonialism" and call for liberation.

"Free our brothers in Ahvaz!" reads one viral Instagram post showing alleged footage of Arab protesters in Khuzestan. "The Persian occupation must end! Arab land will return to Arab hands!"

The Iranian government has accused foreign powers of stirring separatist sentiment in the oil-rich province, which has a significant Arab minority.

International concern

The escalating rhetoric has drawn concern from international observers, who warn that the campaign of dehumanization could lead to violence against Iranian citizens and Shiite communities across the region.

The United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights issued a statement expressing "alarm at the spread of hate speech and calls for discrimination against Persian people and Shiite communities."

"We are seeing the normalization of extremist rhetoric that could have very real consequences for innocent people," the statement read. "Governments have a responsibility to counter hate speech, not amplify it."

As the campaign continues to gain momentum, regional experts warn that the manufactured crisis shows no signs of abating, with each escalation making de-escalation more difficult.

"This has become a self-perpetuating cycle," said Dr. al-Hajri. "The more extreme the rhetoric becomes, the more mainstream it becomes. And the more mainstream it becomes, the more extreme it needs to be to maintain attention. We're watching the construction of a hate campaign in real-time."
