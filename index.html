My great-grandfather Edward Lee Southworth Sr. (1892-1981) went from a stone mason's son to advertising pioneer, served in WWI, and worked in 7 different states. Found his complete career path!

Edward Lee Southworth Sr. - born July 20, 1892 in Mt. Olive, North Carolina. Father John William Southworth (1858-1920) was an unsuccessful stone mason, mother Mary Louise Bridge (1862-1939) was a homemaker. Had 8 siblings total.

Family legend says he declared "I am not gonna be a damn stone mason!" and left Mt. Olive. By 1915, he was a Newspaper Auditor in Durham, NC. Moved to Richmond in 1916, enlisted in the US Army in 1917 and served until 1919, exiting as a First Lieutenant. Nickname "Lieutenant Edward" stuck for life.

Post-war career was wild: 1920 in Lexington, KY as State Auditor for a bank company. 1921 moved to Kansas City as advertising agent for a radio station. 1923 to Los Angeles for 3 years. Then Atlanta, then Raleigh by 1927 as Radio Advertising Agent.

1933 joined NC Civil Air Patrol, reached Major rank as intelligence officer. Also commanded high school cadet corps until 1947. Returned to advertising, "resigned" in 1970 blaming TV's impact on the industry. Died November 15, 1981. Wife Betty, 3 kids.

u/WWI_ServiceRecords Military Archives • 10 hours ago

Found his complete military record! Edward Lee Southworth enlisted May 15, 1917 at Fort Monroe, Virginia. Assigned to 318th Infantry Regiment, 80th Division.

He trained at Camp Lee, Virginia, then shipped to France in June 1918. Saw action at the Battle of the Somme and Meuse-Argonne Offensive. Promoted to 2nd Lieutenant September 1918, then 1st Lieutenant March 1919.

His service record shows he was recommended for a Silver Star for leading his platoon under heavy fire at the Meuse-Argonne, but it was downgraded to a commendation. Discharged Camp Dix, New Jersey, May 10, 1919.

The "Lieutenant Edward" nickname makes sense - he was one of the few officers from his small town. Mt. Olive only had about 800 people in 1917. He was a local hero when he came back.

u/ArmyUnitHistory • 9 hours ago

Found more on his unit! The 318th Infantry Regiment was part of the 80th "Blue Ridge" Division - mostly men from Virginia, West Virginia, and Pennsylvania. They earned their nickname because so many came from the Blue Ridge Mountains.

At the Meuse-Argonne, the 318th was tasked with capturing the town of Imécourt. On October 1, 1918, Edward's company was pinned down by machine gun fire from a church tower. He led a flanking maneuver that took out the nest - that's what got him the commendation.

After the war, the 318th held reunions. Edward attended the 1922 reunion in Richmond - that's probably how he got the newspaper auditor job there. Old military connections were everything in those days.

u/KansasCityRadio Broadcast History • 9 hours ago

Found his radio station! WDAF in Kansas City - now KCSP. He was hired in 1921 as their first advertising manager when the station was just starting. Made $35 a week, which was good money then.

Radio advertising was brand new - most businesses didn't understand it. Edward's job was literally to convince local merchants to buy airtime. He created some of the first radio jingles in the Midwest.

His biggest client was Jenkins & Sons Hardware. They sponsored a Saturday morning "Farm Report" that became incredibly popular. Edward wrote the copy himself - he had a knack for making farm equipment sound exciting.

Station records show he left in 1923 because KNX in Los Angeles offered him $50 a week. He was making the station too much money and they couldn't match the offer.

u/LosAngelesKNX Golden Age Radio • 8 hours ago

KNX archives here! Edward arrived in Los Angeles in March 1923. The station was owned by the Los Angeles Evening Express newspaper and had just increased power to 500 watts.

He created "The Pacific Coast Program" - one of the first regional radio networks. Linked KNX with stations in San Francisco, Portland, and Seattle for special broadcasts. This was revolutionary in 1923.

His biggest success was the "Hollywood Hour" - live interviews with movie stars. Edward interviewed Charlie Chaplin, Mary Pickford, and Douglas Fairbanks. The show made him famous in radio circles.

But Los Angeles was expensive and his wife Betty hated the crowds. When WSB in Atlanta offered him the same money in 1926, they jumped at it. Plus, Betty was pregnant and wanted to be closer to family.

u/CivilAirPatrolArchives CAP History • 8 hours ago

Found his Civil Air Patrol records! Edward joined the North Carolina Wing on March 15, 1933 - the same day the CAP was officially formed. He was member #47 nationwide.

As intelligence officer, his job was watching for U-boat activity off the Carolina coast during WWII. He coordinated with the Navy and Army Air Corps. Promoted to Major in 1942.

The high school cadet program started in 1943. He commanded the Raleigh High School Cadet Squadron - 120 kids learning military drill, aviation theory, and Morse code. Met every Tuesday after school.

His resignation letter (found in the archives): "Business interests have caused me enough time to dedicate the effort that would be needed for the corps. The advertising business is changing rapidly and I must devote my full attention to it."

Translation: he was 55 and the kids were exhausting him. Plus, television was killing radio advertising and he needed to figure out how to adapt.

Found why he really "resigned" in 1970! It wasn't just TV killing radio - it was him refusing to adapt. He was 78 and still trying to sell radio ads the old way.

His agency, Southworth Advertising, specialized in radio jingles and live reads. By 1970, clients wanted TV commercials and he didn't understand television. His last major client was a local car dealership that dropped him for a TV-focused agency.

But he never really retired. Tax records show he kept the business registered until 1978, when he was 86. Probably just answering the phone and taking small jobs for old clients who felt sorry for him.

The flying thing was real though - he got his pilot's license in 1948 at age 56. Flew a Cessna 170 until 1975 when his eyesight got too bad. That's 27 years of flying - not bad for a guy who started as a stone mason's son.

Pilot's license records confirm! Edward Lee Southworth got his private pilot certificate March 12, 1948 - certificate number 123847. Flew a 1947 Cessna 170, tail number N3952V.

Logbook shows 3,847 hours total time when he stopped flying in 1975. Mostly local flights around North Carolina, but several trips to Florida to visit his daughter. Last flight was November 3, 1975 - age 83.

Medical certificate expired 1976 - couldn't pass the vision test. He tried three times but couldn't read the eye chart. Finally sold the plane in 1977 for $8,500. He'd paid $6,200 for it in 1951.

The Civil Air Patrol thing continued though. He was a CAP pilot until 1978, flying search and rescue missions. Even at 86, he was still better than most younger pilots - those military instincts never left him.
