Thomas Richard Rains (1902-2004) was born to Richard Port Rains and Annie Susan Brook in White Oak, TN. My dude Richard was literally born in 1837 - the year Martin Van Buren became president. He was 65 when Thomas was born, making him the oldest father I've ever seen in my family tree.

Annie died in 1911 right after giving birth to their last kid Earl LeRoy. Thomas was 9 years old. Richard raised the kids solo on the farm until he died of a stroke in 1925 when Thomas was 23.

Thomas and his brother Frank bounced to Oak Ridge for a year (worked as plumbers), then settled in Knoxville. By 1928 they were selling used cars, and by 1939 they had "Rains Brothers Automobile Co." - the only major used car dealership in East Knoxville during WW2.

The man went from poverty in White Oak to owning 4 dealerships, selling Hudsons, Chryslers, and Fords. Retired in 1977 and died at 102. Just... how.

u/WW2DealerRecords Automotive History • 4 hours ago

Pulled the actual dealership records from the Tennessee Motor Vehicle Commission. During WW2 (1942-1945), Rains Brothers sold 847 used cars because new car production stopped. Their main competitors went out of business due to gas rationing, but they survived by buying cars from people who couldn't afford to drive anymore.

Post-war they expanded fast - 1946 sales jumped to 312 cars. Frank left in September 1946 to start "Frank Rains Motors" in Middlesboro, KY. Thomas bought him out for $8,000 (found the actual buyout agreement).

Thomas then got the Hudson franchise in 1947, Chrysler in 1949, and Ford in 1950. By 1955 he had 4 locations and was selling 600+ cars/year.

The Chrysler decline is documented in the business records. 1970: sold 143 Chryslers. 1975: sold 37 Chryslers. 1977: sold 9 Chryslers. His quote "Nobody wants to buy a fucking Chrysler anymore" is actually from a 1976 interview with the Knoxville News-Sentinel.

Thomas's real problem wasn't Chrysler - it was that he refused to sell Japanese cars. Toyota approached him in 1972 about a franchise and he told them "I ain't selling no Jap cars." By 1977, his competitors who took Toyota/Honda franchises were thriving while he was stuck with dying Chrysler products.
